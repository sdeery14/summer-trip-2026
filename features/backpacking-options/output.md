## Context and Assumptions

**Who you are for this document:**
- Experienced backpackers with full gear (bear canister, cooking setup, tent, etc.)
- Fit hikers comfortable with 10+ mile days and significant elevation gain
- First time doing an overnight in grizzly country (you have bear spray experience and a canister, but no overnight grizzly-country experience)
- Looking for a 1-2 night backcountry trip as a stretch goal within a ~7-day Greater Yellowstone Loop
- Targeting late August 2026

**What this document does:**
- Presents specific backcountry routes across all three areas of the loop
- Includes permit logistics, campsite specifics, water, bear safety, and the "why it's worth it" for each

## Yellowstone Backcountry Options

Yellowstone has over 300 designated backcountry campsites and 1,000+ miles of trails. The backcountry permit system governs all overnight use. Late August is an excellent time: river crossings are lower, trails are dry, bears are actively foraging (which means they are around but also focused on food — predictable behavior), and crowds thin dramatically once school starts.

### Permit System — Yellowstone

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **System** | Advance reservation + walk-up |
| **Advance reservations** | Available starting **[VERIFY 2026]** approximately early-to-mid January for the coming summer. Historically, Yellowstone used a system where you could submit advance trip requests starting January 1 for a $25 reservation fee. In recent years, this has shifted to an online system through recreation.gov. |
| **Walk-up permits** | Available in person at ranger stations/backcountry offices starting 48 hours before your trip start date. Free (no reservation fee, just the standard backcountry use fee if applicable). |
| **Cost** | $25 advance reservation fee + $10/night backcountry camping fee per person (age 16+) **[VERIFY 2026 — fees have been increasing]** |
| **Where to pick up** | Backcountry offices at: Canyon, Old Faithful, Mammoth, Tower, South Entrance, West Yellowstone, Bridge Bay, Grant Village, Bechler |
| **Bear canister** | Required in some areas. Yellowstone accepts most hard-sided canisters (BV500, Bear Boxer, Garcia, etc.). Check that yours is on the approved list. Hanging food from poles is provided at some designated campsites. |
| **Key link** | https://www.nps.gov/yell/planyourvisit/backcountryhiking.htm |
| **Campsite reservation map** | https://www.nps.gov/yell/planyourvisit/backcountrycampsites.htm |

**Strategy note:** For a late August trip, walk-up permits are a realistic option. Peak backcountry season is July — by late August, many popular campsites are available on walk-up, especially midweek. However, if you want a specific campsite on the Bechler River or Heart Lake, advance reservation is strongly recommended.

### Route 1: Bechler River Trail (Southwest Yellowstone)

**The pitch:** The Bechler region is Yellowstone's hidden masterpiece — a lush, waterfall-studded river corridor in the park's remote southwest corner that most visitors never see. This is the "cascade corner" of Yellowstone, with more waterfalls per mile than anywhere else in the park. An overnight here gets you to places that are genuinely unreachable on a day hike.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 15-30 miles depending on route (see options below) |
| **Configuration** | Out-and-back or point-to-point with car shuttle |
| **Nights** | 1-2 |
| **Elevation gain** | 500-1,500 ft (relatively gentle — this is river corridor hiking) |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate. The terrain is not steep, but river crossings can be knee-to-thigh deep in early summer. By late August, crossings are typically calf-to-knee deep. The distance is the challenge, not the elevation. |

**Route options within the Bechler:**

**Option A: Bechler Meadows Out-and-Back (1 night, ~16 miles RT)**
- Start/end: Bechler Ranger Station trailhead
- Hike in ~8 miles to Bechler Meadows area
- Target campsites: **9A1** (Bechler River, near the meadows) or **9B1** (upper Bechler Meadows)
- Payoff: Vast open meadows, the Bechler River, and waterfalls including Colonnade Falls (~4 miles beyond the meadows on a day-hike extension)
- Good for 1 night: hike in day 1, explore/hike out day 2

**Option B: Bechler to Colonnade and Iris Falls (2 nights, ~26-30 miles RT)**
- Start/end: Bechler Ranger Station trailhead
- Night 1: Camp at **9D2** or **9D3** (near Three River Junction, ~10-11 miles in)
- Day 2: Day hike to Colonnade Falls and Iris Falls (both spectacular 100+ foot waterfalls in a columnar basalt canyon), then continue to **9U1** or **9U4** near Dunanda Falls
- Night 2: Camp near Dunanda Falls area, or reverse direction back to meadows camp
- Hike out day 3 morning
- This is the full Bechler experience

**Option C: Bechler Point-to-Point to Lone Star Geyser (2 nights, ~30 miles)**
- Start: Bechler Ranger Station → End: Lone Star Geyser trailhead (near Old Faithful)
- Requires car shuttle or hitchhiking logistics
- The classic Bechler traverse, hitting all the waterfalls and emerging near Old Faithful
- Night 1: **9D2** or **9D3** near Three River Junction
- Night 2: **OA2** or **OA3** along the upper Bechler
- This is the bucket-list version but adds significant logistics

**Campsites:**

| Site ID | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **9A1** | Bechler River at meadows edge | River access, open sky, good tent pads. Food pole available. |
| **9B1** | Upper Bechler Meadows | Meadow views, slightly more secluded |
| **9D2** | Three River Junction area | Near the confluence of three streams, close to waterfall access |
| **9D3** | Three River Junction area | Similar to 9D2, backup option |
| **9U1** | Near Dunanda Falls | Close to one of Yellowstone's most impressive backcountry waterfalls |

**Water:** Abundant. The Bechler River and its tributaries provide water throughout. Treat all water. Late August flows are reliable — this is a well-watered drainage.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **MODERATE to HIGH**. The Bechler corridor is prime grizzly habitat — lush meadows, river corridors, and berry patches. Bears are actively foraging in late August (pre-hibernation hyperphagia).
- Food storage: Food poles are provided at most designated campsites. Bring your bear canister as backup (and it may be required — **[VERIFY 2026]** check current regulations for the Bechler zone).
- Special concern: Bechler Meadows is grizzly country. Cook 200+ feet from your tent. Store everything scented in the canister or on the food pole. Make noise on the trail, especially approaching river crossings and in the meadows where sightlines are limited.
- Closures: The Bechler area occasionally has bear management closures on specific campsites. Check at the backcountry office when picking up your permit.

**Trailhead access:**
- Bechler Ranger Station is in the far southwest corner of Yellowstone, accessed via Cave Falls Road from Ashton, Idaho (not from inside the park)
- From Jackson: ~2.5 hours via Ashton, ID
- From Old Faithful: You cannot drive directly. It is roughly 2 hours via Ashton.
- The gravel road to the trailhead is passable by passenger car but rough in spots. An SUV/AWD is more comfortable.
- Parking: Small lot at Bechler Ranger Station — typically not full in late August.

**What makes it worth it:** Colonnade Falls dropping into a basalt canyon with nobody else around. Iris Falls, a massive 45-foot curtain waterfall. Dunanda Falls, a 150-foot plunge in the middle of nowhere. Bechler Meadows at dawn with mist rising off the river. This is the Yellowstone that 99% of visitors will never see.

**Honest trade-off:** Getting to the Bechler trailhead is a significant detour from the main Yellowstone Loop. It is in the far southwest corner, accessed from Idaho, not from inside the park's main road system. If you are doing a BZN-to-JAC clockwise loop, the Bechler detour adds 3-5 hours of driving depending on where you are coming from. This is the biggest logistical hurdle.

### Route 2: Heart Lake

**The pitch:** A large backcountry lake at the base of Mount Sheridan (10,308 ft), with hot springs along the shore and one of Yellowstone's best backcountry peak ascents. This combines lakeside camping with a serious summit hike — and the hot springs right at camp are a bonus you will not forget.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | ~16 miles RT (8 miles each way to the lake, plus optional 6-mile RT summit of Mt. Sheridan) |
| **Configuration** | Out-and-back |
| **Nights** | 1 (with optional 2nd night for a more relaxed pace) |
| **Elevation gain** | ~600 ft to the lake; add ~2,700 ft for Mt. Sheridan summit |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate to the lake (flat-to-rolling trail through burned forest). Strenuous if summiting Mt. Sheridan (fire lookout at the top with 360-degree views including Yellowstone Lake and the Tetons). |

**Campsites:**

| Site ID | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **8H1** | Heart Lake, west shore near hot springs | The premier site. Close to the thermal features along the shore. |
| **8H2** | Heart Lake, northwest shore | Good lake views, slightly farther from hot springs |
| **8H3** | Heart Lake, north shore | Near the outlet creek |
| **8H4** | Heart Lake, south shore | More secluded, closer to Mt. Sheridan trail |
| **8H5-8H9** | Various locations around the lake | Multiple options — gives flexibility for walk-up permits |

**Recommended itinerary (1 night):**
- Day 1: Hike 8 miles to Heart Lake. Set up camp at 8H1 or 8H4. Afternoon: explore the hot springs along the west shore (look, do not soak — these are dangerously hot thermal features, not bathing pools). If energy allows, start up Mt. Sheridan.
- Day 2: Summit Mt. Sheridan at dawn for sunrise views (3 miles, 2,700 ft gain from camp — steep but on a maintained trail to the fire lookout). Descend, pack up, hike out 8 miles.

**Recommended itinerary (2 nights):**
- Day 1: Hike in, set up camp, explore lake and hot springs.
- Day 2: Summit Mt. Sheridan with a light pack. Afternoon at the lake — fish (Yellowstone cutthroat trout, catch-and-release, requires a park fishing permit), relax.
- Day 3: Hike out.

**Water:** Heart Lake itself, plus Witch Creek and the inlet stream. Abundant and reliable in late August. Treat all water. Be aware that water near thermal features may be too hot — draw from the lake or from streams away from hot springs.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **HIGH**. The Heart Lake area is one of Yellowstone's designated bear management areas. Grizzly bears are common here — the cutthroat trout spawning streams and berry patches draw them in.
- Food storage: Food poles are at designated campsites. Bear canister strongly recommended as backup.
- Special concern: This is serious grizzly habitat. The trail to Heart Lake passes through burned forest with limited sightlines. Make noise consistently. The hot springs area attracts bears to the warm ground. Camp defensively — cook far from your tent, hang or canister everything scented.
- Closures: Heart Lake campsites are sometimes temporarily closed for bear activity. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check current closures when picking up your permit. The area around 8H1-8H3 has been closed for bears in some recent seasons.

**Trailhead access:**
- Heart Lake trailhead is on the South Entrance Road (between Grant Village and the south entrance), about 7 miles south of Grant Village
- Easy to access from the main park road — no detour required
- Parking: Small lot, fills by mid-morning in peak season but should be manageable in late August
- This is one of the most accessible backcountry trailheads in Yellowstone — it fits naturally into the loop itinerary

**What makes it worth it:** Camping next to steaming hot springs on the shore of a backcountry lake, with a peak to summit at dawn. The sunrise from Mt. Sheridan's fire lookout is one of the best views in the park — you can see Yellowstone Lake, the Tetons, the Absarokas, and the park's thermal basins all from one point. And you will very likely see grizzly bears. This is peak Yellowstone backcountry.

**Honest trade-off:** The trail to Heart Lake is not scenic for the first 5 miles — it passes through burned lodgepole pine forest from the 1988 fires (now regenerating but still somewhat monotonous). The payoff comes at the lake. Also, the high bear activity means this is not a place to be complacent — you need to be dialed on bear protocol every moment.

### Route 3: Black Canyon of the Yellowstone

**The pitch:** The Yellowstone River carves a deep canyon downstream from Tower Fall, dropping through a series of dramatic narrows with Class IV-V rapids visible from the trail. This is a rugged, less-traveled route with multiple backcountry camps along the canyon rim and river.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 18.5 miles one way (Hellroaring trailhead to Gardiner); shorter out-and-back options available |
| **Configuration** | Point-to-point (car shuttle from Gardiner to Hellroaring trailhead) or out-and-back |
| **Nights** | 1-2 |
| **Elevation gain** | ~1,200 ft loss to the river, regain on the way out. Rolling terrain along the canyon. Net downhill if doing point-to-point. |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate to strenuous. The trail drops steeply to the river and traverses rocky, exposed terrain. The full point-to-point is a long day or comfortable 2-night trip. |

**Route options:**

**Option A: Hellroaring Creek Out-and-Back (1 night, ~10-12 miles RT)**
- Start: Hellroaring trailhead (near Tower Junction)
- Hike down 1,400 ft to the Yellowstone River confluence with Hellroaring Creek
- Target campsites: **2H1**, **2H2**, or **2H3** (Hellroaring Creek area)
- Payoff: Dramatic river canyon, suspension bridge crossing, and a feeling of deep wilderness despite relatively short mileage. Good fishing for cutthroat trout.
- The steep descent/ascent makes this feel harder than the mileage suggests

**Option B: Hellroaring to Blacktail Creek (2 nights, ~18 miles point-to-point)**
- Start: Hellroaring trailhead
- Night 1: Camp at Hellroaring Creek area (2H1-2H3)
- Day 2: Hike downstream along the Yellowstone River to Blacktail Creek area
- Night 2: Camp at **1Y6** or **1Y7** (Yellowstone River camps)
- Day 3: Hike out to the Blacktail Creek trailhead on the Mammoth-Tower road, or continue to Gardiner
- Requires car shuttle

**Campsites:**

| Site ID | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **2H1** | Hellroaring Creek near Yellowstone River confluence | River access, dramatic setting at the canyon bottom |
| **2H2** | Hellroaring Creek | Slightly upstream, more secluded |
| **2H3** | Hellroaring Creek | Third option in the drainage |
| **1Y6** | Yellowstone River downstream | Canyon views, river access |
| **1Y7** | Yellowstone River downstream | Similar to 1Y6, good backup |

**Water:** The Yellowstone River and Hellroaring Creek provide abundant water year-round. Treat all water.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **MODERATE**. The Black Canyon area has bear traffic, especially in the Hellroaring Creek drainage where bears fish and forage. However, the rocky, open terrain gives better sightlines than forested routes.
- Food storage: Food poles at designated sites. Bear canister recommended.
- This area also has mountain lions and rattlesnakes (yes, in Yellowstone — the Black Canyon is one of the few areas warm enough).

**Trailhead access:**
- Hellroaring trailhead: Off the Tower-Mammoth road, ~3.5 miles west of Tower Junction. Easy to reach from the main loop road.
- Parking: Moderate-sized lot. Manageable in late August.
- If doing point-to-point to Gardiner, you need a car shuttle (leave a car in Gardiner, drive to the trailhead, or hitch a ride — Gardiner to Hellroaring trailhead is about 30 minutes).

**What makes it worth it:** Standing at the bottom of a 1,000-foot canyon watching the Yellowstone River rage through narrows that most visitors only glimpse from an overlook. The solitude is real — this trail sees a fraction of the traffic that popular trails get. Evening light in the canyon is extraordinary.

**Honest trade-off:** The canyon is hot and exposed in summer (south-facing slopes). The descent/ascent is steep and rocky — hard on knees. The point-to-point requires logistics (car shuttle). The scenery, while dramatic, is more sagebrush-and-canyon than the alpine-lake-and-waterfall experience of Bechler or Heart Lake.

### Route 4: Lamar River / Cache Creek

**The pitch:** The Lamar Valley is Yellowstone's Serengeti — the widest, most wildlife-rich valley in the park. Backpacking up the Lamar River takes you into the heart of wolf and grizzly territory, through vast meadows with the Absaroka Range as a backdrop.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | Variable: 10-24+ miles RT depending on how far you go |
| **Configuration** | Out-and-back up the Lamar River valley, or a loop via Cache Creek |
| **Nights** | 1-2 |
| **Elevation gain** | Gentle — 200-500 ft for most of the river corridor |
| **Difficulty** | Easy to moderate (terrain is gentle), but river fords can be challenging and the remoteness factor is high. Navigation skills helpful if you go off-trail in the meadows. |

**Route option: Lamar River to Cache Creek Loop (1-2 nights, ~18-22 miles)**
- Start: Lamar River trailhead (off the Northeast Entrance Road near Soda Butte)
- Hike south along the Lamar River ~6-8 miles
- Target campsites: **3L1**, **3L3**, or **3L5** (Lamar River corridor)
- Option to loop back via Cache Creek drainage for variety
- Night 1: Camp in the Lamar valley
- Day 2: Explore up-valley or return via Cache Creek

**Campsites:**

| Site ID | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **3L1** | Lower Lamar River | Close to trailhead, good for a short first day |
| **3L3** | Mid Lamar River | Deeper into the valley, better solitude |
| **3L5** | Upper Lamar River | Remote, surrounded by meadows and peaks |
| **3C1-3C3** | Cache Creek drainage | Alternative camps for a loop option |

**Water:** The Lamar River is a major drainage — water is abundant and reliable throughout the season. Multiple tributary streams. Treat all water.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **VERY HIGH**. The Lamar Valley is premier grizzly habitat. This is where bears live, hunt, fish, and forage. You will almost certainly see grizzlies, possibly at close range. The meadow terrain means you can usually see them coming, but river bottoms and willow thickets create blind spots.
- Food storage: This is one of the areas where a bear canister may be **required** (not all sites have food poles). **[VERIFY 2026]** Confirm canister requirements for the Lamar River campsites.
- Special concern: This is the most bear-intensive route on this list. Wolves are also present. You need to be extremely disciplined about food storage, cooking distance, and noise discipline. This is not the best choice for your first-ever grizzly-country overnight — save it for your second trip when you have one overnight under your belt.

**Trailhead access:**
- Lamar River trailhead is on the Northeast Entrance Road (between Tower Junction and Cooke City), at the Soda Butte Creek bridge area
- Very easy to access from the main loop — no detour
- Parking: Small pullout/lot. Manageable in late August.

**What makes it worth it:** Waking up in the Lamar Valley at dawn with mist rising over the meadows, wolves howling in the distance, and the chance to see a grizzly sow with cubs from your tent vestibule. This is the wildest backpacking experience in the lower 48.

**Honest trade-off:** The extremely high bear activity makes this an intense choice for first-timers in grizzly country. The terrain is flat and open — beautiful but not the "alpine grandeur" experience. River fords on the Lamar can be tricky even in late August. And the wildlife that makes this special is also what demands the most from you in terms of vigilance and bear protocol.

## Grand Teton Backcountry Options

Grand Teton's backcountry is steeper, more alpine, and more dramatic than Yellowstone's. The permit system is different, and the camping is organized into "camping zones" (where you can camp anywhere within the zone) and designated campsites. Late August is ideal — snow is gone from all but the highest passes, wildflowers persist in alpine areas, and the fall clarity begins.

### Permit System — Grand Teton

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **System** | Advance reservation (one-third of permits) + walk-up (two-thirds of permits) |
| **Advance reservations** | Accepted starting **January 10** (typically) for the coming summer. **[VERIFY 2026]** Submit online via recreation.gov. $45 reservation fee (non-refundable) + $25/night backcountry camping fee. |
| **Walk-up permits** | Available in person at the Craig Thomas Discovery and Visitor Center (Moose) starting at 8 AM, one day before your trip start date. Two-thirds of all permits are held for walk-up. |
| **Cost** | Advance: $45 reservation fee + $25/night/trip. Walk-up: $25/night/trip. **[VERIFY 2026 — fees may have changed]** |
| **Bear canister** | **Required** for all overnight backcountry trips in Grand Teton NP. Hard-sided canisters only. The park rents bear canisters at the backcountry permit office if needed. Ursacks are NOT accepted. Your BV500 or equivalent hard-sided canister works. |
| **Key link** | https://www.nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/back.htm |
| **Camping zones** | Upper-elevation zones allow camping anywhere within the zone (above certain elevations, away from trails and water). Lower-elevation sites are designated. |

**Strategy note:** Grand Teton walk-up permits for popular zones (Paintbrush/Cascade area, Death Canyon Shelf, Alaska Basin) go fast even in late August. Show up at the visitor center by 7 AM (one hour before opening) for the best selection. Weekdays are much easier than weekends. Alternatively, apply for advance reservations in January — you are reading this in March, so the January window may have passed for 2026. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check if advance reservations are still available or if you are in walk-up territory.

### Route 5: Death Canyon Shelf to Alaska Basin (Teton Crest Trail Section)

**The pitch:** This is a section of the famous Teton Crest Trail, widely considered one of the top backpacking routes in North America. Death Canyon Shelf is a high-altitude bench suspended between canyon walls with views that stop you in your tracks. Alaska Basin is a vast alpine meadow at 9,700 ft surrounded by Teton peaks. Doing this section as an overnight gives you the crown jewel of Teton backpacking.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | ~18-22 miles (depending on exact route and camps) |
| **Configuration** | Point-to-point (car shuttle) or out-and-back from Death Canyon trailhead |
| **Nights** | 1-2 |
| **Elevation gain** | ~4,200 ft (Death Canyon trailhead to Alaska Basin via the Shelf) |
| **Difficulty** | Strenuous. Sustained climbing, exposed terrain on the Shelf, and a high-altitude traverse. The trail is well-maintained but long and steep. |

**Recommended itinerary (2 nights):**
- Day 1: Start at Death Canyon trailhead (near the Whitegrass Ranger Station in Grand Teton NP). Hike up Death Canyon (~4 miles, 2,500 ft gain) to the Death Canyon Shelf. Camp in the **Death Canyon Shelf camping zone** (no designated sites — choose any legal spot within the zone, 200 ft from trail and water).
- Day 2: Traverse the Shelf (2-3 miles of jaw-dropping ridgeline hiking above Death Canyon) to the **Alaska Basin** via Fox Creek Pass (10,400 ft). Camp in the **Alaska Basin camping zone** (Basin Lakes area is popular). Day-hike options: scramble toward the Teton divide or explore the basin's lake system.
- Day 3: Hike out via the Teton Crest Trail to Teton Canyon (west side, into the Jedediah Smith Wilderness in Idaho) and down to the Teton Canyon trailhead near Driggs. Or reverse back over the Shelf and down Death Canyon.

**Recommended itinerary (1 night — Death Canyon Shelf only):**
- Day 1: Hike up Death Canyon to the Shelf. Camp on the Shelf (~5-6 miles, 3,200 ft gain).
- Day 2: Morning on the Shelf (watch sunrise over Jackson Hole from 9,600 ft), then hike out back down Death Canyon.
- Total: ~12 miles RT, 3,200 ft gain. Doable in one strong push as a day hike, but camping on the Shelf turns it into something transcendent.

**Camping zones:**

| Zone | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Death Canyon Shelf** | High bench at ~9,600 ft between Death Canyon and the crest | Flat tent pads on alpine meadow. No trees — fully exposed to weather and views. Wind can be extreme. Water from small seasonal streams (check reliability). |
| **Alaska Basin** | Large alpine basin at ~9,700 ft on the Idaho/Wyoming border | Multiple lakes, meadows, wind-sheltered spots near boulders. Abundant water from lakes and streams. Technically in the Jedediah Smith Wilderness (USFS), not Grand Teton NP — different regulations apply if you camp here. |

**Important note on Alaska Basin:** Alaska Basin straddles the park boundary. The basin itself is in the Jedediah Smith Wilderness (Caribou-Targhee National Forest), not Grand Teton NP. This means:
- No NPS backcountry permit required for camping in Alaska Basin itself (USFS land is generally permit-free for overnight use)
- However, you need a Grand Teton backcountry permit if you are traveling through the park to get there (Death Canyon approach)
- If you enter from the west side (Teton Canyon trailhead near Driggs), you can access Alaska Basin without a Grand Teton permit
- Bear canisters: Still strongly recommended even on USFS land — bears do not respect jurisdictional boundaries. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check if Caribou-Targhee NF has implemented a canister requirement for Alaska Basin.

**Water:**
- Death Canyon: Reliable stream in the canyon bottom. On the Shelf, water is limited — small seasonal tarns and snowmelt streams that can dry up by late August. **Carry extra water up to the Shelf** (2-3 liters minimum).
- Alaska Basin: Abundant. Multiple lakes and streams. Reliable through the season.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **MODERATE**. Grizzly bears use these higher elevations but at lower density than valley-floor routes. Black bears are also present. The open terrain provides good sightlines.
- Food storage: Bear canister required in Grand Teton NP zones. In Alaska Basin (USFS), no food pole — canister is essential.
- The biggest wildlife risk on the Shelf is actually weather — afternoon thunderstorms on the exposed ridge are dangerous. Start early, be off the exposed Shelf by early afternoon.

**Trailhead access:**
- **Death Canyon trailhead:** At the end of a short paved/gravel road off the Moose-Wilson Road in Grand Teton NP. Small parking lot — arrive early (by 7 AM in peak season; late August is more forgiving). About 20 minutes from Moose Junction.
- **Teton Canyon trailhead (west side exit):** Near Driggs/Alta, Idaho. About 1 hour from Jackson via Teton Pass. This is the exit point if doing point-to-point.
- Car shuttle for point-to-point: You need to leave a car at Teton Canyon trailhead (Driggs side) and drive to Death Canyon trailhead, or vice versa. The drive between the two trailheads is about 1.5 hours via Teton Pass.

**What makes it worth it:** Death Canyon Shelf is, without exaggeration, one of the most beautiful places in the American West. You are standing on a flat bench at 9,600 feet with Death Canyon dropping 3,000 feet on one side and the Teton peaks towering above you on the other. At sunset, the light on the Tetons from the Shelf is otherworldly. Alaska Basin adds a huge alpine meadow ringed by 11,000-12,000 ft peaks. This is the route that people who have hiked everywhere say is their favorite.

**Honest trade-off:** The logistics are complex — car shuttle for point-to-point, two different jurisdictions (NPS and USFS), and the walk-up permit for Death Canyon Shelf zone is competitive. Water on the Shelf can be unreliable in late August. The Shelf is fully exposed to weather — if storms roll in, there is no shelter. And it is genuinely strenuous — 4,000+ feet of climbing with a full pack.

### Route 6: Paintbrush Canyon to Lake Solitude (Overnight Version)

**The pitch:** The Paintbrush-Cascade Canyon loop is one of the premier day hikes in North America. Doing it as an overnight lets you break a grueling 19.5-mile day into two manageable days, and camp at one of the most beautiful spots in the Tetons — the upper Paintbrush Canyon zone or near Holly Lake, at the foot of the Paintbrush Divide.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | ~19.5 miles (full loop); ~14 miles if out-and-back to Lake Solitude only |
| **Configuration** | Loop (Paintbrush up, Cascade Canyon down, or vice versa) or out-and-back |
| **Nights** | 1 |
| **Elevation gain** | ~4,350 ft for the full loop (Paintbrush Divide at 10,720 ft) |
| **Difficulty** | Strenuous. Paintbrush Divide involves steep switchbacks on potentially icy terrain (late-season snow patches possible). The divide is narrow and exposed. |

**Recommended itinerary (1 night):**
- Day 1: Start at String Lake trailhead. Hike up Paintbrush Canyon to the **Upper Paintbrush camping zone** (~7 miles, 3,600 ft gain). Camp near Holly Lake (a stunning alpine tarn at ~9,400 ft below the Paintbrush Divide).
- Day 2: Climb Paintbrush Divide (10,720 ft) — the highest point on the loop. Descend into Cascade Canyon. Hike past Lake Solitude (9,035 ft) and down the length of Cascade Canyon to Jenny Lake. Boat or hike back to String Lake. ~12 miles, mostly downhill.

**Alternate itinerary (1 night, Cascade Canyon approach):**
- Day 1: Take the Jenny Lake boat across (~$20 RT, saves 4 miles of flat hiking), hike up Cascade Canyon to the **South Fork Cascade camping zone** or **North Fork Cascade camping zone** near Lake Solitude.
- Day 2: Climb Paintbrush Divide from the south side, descend Paintbrush Canyon, return to String Lake.

**Camping zones:**

| Zone | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Upper Paintbrush** | Upper Paintbrush Canyon, ~9,000-9,800 ft | Holly Lake area. Alpine terrain, spectacular mountain walls, wildflower meadows (diminishing by late August). Limited flat tent sites — scout carefully. |
| **North Fork Cascade** | Upper Cascade Canyon near Lake Solitude | Subalpine forest and meadows. More sheltered than Paintbrush. Close to Lake Solitude. |
| **South Fork Cascade** | South fork of upper Cascade Canyon | Popular zone. Meadows below the Teton divide. |

**Water:** Reliable throughout. Holly Lake, streams in Paintbrush Canyon, Lake Solitude, and Cascade Creek all provide water. Late August: all sources flowing.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **MODERATE to HIGH**. Cascade Canyon is prime black bear habitat (huckleberries ripen in August). Grizzly bears have been increasingly seen in Cascade Canyon in recent years. The upper elevations (Paintbrush zone, near Holly Lake) see less bear activity.
- Food storage: Bear canister required throughout. No food poles in camping zones.
- The populated nature of this area (many day hikers in Cascade Canyon) means bears here are more habituated to human presence — which is both good (they tend to avoid people) and concerning (they are less scared of you).

**Trailhead access:**
- **String Lake trailhead:** One of the most popular trailheads in Grand Teton NP. Off Teton Park Road, about 2 miles north of Jenny Lake. Large parking lot, but it fills by 8-9 AM in summer. In late August, 7-7:30 AM arrival should secure a spot.
- **Jenny Lake boat:** Runs across Jenny Lake to the Cascade Canyon trailhead. Saves ~2 miles each way of flat lakeshore hiking. ~$20 RT per person. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check schedule and pricing. Last boat times matter for your exit day.
- Very easy to access — no detour from the main Teton loop.

**What makes it worth it:** Holly Lake at sunset with the Paintbrush Divide towering above you. Crossing the divide at dawn the next morning with views of Mount Moran, Jackson Lake, and the entire Teton Range. Lake Solitude in morning light. This is the classic Teton experience, and doing it overnight lets you savor it instead of death-marching 19.5 miles in one day.

**Honest trade-off:** The Paintbrush camping zones are popular — walk-up permits go fast. The terrain near Holly Lake has limited flat spots for tents. Paintbrush Divide can hold snow patches into late August — microspikes might be warranted (**[VERIFY 2026]** check trip reports closer to the date for late-season snow conditions). And this is the most popular backcountry area in the Tetons — you will not be alone, even overnight.

**The pitch:** A less-trafficked alternative to the Cascade/Paintbrush corridor, Granite Canyon provides a quieter Teton backcountry experience with a beautiful alpine lake camp. This is the "solitude" option for Teton backpacking.

**Recommended itinerary (1 night):**

- Day 2: Morning at Marion Lake. Options: extend to the Teton Crest Trail junction and the views toward the Middle Teton, or hike out back down Granite Canyon. Alternate exit: continue north on the Teton Crest Trail to the top of the Jackson Hole Mountain Resort tram and ride it down to Teton Village ($40-50 one way).

**Camping zone:**

| Zone | Location | Features |
|---|---|---|

**Bear safety:**

**Trailhead access:**

## Beartooth Wilderness Options

The Beartooth Plateau and surrounding Absaroka-Beartooth Wilderness offer a completely different backcountry experience: vast alpine tundra above treeline, hundreds of glacial lakes, and an overwhelming feeling of space and sky. This is high-altitude wilderness (9,500-12,000 ft) with minimal trail infrastructure.

### Permit System — Beartooth Wilderness

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **System** | **No permit required.** The Absaroka-Beartooth Wilderness is managed by the Custer Gallatin National Forest (and Shoshone National Forest on the south side). No backcountry permits are needed for overnight camping. |
| **Regulations** | Practice Leave No Trace. Camp 200 ft from lakes and trails. No campfires above treeline (generally above ~9,500 ft) or within 0.5 miles of some popular lakes — check specific area regulations. Pack out all trash. |
| **Bear canister** | Not formally required in most of the Beartooth Wilderness, but **strongly recommended**. Grizzly bears are present throughout. There are no food poles — you are responsible for your own food storage. Bring your canister. |
| **Cost** | Free. No fees. No reservation. |
| **Key link** | https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/custergallatin/recarea/?recid=60907 |

**This is the easiest permit logistics of any option on this list.** Just show up and go.

### Route 8: Beartooth High Lakes — Island and Night Lakes Loop

**The pitch:** A high-altitude lake-hopping route on the Beartooth Plateau, entirely above treeline. You camp at a glacial lake at 9,800+ feet with the vast tundra stretching in every direction. The Beartooth Plateau is the largest contiguous area above 10,000 ft in the lower 48, and this route puts you in the heart of it.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | ~10-14 miles RT (depending on route choices — the Beartooth is open-terrain navigation) |
| **Configuration** | Out-and-back or loop |
| **Nights** | 1 |
| **Elevation gain** | ~1,000-1,500 ft (you start high — the trailhead is at ~9,400 ft) |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate. The altitude (9,500-10,500 ft) is the challenge — you will feel it if you are not acclimatized. The terrain is open tundra with some boulder fields. Route-finding can be tricky when trails fade on the plateau — a GPS/map is essential. |

**Recommended itinerary (1 night):**
- Day 1: Start at the Beartooth High Lakes trailhead (off the Beartooth Highway near the summit, at Island Lake parking area). Hike past Island Lake and Beauty Lake (~2-3 miles), then continue cross-country or on faint trails to **Night Lake** or one of the dozens of unnamed tarns in the 9,800-10,200 ft range (~5-6 miles total).
- Camp at a lake's edge (200 ft from water). Sunset on the plateau — the sky is enormous.
- Day 2: Explore additional lakes or scramble a nearby highpoint (Beartooth Butte area, or just wander the tundra), then hike out.

**Alternate route: Losine Lake area**
- Start from the same trailhead area
- Hike south toward Losine Lake and the chain of lakes in the Beartooth Creek drainage
- Less visited than the Island/Beauty Lake corridor
- ~8-12 miles RT

**Camping:**
- No designated sites. Camp anywhere that is 200 ft from water and trails.
- The plateau is mostly flat, open tundra — tent sites are abundant. Choose a site protected from wind (behind a boulder cluster or in a slight depression).
- **Wind is the dominant hazard.** The Beartooth Plateau is exposed to extreme winds. Your tent must be well-staked and you should carry extra guylines/stakes. A freestanding tent is safer here than a trekking-pole shelter.

**Water:** Abundant. Glacial lakes and streams everywhere. Late August flows are reliable. Water is cold and clear but still requires treatment.

**Bear safety:**
- Bear activity: **LOW to MODERATE**. Grizzly bears are present in the Beartooth Wilderness but at lower density on the open plateau above treeline. They tend to use the forested drainages and lower elevations. The tundra has less food for them.
- Food storage: Bring your bear canister. No food poles anywhere. Even though bear density is lower, a bear visit is always possible.
- You are more likely to encounter mountain goats, pikas, and marmots than grizzlies on the plateau.

**Trailhead access:**
- Island Lake trailhead is directly off the Beartooth Highway (US-212), about 4 miles east of the summit and 30 miles west of Red Lodge.
- Large parking area. Very accessible — you literally park on the highway and start hiking.
- The Beartooth Highway is part of your loop route, so no detour required. You drive right past these trailheads.

**What makes it worth it:** The scale. Standing at 10,000 feet on open tundra with glacial lakes in every direction, no trees, no trails, no people — just sky and stone and water. Sunset turns the granite pink. Stars at 10,000 feet above treeline with no light pollution are the best you will ever see outside of the desert. This is not dramatic like the Tetons — it is vast and humbling.

**Honest trade-off:** The altitude hits hard if you are not acclimatized (you are flying in from sea level in Boston). Headaches, shortness of breath, and poor sleep are common above 9,500 ft on your first night at altitude. There is no shelter from weather — afternoon thunderstorms on the plateau are dangerous (lightning and hypothermia). The scenery is subtle — tundra-and-lakes rather than towering peaks. If you want drama, this is not the route. If you want solitude and wildness, this is hard to beat. And navigation on the plateau requires competence with a map/GPS — trails fade in and out on the tundra.

## First-Timers in Grizzly Country -- Overnight Edition

You have bear spray experience (from videos), you own a bear canister, and you know the basics of cooking away from camp. That is a solid foundation. Here is what you need to add for your first grizzly-country overnight.

### What You Already Know (Confirm These Habits)

- Cook and eat 200+ feet from your tent (ideally downwind)
- Store all food, toiletries, sunscreen, and anything scented in the bear canister
- Make noise on the trail to avoid surprising bears
- Know how to use bear spray (practice drawing from the holster — 1-2 seconds to firing position)

### What You Need to Add for Overnights

**1. The "Bear Triangle" camp setup**

Set up your camp as a triangle with 200+ feet between each point:
- **Point A: Tent** (upwind if possible)
- **Point B: Cooking area** (downwind from tent)
- **Point C: Bear canister** (at least 200 ft from tent, 100 ft from cooking area, and 200 ft from any water source)

This ensures that if a bear investigates your food (canister) or cooking area, it is far from where you sleep. The canister goes on the ground — bears may bat it around but cannot open an approved hard-sided canister. Place it on a flat, open area where you can see it from a distance (not hidden in bushes).

**2. Sleep with bear spray**

Keep bear spray inside the tent vestibule (or just inside the door) at night, within arm's reach. Make sure the safety clip is on but that you can draw and fire quickly. Practice this.

**3. Nothing scented in the tent — nothing**

This is the most critical overnight rule. No food, no wrappers, no toothpaste, no deodorant, no chapstick, no sunscreen. Everything goes in the canister. Wear clean sleeping clothes that you have NOT cooked in. Change out of your cooking clothes before getting in the tent and store them in or near the canister (or hung outside the tent, away from it).

**4. Know the common overnight bear encounter**

The most common overnight bear encounter is a bear walking through camp at night, investigating smells. If your food is in the canister and nothing scented is in your tent, the bear will typically investigate the canister, fail to open it, and leave. What to do:
- If a bear approaches your tent, make calm noise (talk firmly, "Hey bear"). Do not scream or run.
- If a bear is investigating your canister, let it. Do not leave your tent to confront it. It will fail and leave.
- If a bear is persistent and does not leave after 15-20 minutes of your making noise, use your air horn (if you have one) or bang pots.
- If a bear contacts your tent, yell loudly, spray through the mesh if needed. This is extremely rare if you have no food in the tent.

**5. Morning routine matters**

Before exiting your tent in the morning, unzip and look around. Bears are most active at dawn and dusk. Scan the area before stepping out. Make noise.

**6. Get a ranger briefing**

When you pick up your backcountry permit at the ranger station (Yellowstone or Grand Teton), you will receive a mandatory bear safety briefing from a ranger. Ask questions. Specific things to ask:
- "What is the current bear activity in the area we are camping?"
- "Are there any recent bear incidents or closures we should know about?"
- "Is there anything specific to this campsite we should be aware of?"

Rangers are there to help, and they will appreciate that you are taking it seriously.

**7. Buy bear spray locally**

You cannot fly with bear spray. Buy two cans (one per person) near the airport when you land:
- **Bozeman:** REI Bozeman, Schnees Boots & Shoes, Bob Ward & Sons (all carry Counter Assault or UDAP bear spray)
- **Jackson:** Teton Mountaineering, Skinny Skis, Jackson Hole general stores
- **Yellowstone general stores:** Available at most general stores inside the park, plus at Gardiner shops at the north entrance
- Cost: $40-55 per can
- Get the full-size cans (7-9 oz), not the miniature ones. Counter Assault and UDAP are the two most recommended brands.
- Buy holsters that attach to your hip belt or chest strap. Practice drawing.

**8. Carry an air horn (optional but recommended for first-timers)**

A small marine air horn ($10 at Walmart) is loud enough to scare off a curious bear at range. It is louder than yelling and does not require aiming. Good insurance for your first overnight.

### Honest Assessment of Risk

Grizzly bear attacks on backcountry campers are extremely rare. Yellowstone and Grand Teton together see roughly 1-3 bear incidents per year (most are daytime trail encounters, not overnight camp encounters). The vast majority of backcountry overnights are uneventful. Your bear canister, spray, and the food storage triangle reduce risk to near-zero. The biggest adjustment is psychological — you will lie in your tent at 2 AM listening to every sound. That is normal. It gets easier after the first night.

The routes in this document are ranked partly by bear risk. Death Canyon Shelf (moderate, high-elevation) and Beartooth High Lakes (low, above treeline) are the gentlest introductions. Heart Lake (high, designated bear management area) and Lamar River (very high) are more intense and better suited for a return trip after you have one grizzly-country overnight under your belt.

### Gear Checklist Specific to Grizzly-Country Overnights

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Bear canister** (approved hard-sided) | You own one. Confirm it is on the approved list for both Yellowstone and Grand Teton. BV500, Bear Boxer, Garcia, Counter Assault Cache are all accepted. |
| **Bear spray** (2 cans, one per person) | Buy at destination. Full-size cans (7+ oz). Counter Assault or UDAP. |
| **Bear spray holsters** (2) | Buy with the spray. Hip-belt or chest-strap mounting. Practice drawing. |
| **Dedicated sleeping clothes** | Clothes you have NOT cooked in. Change before bed. |
| **Ziplock bags** | For containing odors of trash, toiletries. Everything goes in the canister. |

## Summary: Your Decision Framework

Answer these questions to choose your route:

**1. How many nights can you spare from the overall loop?**
- 1 night: Heart Lake, Paintbrush overnight, or Beartooth High Lakes
- 2 nights: Death Canyon Shelf to Alaska Basin (the top recommendation)

**2. How comfortable are you with high bear activity for your first overnight?**
- Very comfortable: Heart Lake or Lamar River
- Moderate: Death Canyon Shelf or Paintbrush Canyon
- Prefer minimal bear exposure: Beartooth High Lakes

**3. Do you want to plan in advance or decide spontaneously?**
- Plan in advance: Death Canyon Shelf (permit needed, competitive) or Heart Lake (permit needed)
- Decide on the trip: Beartooth High Lakes (no permit, no reservation, just go)

**4. What scenery do you value most?**
- Dramatic peaks and ridges: Death Canyon Shelf or Paintbrush Divide
- Lakes and hot springs with a summit: Heart Lake
- Vast alpine tundra and solitude: Beartooth High Lakes
- Waterfalls and river corridors: Bechler River

**5. How important is logistical simplicity?**
- Maximum simplicity: Beartooth High Lakes (no permit, on-route, no shuttle)
- Moderate complexity: Heart Lake (permit, but on-route, out-and-back)
- Most complex: Death Canyon Shelf to Alaska Basin (permit, car shuttle, two jurisdictions)
