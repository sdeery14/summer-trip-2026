## How to Use This Document

Each day offers a menu of 3-4 hike options grouped by effort level:

| Effort Level | What It Means for You |
|---|---|
| **Chill Day** | 5-8 miles, moderate elevation gain. You'll feel refreshed, not wiped. Good for recovery days, smoky days, or when you'd rather save energy for a brewery evening. |
| **Standard Day** | 8-14 miles, solid elevation. This is your baseline — a full day of hiking that'll tire your legs pleasantly. You do this in your sleep. |
| **Big Day** | 14-20+ miles, significant elevation or technical terrain. The days you'll brag about. Start early, finish tired, earn that sunset beer. |

**Pick based on weather, energy, and mood.** That's the whole point — you don't need to decide until morning.

**Trailhead timing rule of thumb (late August):**
- Popular trailheads (Jenny Lake, Canyon area, Fairy Falls): Arrive by 7:00 AM or you may not find parking
- Moderate trailheads (Slough Creek, Avalanche Peak): Arrive by 8:00 AM
- Quiet trailheads (Specimen Ridge, Beartooth Plateau, Death Canyon): Flexible, but earlier is always better for wildlife and solitude

## Day 0: Fly into BZN, Drive South to Tetons

**Context:** You land at BZN (Bozeman), pick up the rental car and bear spray, do a Walmart grocery run, and drive south to the Tetons (~3.5 hours via Gallatin Canyon / US-191 through the west side of Yellowstone). You probably arrive in the Teton area by evening. This is not a hiking day — it's a travel day. But the drive through Gallatin Canyon is scenic and there are a couple of stretch-your-legs options along the way.

### Option: Ousel Falls (Big Sky, MT — on the way)

- **Distance:** 1.6 miles round trip
- **Elevation Gain:** 240 ft
- **Trailhead:** Ousel Falls Trailhead, off Ousel Falls Road in Big Sky, MT (about 1 hour south of Bozeman, right along your route)
- **Parking:** Dedicated lot with good capacity
- **What it is:** A well-maintained trail along the South Fork of the Gallatin River to a dramatic 70-ft waterfall. Easy walk through forest and along the canyon rim. The falls are beautiful and the trail is a perfect leg-stretcher after a flight.
- **Crowd level:** Moderate — popular with Big Sky locals and resort visitors, but quick turnover
- **Why do this:** You're driving right through Big Sky. It takes 45 minutes total and gets your blood moving after the flight. Good introduction to Montana scenery.

### Option: Gallatin River Pullouts

- **Distance:** As much or as little walking as you want
- **What it is:** The drive through Gallatin Canyon follows the Gallatin River for 40+ miles. There are numerous pullouts where you can stop, walk to the river, and soak in the scenery. In late August, the river runs clear and the canyon walls are dramatic. This is a "stop when something looks beautiful" approach.

### Camp Tonight: Gros Ventre Campground or dispersed camping near the Tetons

## Day 1: Grand Tetons — Big Day

**Context:** This is your first full day, and you're in Grand Teton National Park with the most dramatic alpine scenery of the entire trip towering above you. The Tetons offer steep, cathedral-like peaks as the backdrop to every hike. You're fresh and excited — this is the day to tackle the marquee hike. You slept at Jenny Lake (if you scored a FCFS site at dawn) or Gros Ventre campground, and you're ready to go.

**Pro tip:** For the Paintbrush-Cascade loop (the marquee hike), start from the String Lake Trailhead, going counterclockwise (Paintbrush Canyon up, Cascade Canyon down). This puts the steepest, most relentless climbing in Paintbrush Canyon first when you're fresh, and gives you the more gradual descent through Cascade Canyon when you're tired. Start by 6:00 AM — this is a long day and you want to be off the high passes before afternoon thunderstorms build.

### Chill Day: Taggart Lake and Bradley Lake Loop

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 6 miles loop |
| **Elevation Gain** | 600 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Taggart Lake Trailhead, on Teton Park Road about 3 miles south of Jenny Lake |
| **Parking** | Medium lot, maybe 40-50 cars. Fills by 9 AM in peak season. Arrive by 8 AM or go late afternoon. |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate — popular with families and casual hikers. Less crowded in late August. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None |

**What makes it special:** Two glacial lakes at the base of the Teton Range with stunning mountain reflections in calm morning light. The loop connects both lakes through a fire-regeneration area (the 2003 fire opened up views that didn't exist before). Bradley Lake is slightly less visited than Taggart and has better mountain reflections. This is a good warm-up or recovery day hike that still puts you right at the foot of the Tetons.

**Key waypoints:**
- Taggart Lake (mile 1.6) — turquoise glacial lake with the Grand Teton rising behind it
- Continue on the loop to Bradley Lake (mile 3) — more secluded, better reflections
- Return via the loop trail through moraine hills back to the trailhead

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Taggart Lake Bradley Lake Loop Grand Teton"

### Standard Day: Lake Solitude via Cascade Canyon

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 14.2 miles round trip (from Jenny Lake boat dock, taking the boat shuttle across) or 18 miles round trip (hiking around the south shore of Jenny Lake) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 2,700 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Jenny Lake Visitor Center / Jenny Lake Boat Dock. Take the shuttle boat across Jenny Lake ($18/person round trip in 2025 — [VERIFY 2026] for current price) to save 4 miles of flat walking. |
| **Parking** | Jenny Lake South lot is small (maybe 50 cars) and fills by 8 AM. Overflow lots along the road add capacity but fill too. Arrive by 7:00 AM or use the larger String Lake lot and walk/bike the connecting trail. |
| **Crowd Level** | Heavy in lower Cascade Canyon (first 3 miles). Moderate to Lake Solitude. Light above the lake. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking. Boat shuttle runs continuously from ~7 AM to 6 PM (varies by season — [VERIFY 2026]). |

**What makes it special:** Cascade Canyon is the most popular trail in Grand Teton NP for good reason: it cuts directly into the heart of the Teton Range between towering peaks. The canyon is broad and U-shaped (carved by glaciers), with a rushing creek, wildflower meadows, and massive granite walls rising thousands of feet on both sides. Moose are frequently seen in the lower canyon. Lake Solitude sits in an alpine cirque at 9,035 ft beneath the north face of the Grand Teton — a green-blue glacial lake surrounded by snow and rock. Despite the name, Lake Solitude is not particularly solitary on summer afternoons, but it's still a magnificent destination.

**Key waypoints:**
- Jenny Lake boat shuttle: saves 2 miles each way. Worth the $18.
- Cascade Canyon junction (0.5 miles from the west shore boat dock): turn left (south) into Cascade Canyon. The right fork goes to Hidden Falls and Inspiration Point — worth a quick detour (0.5 miles, 200 ft gain to Inspiration Point for views over Jenny Lake).
- Mile 0-4.5 (in the canyon): Gentle grade along Cascade Creek. Moose, elk, and pikas common. The walls of the canyon are massive — the Grand Teton (13,775 ft) towers above to the south.
- Mile 4.5-7.1: The trail steepens, leaving the canyon floor and climbing through switchbacks to Lake Solitude
- Lake Solitude (mile 7.1 from the boat dock): alpine cirque lake at 9,035 ft. Snowfields linger into August. The Grand Teton is visible above.

**Why choose this one:** This is the Teton classic — a magnificent canyon hike to an alpine lake with the highest peak in the range as the backdrop. The boat shuttle makes it efficient. If you can't do the full Paintbrush-Cascade loop (Big Day option), Lake Solitude is the next best thing.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Lake Solitude via Cascade Canyon Grand Teton"

### Standard Day Alternative: Death Canyon to the Shelf

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 10 miles round trip (to the Shelf and back) or 12 miles loop (Shelf to Static Peak Divide and back via same trail) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 3,000 ft to the Shelf; 4,200 ft if continuing to Static Peak Divide |
| **Trailhead** | Death Canyon Trailhead, at the end of a 1.5-mile rough road south of Teton Village. The road is unpaved but passable for any vehicle — slow and bumpy. |
| **Parking** | Small lot, maybe 15-20 cars. Fills by 8-9 AM. There's an overflow area on the road. |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate in the lower canyon. Light on the Shelf. Solitude at Static Peak Divide. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking |

**What makes it special:** Death Canyon Shelf is one of the most stunning features in the Teton Range — a broad, flat bench at 9,700 ft that runs for 2+ miles along the west side of the range, like a hanging balcony above the valley. You climb steeply through forest and then up the canyon wall to suddenly emerge onto this improbably flat shelf of alpine meadows with views west over Alaska Basin and the Jedediah Smith Wilderness. It feels like walking on a suspended platform in the sky. If you continue to Static Peak Divide (10,790 ft), the views become even more dramatic — you look straight down into both Death Canyon and the Jackson Hole valley, with the Grand Teton and Mount Moran visible to the north.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-1: Phelps Lake overlook (short spur — worth the 5-minute detour for views over the lake)
- Mile 1-3: Descent to Phelps Lake, then steep climb up Death Canyon — the canyon walls close in dramatically
- Mile 3-5: The Shelf — you emerge from the forest onto the broad alpine bench. Walk along it. Find a lunch spot on the edge with views into Alaska Basin.
- Optional extension to Static Peak Divide: another 2 miles and 1,200 ft of gain from the Shelf. The divide at 10,790 ft is one of the highest points you can reach on a maintained trail in the Tetons.

**Why choose this one:** Death Canyon Shelf is the "going deeper" option in the Tetons. While most hikers are in Cascade Canyon, the Shelf offers comparable scenery with a fraction of the people and a more dramatic sense of exposure — you're walking on a narrow bench with cliffs above and below. Static Peak Divide is a legitimate high-altitude objective.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Death Canyon Shelf Trail Grand Teton" or "Static Peak Divide Grand Teton"

### Big Day: Paintbrush Canyon to Cascade Canyon Loop

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 19.5 miles loop (from String Lake Trailhead, counterclockwise) or 15.7 miles with the Jenny Lake boat shuttle for the Cascade Canyon descent |
| **Elevation Gain** | 4,350 ft |
| **Trailhead** | String Lake Trailhead (if doing the full loop on foot) or Jenny Lake Visitor Center (if using the boat shuttle for the return leg). String Lake has a larger parking lot. |
| **Parking** | String Lake: medium-large lot, maybe 60-70 cars. Fills by 8 AM in peak season. Arrive by 6:30 AM. Jenny Lake: smaller lots, fill earlier. |
| **Crowd Level** | Light to moderate in Paintbrush Canyon (fewer people start here). Moderate in upper Cascade Canyon. Heavy in lower Cascade Canyon (as you exit). |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking. [VERIFY 2026] Check NPS trail status — the Paintbrush Divide (10,720 ft) can hold snow into August in heavy snow years. Microspikes may be necessary early in the season. By late August, it's usually snow-free. |

**What makes it special:** This is the marquee day hike in the Teton Range and one of the best day hikes in North America. The loop connects two spectacular glacier-carved canyons via Paintbrush Divide (10,720 ft), a rocky notch with views in every direction that makes you feel like you're standing on the spine of the world. You traverse from the wildflower meadows and waterfalls of Paintbrush Canyon, over the rugged alpine divide, and down through the broad, glacier-polished Cascade Canyon with the Grand Teton looming above. The variety is extraordinary — lakes, meadows, waterfalls, alpine tundra, glacial moraines, and granite walls — all in a single day.

**Key waypoints (counterclockwise from String Lake):**

- **Mile 0-2:** String Lake to Paintbrush Canyon junction. Flat walking along the lake, then left turn into Paintbrush Canyon.
- **Mile 2-4:** Lower Paintbrush Canyon. Gentle climb through forest with the creek rushing beside you. Wildflowers in the meadows. Watch for moose.
- **Mile 4-6:** Upper Paintbrush Canyon. The grade steepens. You climb through flower-filled meadows with towering rock walls on both sides. Holly Lake (at mile 5.5) sits in a gorgeous glacial cirque — a worthwhile side trip (0.5 miles off the main trail).
- **Mile 6-8:** The climb to Paintbrush Divide (10,720 ft). Steep switchbacks over rocky terrain. This is the hardest section physically. The final approach to the divide is over rocky moraine and possibly lingering snow patches (late August is usually clear). The divide itself is a narrow rocky saddle with staggering views in both directions.
- **Mile 8-10:** Descent from the divide into the upper Cascade Canyon. Rocky trail, steep switchbacks. You pass Lake Solitude (9,035 ft) — the green-blue glacial lake in its granite bowl.
- **Mile 10-15:** Cascade Canyon descent. The gradient eases and the canyon broadens. Cascade Creek rushes alongside. Moose, marmots, pikas. The walls of the canyon are enormous — the Grand Teton and surrounding peaks rise thousands of feet above you.
- **Mile 15-16:** The Canyon forks junction — left to Hurricane Pass (don't go that way today), right to continue toward Jenny Lake.
- **Mile 16-17:** Pass Hidden Falls (a beautiful 200-ft waterfall) and Inspiration Point (overlook above Jenny Lake).
- **Mile 17-19.5:** If hiking the full loop, walk around Jenny Lake's east shore back to String Lake Trailhead. If using the boat shuttle, catch it at the west shore dock and ride across, then walk 2 miles from Jenny Lake to String Lake to retrieve your car (total 15.7 miles).

**Strategy for the boat shuttle:**
- Going counterclockwise (recommended), you'd descend Cascade Canyon and exit at the Jenny Lake west shore boat dock. Take the shuttle boat across (saves 2 miles of flat walking). Then walk or arrange transport the 2 miles north along the road/trail from Jenny Lake to String Lake where your car is parked.
- The boat shuttle runs until about 6 PM. If you started by 6 AM and kept a steady pace, you should easily finish by 4-5 PM.

**Why choose this one:** Because it's one of the best day hikes you will ever do, anywhere, period. The combination of two canyon systems, an alpine divide crossing, and the raw scale of the Teton peaks is unmatched in the lower 48 for a single-day loop hike. You'll finish exhausted, grinning, and certain that this was the right trip. This is the hike you came for.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Paintbrush Canyon Cascade Canyon Loop Grand Teton"

### Big Day Alternative: Table Mountain (from the West Side)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 12 miles round trip |
| **Elevation Gain** | 4,100 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Table Mountain Trailhead in the Jedediah Smith Wilderness, accessed from Driggs, ID via the Teton Canyon Road. About 1 hour drive from Jenny Lake area (over Teton Pass, 8,431 ft). |
| **Parking** | Medium lot at the end of Teton Canyon Road. Usually available in late August. |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate — popular hike but crowds are lighter than the east side. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking. The trailhead is in Caribou-Targhee National Forest (west side of the Tetons). |

**What makes it special:** Table Mountain (11,106 ft) is the peak from which William Henry Jackson took the first photographs of the Tetons in 1872 — the images that helped convince Congress to establish the national park. From the flat summit, you look directly across at the Grand Teton from the west side — a perspective most people never see. The mountain is a massive table-shaped summit with sheer cliffs on three sides and a hiking trail on the fourth. The view of the Grand Teton and the full range from this angle is arguably the best single view in the Tetons.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-3: Trail climbs steadily through forest in Teton Canyon (also known as North Fork Teton Canyon on some maps)
- Mile 3-4: Enter Alaska Basin approach — meadows and boulder fields
- Mile 4-5: Steep switchbacks to the Table Mountain plateau
- Mile 5-6: Final approach across the flat summit plateau to the eastern edge and the Grand Teton view

**Why choose this one:** If the Paintbrush-Cascade loop doesn't appeal (or if Paintbrush Divide still has snow), Table Mountain is the best alternative big day in the Tetons. The view of the Grand Teton from the summit is jaw-dropping, and coming from the west side gives you a completely different perspective on the range.

**Logistics note:** This requires driving to Driggs, ID — about 1 hour from Jenny Lake. If you're doing a bouldering day at Teton Boulder Park (near Driggs) on Day 2, you could combine Table Mountain with the bouldering day to minimize driving over Teton Pass.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Table Mountain Trail Grand Teton" or "Table Mountain Jedediah Smith Wilderness"

## Day 2: Grand Tetons — Bouldering + Shorter Hike

**Context:** If you crushed the Paintbrush-Cascade loop yesterday, your legs are talking to you. Today is a natural bouldering day — Teton Boulder Park near Driggs, ID is about an hour from the Jenny Lake area over Teton Pass. She gets to send on real rock at altitude, and you can combine it with a shorter hike either on the west side of the Tetons (near Driggs) or back on the east side. Alternatively, if the weather is off or you want a full hiking day, this menu has recovery-friendly options.

**Pro tip:** Drive to Driggs in the morning for bouldering. Teton Boulder Park has world-class granite boulders with problems V0-V12. Rent pads at Yostmark Mountain Equipment in Driggs (see bouldering feature doc). After a bouldering session, do a shorter hike nearby or drive back over Teton Pass for an evening hike and dinner in Jackson. Snake River Brewing is the move.

### Chill Day: String Lake and Leigh Lake

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 7.5 miles round trip (String Lake loop + walk to Leigh Lake and back) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 200 ft |
| **Trailhead** | String Lake Trailhead (same as the Paintbrush-Cascade loop start) |
| **Parking** | String Lake lot. Fills by 9 AM in peak season but has more capacity than Jenny Lake. |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate at String Lake (popular swimming spot). Light at Leigh Lake. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None |

**What makes it special:** String Lake is a shallow, relatively warm lake (by Teton standards) that's popular for swimming, paddleboarding, and canoeing. The loop trail circles the lake with constant views of Mount Moran (12,605 ft), one of the most photogenic peaks in the range. Continuing north to Leigh Lake takes you to a quieter, deeper lake with stunning reflections of Moran. This is a recovery-day hike — low effort, high beauty, with the option to swim.

**Key waypoints:**
- String Lake loop (3.5 miles) — flat, scenic, good for active recovery
- Leigh Lake (2 miles north of String Lake) — quieter, more remote feel. Beautiful pebble beaches.
- Swimming in String Lake is best at the south end near the picnic area — warmest water

**Pair with:** Afternoon canoe/kayak rental on String Lake or Jenny Lake. Evening in Jackson.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "String Lake Leigh Lake Loop Grand Teton"

### Chill Day Alternative: Schwabacher Landing and Mormon Row (Photography Walk)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 2-3 miles (casual walking between photo spots) |
| **Elevation Gain** | Negligible |
| **Access** | Schwabacher Landing: signed turnoff from US-191 about 4 miles south of Moose Junction. Short dirt road to the river. Mormon Row: signed turnoff to the east about 1 mile south of Moose Junction. |
| **Parking** | Schwabacher: small lot, fills at sunrise for photographers. Mormon Row: roadside parking near the Moulton Barns. |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate at sunrise (photographers), light otherwise |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None |

**What makes it special:** These are the iconic Teton photo locations. Schwabacher Landing is a beaver pond area along the Snake River where the full Teton Range reflects in calm water at sunrise — the classic postcard image. Mormon Row has the historic John and T.A. Moulton barns with the Tetons as backdrop — perhaps the most photographed barns in America. Neither requires real hiking, but if you're a morning person, sunrise at Schwabacher is a near-spiritual experience. This is a good option if you want to be lazy and just soak in the scenery.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Schwabacher Landing Grand Teton" or "Mormon Row Grand Teton"

### Chill Day Alternative: Phelps Lake Loop (via Laurance S. Rockefeller Preserve)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 6 miles loop |
| **Elevation Gain** | 500 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Laurance S. Rockefeller Preserve Center, on Moose-Wilson Road about 4 miles south of Moose Junction |
| **Parking** | Small lot, maybe 40 cars. [VERIFY 2026] Parking reservation system may be in effect — the preserve limits the number of visitors. Check NPS Grand Teton website. |
| **Crowd Level** | Light to moderate — the parking limit keeps crowds manageable |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None, but parking may require a reservation |

**What makes it special:** The Rockefeller Preserve was the Laurance Rockefeller family's private ranch until 2001, when it was donated to the park. The preserve center is a beautiful, LEED-certified building with interpretive exhibits about the Teton landscape (worth 20 minutes inside). The loop trail circles Phelps Lake through old-growth forest, wildflower meadows, and along the lakeshore with views of Death Canyon's walls rising above. The trail is exceptionally well-maintained and peaceful — the parking cap means you'll never feel crowded. There's a small beach at the lake's south end for swimming.

**Key waypoints:**
- Preserve Center (stop inside first — it sets the mood)
- Lake Creek Trail to the lakeshore — views open up dramatically
- Phelps Lake beach (south end) — cold, clear water. Good for a dip on a hot day.
- Return via Woodland Trail through mature spruce-fir forest

**Why choose this one:** This is the most "curated" hiking experience in the Tetons — serene, well-maintained, and never overcrowded. Perfect for a recovery day or to pair with a half-day of bouldering.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Phelps Lake Loop Laurance Rockefeller Preserve"

### Standard Day: Garnet Canyon (to the Platforms)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 10 miles round trip (to the Platforms/Meadows) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 2,800 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Lupine Meadows Trailhead, on the Lupine Meadows Road south of Jenny Lake |
| **Parking** | Medium lot, maybe 40 cars. Fills by 7 AM — this is the main trailhead for Grand Teton mountaineering parties. Arrive early. |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate to the Meadows junction. Light beyond (most people turning off to the Middle Teton or Grand Teton). |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking to the Meadows/Platforms area. Mountaineering beyond the Platforms toward the Grand Teton requires experience and possibly a permit for overnight camping. |

**What makes it special:** Garnet Canyon is the mountaineers' route to the Grand Teton, and hiking to the Platforms (also called the Meadows) puts you deep in the heart of the range's most dramatic terrain. You climb 2,800 ft through forest and then rocky moraine to a flat bench at about 10,000 ft with the Middle Teton, South Teton, and Grand Teton towering above you on three sides. You're surrounded by massive granite walls, active glaciers (Middle Teton Glacier), and the detritus of mountain-building at its most dramatic. Climbers heading for the Grand Teton camp here — you'll share the Platforms with serious mountaineers and their stories.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-1.5: Steep switchbacks through forest — relentless uphill from the start
- Mile 1.5-3: Open slopes with views back over the valley. The trail is the most sustained steep hiking in the Tetons.
- Mile 3-4: Enter Garnet Canyon proper. Rocky terrain, boulder fields. The canyon narrows.
- Mile 4-5: The Platforms/Meadows — a surprisingly flat area surrounded by peaks. The Grand Teton is directly above to the north. Middle Teton Glacier is visible. This is your turnaround for a day hike.

**Why choose this one:** This is the closest you'll get to the Grand Teton's base without ropes and mountaineering gear. The terrain is raw and alpine — talus fields, glacial moraines, and towering granite. It's a very different experience from the canyon hikes (Cascade, Death Canyon) — more vertical, more exposed, more mountain-feeling.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Garnet Canyon Trail Grand Teton"

### Standard Day Alternative: Surprise and Amphitheater Lakes

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 10.1 miles round trip |
| **Elevation Gain** | 3,000 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Lupine Meadows Trailhead (same as Garnet Canyon) |
| **Parking** | Same as Garnet Canyon — arrive by 7 AM |
| **Crowd Level** | Moderate — this is a popular hike, but less so than Cascade Canyon |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking |

**What makes it special:** This trail shares the first 1.7 miles with the Garnet Canyon trail, then branches left to climb to two alpine lakes set in a high cirque directly beneath the Grand Teton. Surprise Lake (9,540 ft) is a beautiful glacial lake in a forested bowl. A short scramble above it leads to Amphitheater Lake (9,698 ft) — a stark, treeless tarn at the base of Disappointment Peak's massive granite wall. The sense of scale is staggering — you're sitting beside a lake with a 4,000-ft granite wall rising directly behind it. This is one of the highest lakes you can reach on a maintained trail in the Tetons.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-1.7: Steep switchbacks through forest (same as Garnet Canyon approach)
- Mile 1.7: Trail junction — left for Surprise/Amphitheater, right for Garnet Canyon
- Mile 1.7-4.5: Continued steep climbing through forest and open slopes. Views begin to open up.
- Mile 4.5: Surprise Lake — lovely, partially forested. Good rest stop.
- Mile 4.5-5: Short, steep climb to Amphitheater Lake. The trees fall away and you're in a granite amphitheater.
- Amphitheater Lake: stark alpine beauty. Grand Teton and Disappointment Peak tower above. Snow patches and rocky terrain surround the lake.

**Why choose this one:** This is the best summit-lake experience in the Tetons. The combination of 3,000 ft of climbing and the payoff of Amphitheater Lake — sitting in a giant granite bowl beneath the highest peak in the range — is hard to beat. It's less crowded than Lake Solitude and arguably more dramatic.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Surprise Lake Amphitheater Lake Trail Grand Teton"

### Big Day: Teton Crest Trail Section — Death Canyon Shelf to Alaska Basin

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 16-18 miles (out-and-back from Death Canyon Trailhead to Alaska Basin and back, or loop via Teton Canyon with car shuttle) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 4,500 ft |

**What makes it special:** Alaska Basin is a massive, high-altitude alpine basin on the west side of the Teton divide, filled with lakes, wildflower meadows, and granite boulders. Getting there as a day hike from the east side involves climbing through Death Canyon and traversing the Death Canyon Shelf (see Day 1 Standard alternative), then continuing over the Fox Creek Pass (9,600 ft) and descending into Alaska Basin. **Key waypoints:**
- Death Canyon approach and Shelf (see Day 1 Death Canyon description)
- Fox Creek Pass (9,600 ft) — the saddle between Death Canyon and Alaska Basin
- Alaska Basin: multiple lakes (Sunset Lake, Basin Lakes), expansive meadows, views of the Teton peaks from the west
- Return via the same route (massive day) or, with a car shuttle, continue west and descend Teton Canyon to the Teton Canyon Trailhead near Driggs

**Why choose this one:** This is a sampler of the Teton Crest Trail — one of the top 10 backpacking routes in America — done in a single day. Alaska Basin is otherworldly: a broad alpine garden surrounded by peaks, with lakes and wildflowers everywhere. If you're fit enough for an 18-mile day with 4,500 ft of gain (and you are), this is an experience most people take 3-4 days to see.

**Logistics note:** If doing this as a point-to-point with a car shuttle ending at Teton Canyon Trailhead near Driggs, you end on the west side — perfect to combine with bouldering at Teton Boulder Park or dinner in Driggs.

### Chill Day: South Rim Trail and Uncle Tom's Trail + Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 5.5 miles (combining South Rim Trail, Uncle Tom's Trail, and North Rim Trail) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 1,000 ft (including Uncle Tom's Trail — 328 steel steps down to the base of the Lower Falls) |

- Inspiration Point (North Rim) — wider canyon view, somewhat less crowded
- The connecting trails between viewpoints follow the canyon rim and add solitude between the concentrated viewpoint crowds.

**Why choose this one:** Because you should see it. This is Yellowstone's most dramatic landscape feature, and the Uncle Tom's Trail descent makes it a physical experience, not just a viewpoint. Pair it with a geothermal stop (Norris Geyser Basin or Old Faithful) in the afternoon.

| **Trailhead** | Avalanche Peak Trailhead, across from Eleanor Lake on the road between Yellowstone Lake (Fishing Bridge) and the East Entrance. Look for the unmarked pullout — there is no sign. |
| **Parking** | Small pullout, maybe 6-8 cars. Rarely fills — most people don't know this trail exists. |
| **Crowd Level** | Light — this is one of Yellowstone's best-kept secrets for a summit hike |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | [VERIFY 2026] Sometimes closed until July 4 for bear management (grizzlies use the snowfields). Usually open by late August. No permit needed. |

**What makes it special:** Avalanche Peak packs more elevation gain per mile than almost any other trail in Yellowstone. It's short but brutally steep — 2,100 ft of gain in just over 2 miles. The trail is unmaintained and unmarked (no sign at the trailhead — just a small pullout), which keeps crowds away. You climb through forest, break into alpine meadows, and then scramble up a rocky ridge to the 10,568 ft summit. The views rival Mount Washburn but with a fraction of the people: Yellowstone Lake spreads out below you, the Absarokas wall the eastern horizon, and you can see the steam plumes from the thermal areas 30 miles away.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-0.5: Forest climb, steep but trail is visible
- Mile 0.5-1.5: Open slopes — steep, loose in places. Early-season snow fields possible but unlikely in late August.
- Mile 1.5-2.2: Rocky ridge scramble to the summit. Class 2 terrain — hands-on-rock in a few spots but nothing technical.
- Summit: 10,568 ft. Windswept. Bring a layer.

**Why choose this one:** This is the "go deeper" mountain summit in Yellowstone. Where Mount Washburn gives you a fire lookout and a crowd, Avalanche Peak gives you a rugged scramble and solitude. The effort-to-views ratio is extraordinary. If you can handle 2,100 ft of steep gain in 2 miles, this is the better summit experience.

### Big Day: Electric Peak

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 18-20 miles round trip |
| **Elevation Gain** | 5,000 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Glen Creek Trailhead, near Bunsen Peak Trailhead, south of Mammoth Hot Springs (about 5 miles south of Mammoth on the road to Norris) |
| **Parking** | Small lot at Glen Creek/Bunsen Peak area. Arrive early. |
| **Crowd Level** | Solitude beyond the first 3 miles. You'll see almost no one on the upper mountain. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking. The upper mountain is fully exposed — this is not a safe hike in thunderstorm conditions. Check the forecast and be prepared to turn back. |

**What makes it special:** Electric Peak (10,969 ft) is the highest peak in the Gallatin Range within Yellowstone and one of the most challenging day hikes in the park. Named for the electric shocks early survey teams felt from static electricity on the summit during storms, it's a genuine mountain climb that takes you from the sagebrush flats near Mammoth to the windswept summit of a peak that towers 5,000 feet above your starting point. The upper mountain involves Class 2-3 scrambling on loose rock — not technical climbing, but it requires confidence on steep, exposed terrain. From the top, you see the entire northern half of Yellowstone, the Gallatin Range stretching north to Bozeman, and the Absaroka Range to the east.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-3: Glen Creek Trail through open sagebrush flats. Elk and pronghorn common. Gentle.
- Mile 3-5: The trail enters the Sportsman Lake Trail corridor and begins climbing through forest.
- Mile 5-7: Leave the maintained trail and follow the user trail up Electric Peak's southwest ridge. Steep, sustained climbing through alpine meadows.
- Mile 7-9: Upper mountain — loose rock, scrambling sections, exposed ridge. Class 2-3 terrain. The final 500 ft is the steepest and most exposed.
- Summit: 10,969 ft. Spectacular. If thunderstorms threaten, do not be here.

**Why choose this one:** This is the biggest, hardest day hike you can do in Yellowstone proper. 5,000 ft of elevation gain on a 20-mile day is a serious undertaking, and the upper mountain scramble adds a mountaineering dimension. You'll be completely alone above treeline. This is the hike for the day when you want to push your absolute limit.

**Important note:** This hike requires an early start (6 AM or earlier) and puts you at high altitude on an exposed summit. Be off the summit by noon to avoid afternoon thunderstorms. The descent on tired legs over loose rock requires care. Electric Peak is near Mammoth, which may add driving if you're camped near Canyon Village (about 45 minutes).

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Electric Peak Trail Yellowstone"

### Big Day: Specimen Ridge Traverse

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 17-19 miles (point-to-point, requires car shuttle or hitchhike) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 3,400 ft cumulative |
| **Trailhead (start)** | Specimen Ridge Trailhead, near the Yellowstone River bridge on the road between Tower Junction and Lamar Valley |
| **Trailhead (end)** | Lamar Valley Trailhead area (near the Lamar Buffalo Ranch) |
| **Parking** | Start: small pullout near the bridge. End: pullouts along the Lamar Valley road. Arrange a car shuttle by parking one car at each end, or plan on hitching back (ranger vehicles and friendly hikers frequently drive this road). |
| **Crowd Level** | Solitude — you may see 0-3 other parties all day |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking. Serious bear country — this ridge has frequent grizzly activity. Bear spray mandatory. |

**What makes it special:** Specimen Ridge is one of Yellowstone's best-kept secrets. The ridge contains the world's largest collection of petrified trees — not scattered fragments, but entire standing petrified tree trunks embedded in volcanic ash layers, stacked in multiple successive forests (up to 27 distinct forest layers fossilized on top of each other over millions of years). The traverse follows the ridgeline above the Lamar Valley with massive 360-degree views: the Absaroka Range to the east, the Lamar Valley below, and the Yellowstone interior to the south and west. You're on open alpine ridge for most of the day, above treeline, with wildflowers and ptarmigan. This is the kind of hike that makes you feel like an explorer.

**Key waypoints:**
- Mile 0-3: Steep climb from the Yellowstone River up to the ridgeline (this is the hardest part — 2,000 ft gain in 3 miles)
- Mile 3-5: Ridge walking with petrified tree specimens. Look for standing trunks on the northeast-facing slopes.
- Mile 5-10: The high traverse — open ridgeline above 9,000 ft with views in every direction. Amethyst Mountain (9,614 ft) is the high point.
- Mile 10-14: Descending the eastern end of the ridge toward the Lamar River
- Mile 14-19: Drop into the Lamar Valley and hike out to the road

**Logistics note:** This is a point-to-point traverse. Options:
1. **Car shuttle:** Park one vehicle at the Lamar Buffalo Ranch area and drive the other to the start trailhead (they're about 15 miles apart on the road). This is the cleanest option.
2. **Out and back from the west end:** Hike up to the ridge, explore the petrified forests and high terrain for several miles, and return the way you came. This makes it a 10-14 mile out-and-back with 2,500+ ft gain — still a worthy day.
3. **Hitchhike:** The road between Tower and Lamar is well-traveled. A hitchhike back to your car is very doable.

**Why choose this one:** This is the "going deeper than typical tourists" hike. Most Yellowstone visitors have never heard of Specimen Ridge. You'll hike all day on an open ridge above one of the most famous wildlife valleys in the world, past 50-million-year-old petrified forests, and probably won't see another person after the first 3 miles. This is the hike you'll talk about for years.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Specimen Ridge Trail Yellowstone"

## Day 8: Beartooth Highway and First Plateau Hikes

**Context:** Today you drive from Cooke City (just outside Yellowstone's Northeast Entrance) up and over Beartooth Pass (10,947 ft) to the Red Lodge area. ### Standard Day: Beartooth High Lakes — Beauty Lake and Becker Lake Loop

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 8-10 miles round trip (variable depending on route — much of this is cross-country on open tundra) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 1,200 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Beartooth Lake Trailhead or the unmarked pullout near the Beartooth Plateau summit area on US-212. The "trail" to Beauty Lake is partly maintained and partly cross-country. |
| **Parking** | Pullouts along the highway near the summit. The specific trailhead depends on which lakes you're targeting. |
| **Crowd Level** | Light to solitude — once you leave the highway, you're alone |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None. Shoshone National Forest. |

**What makes it special:** The Beartooth Plateau is one of the largest alpine tundra environments in the lower 48, and hiking across it feels like walking on the roof of the world. Beauty Lake and Becker Lake are glacial tarns sitting in granite basins above 9,500 ft. There are no real trails for much of this — you navigate by sight across open tundra, crossing streams and hopping between alpine meadows and granite slabs. The navigation is straightforward because you can see for miles in every direction. Wildflowers (if you're early enough in August) carpet the tundra. Mountain goats frequent the surrounding ridges.

**Key waypoints:**
- From Beartooth Lake, follow the trail southwest toward Beauty Lake (about 3 miles)
- Beauty Lake: deep blue glacial tarn in a granite bowl. Good lunch spot.
- Continue cross-country southeast to Becker Lake (about 1.5 miles of open tundra walking)
- Return via the same route or loop back to the highway (requires map and compass/GPS — load the route into Gaia GPS or CalTopo before you go)

**Why choose this one:** This is pure alpine wandering. No trees, no trail in places, just you and the tundra and the sky. It's the most "un-Yellowstone" hiking you'll do on this trip — more like hiking above treeline in Colorado or the Swiss Alps.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Beauty Lake Beartooth" or "Becker Lake Beartooth"

### Big Day: Beartooth Plateau Grand Traverse — Line Creek Plateau

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 14-16 miles (loop or out-and-back depending on route) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 2,200 ft |
| **Trailhead** | Line Creek Plateau Trailhead, near the Montana/Wyoming border on US-212 (about 10 miles west of Red Lodge on the highway). Alternatively, start from the Beartooth Pass summit area. |
| **Parking** | Pullouts and small parking areas along the highway |
| **Crowd Level** | Solitude — you will almost certainly be alone all day |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None. You'll cross between Shoshone and Custer Gallatin National Forests. |

**What makes it special:** Line Creek Plateau is a massive, flat-topped alpine plateau stretching south from the Beartooth Highway at elevations above 10,000 ft. This is wilderness walking at its purest: no trail, no trees, no people, just miles of tundra rolling toward the horizon with the Absaroka Range and Beartooth peaks surrounding you. You walk by compass and GPS across terrain that looks like the Arctic tundra transported to Wyoming. Alpine wildflowers, pikas, ptarmigan, and mountain goats are your only company. The plateau is broad enough that you can wander freely, choosing your own route between lakes, ridges, and meadows.

**Key waypoints:**
- Climb from the highway to the plateau rim (first 2 miles — steepest section)
- Line Creek Plateau proper: miles of open tundra above 10,000 ft
- Several unnamed alpine tarns for water and scenic breaks
- The plateau edge offers dramatic views into Line Creek canyon to the east
- Navigate to Sawtooth Mountain or Lonesome Mountain for optional summits (add 2-3 miles and 800+ ft gain)

**Navigation note:** There is no maintained trail for much of this route. Load your route into Gaia GPS or CalTopo before you go. Carry a compass. Weather can change fast at this altitude — be prepared to navigate in clouds. If thunderstorms build (common in summer afternoons), get off the exposed plateau immediately.

**Why choose this one:** This is the most remote, wild hiking you'll do on the entire trip. It's not technically difficult — the terrain is gentle for alpine tundra — but it demands confidence in navigation and weather judgment. The reward is one of the most spacious, empty landscapes you can reach on a day hike anywhere in the lower 48.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Line Creek Plateau Beartooth" — note that this may have limited AllTrails coverage. CalTopo topo maps are your best resource here.

### Standard Day: Lake Fork Trail to Lost Lake

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 10 miles round trip |

**What makes it special:** The Lake Fork trail follows Lake Fork of Rock Creek into the heart of the Beartooth Wilderness, climbing steadily through forest and alpine meadows to a chain of high lakes. **Why choose this one:** This is the classic Beartooth Wilderness day hike — maintained trail, beautiful high lake, and a taste of the vast lake-filled wilderness that stretches south for miles. It's also a great way to scout for a potential backpacking trip deeper into the range.

### Standard Day Alternative: East Rosebud Trail to Elk Lake

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 10 miles round trip |

### Big Day: Granite Peak Approach — Froze to Death Plateau

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Distance** | 14-18 miles round trip (depending on route and how far you go) |
| **Elevation Gain** | 3,500-4,500 ft |
| **Trailhead** | West Fork of Rock Creek Trailhead or Mystic Lake Trailhead (via the West Rosebud drainage, about 30 miles from Red Lodge) |
| **Parking** | Small lots at either trailhead |
| **Crowd Level** | Solitude — very few people venture to Froze to Death Plateau on day hikes. You may see mountaineering parties heading for Granite Peak. |
| **Permits/Restrictions** | None for day hiking. Deep Absaroka-Beartooth Wilderness. |

**What makes it special:** Granite Peak (12,799 ft) is the highest point in Montana — a serious technical mountain that most people spend 3-4 days climbing. You're not going to summit Granite Peak on a day hike, but you can hike to Froze to Death Plateau, the vast alpine plateau at about 10,500 ft that serves as the approach for Granite Peak climbers. The name alone is worth the trip. The plateau sits above a landscape of glacial lakes, snowfields, and granite towers, with Granite Peak's dramatic summit pyramid visible to the south. The scale of the terrain is staggering — you're on a tundra plateau the size of a small town, surrounded by Montana's highest and wildest mountains.

**Key waypoints (via Mystic Lake approach):**
- Mile 0-3: Trail to Mystic Lake — a large, beautiful lake in a dramatic canyon setting. Steady climb.
- Mile 3-6: Continue beyond Mystic Lake, climbing through increasingly alpine terrain
- Mile 6-8: Reach the plateau proper. The terrain opens up to vast alpine tundra with snowfields and granite. Granite Peak is visible to the south.
- Explore the plateau, visit unnamed tarns, and soak in the scale of the landscape

**Navigation note:** Above Mystic Lake, the trail becomes less maintained and route-finding is required. Load your route into Gaia GPS. This is genuinely remote wilderness — be prepared for changing weather and challenging terrain.

**Why choose this one:** This is the wildest, most remote day hike on the entire trip. You're walking into the approach for Montana's highest peak through terrain that feels like Patagonia. Even if you never get close to Granite Peak's summit, the plateau itself is a destination worthy of bragging rights.

**CalTopo/Gaia/AllTrails search:** "Froze to Death Plateau Beartooth" or "Mystic Lake Trail Beartooth" or "Granite Peak Approach"

## General Notes: Permits, Trailhead Timing, and Bear Safety

### Permits

**Day hiking in Grand Teton:** No permits required for any day hike. Just park and go.

**Day hiking in Yellowstone:** No permits required for any day hike. Just park and go.

**Day hiking in Beartooth / Shoshone NF:** No permits required. No entry fees for national forest land.

**Backcountry overnight permits:** Required for all overnight backcountry camping in both national parks. Not required in national forest / wilderness areas (but follow Leave No Trace and wilderness regulations). See the backpacking-options feature document for details.

### Bear Safety on the Trail

This is your first time hiking in grizzly country. The hiking-specific bear safety essentials:

1. **Bear spray:** Carry it on your hip or chest strap — not in your pack. Both of you should carry a can. Practice drawing and firing position (without actually firing) before you hit the trail.

2. **Make noise:** Talk, clap, or call out "hey bear" when approaching blind corners, dense vegetation, or stream crossings. Most bear encounters happen when you surprise a bear at close range. Don't be quiet hikers in grizzly country.

3. **Hiking in pairs:** You're a couple, so you'll naturally be together. Two people making normal conversation is usually enough noise to alert bears. Never use earbuds/headphones on the trail.

4. **Food on the trail:** Keep snacks in sealed bags. Eat in open areas where you can see around you. Pack out all food waste. On day hikes, this is simpler than backcountry — you're not cooking, just snacking.

5. **Carcasses and fresh sign:** If you smell something rotten or see a carcass, leave the area immediately. A grizzly may be guarding it nearby. If you see very fresh bear scat (wet, steaming) or fresh digging, be extra alert — you're in an area a bear was using recently.

6. **Trail closures:** Yellowstone and Grand Teton both close trails temporarily for bear management (especially when bears are feeding near trails in early summer). By late August, most closures have lifted, but check NPS trail status boards at visitor centers each morning.

7. **Dawn and dusk hiking:** Bears are most active at dawn and dusk. If you're starting very early (5-6 AM) for a big day, be especially noise-conscious in the first and last hours. That said, don't avoid dawn starts — just be aware and make noise.

8. **The reality check:** Grizzly encounters resulting in injury are extremely rare. Millions of people hike in grizzly country every year. Bear spray is effective in 98% of encounters when used properly. Be aware, be prepared, and then go enjoy the trails. Don't let bear anxiety ruin the trip.

### Altitude Considerations

- Teton valleys: 6,500-7,000 ft
- Teton summits/passes: 9,500-11,000 ft
- Yellowstone valleys: 6,000-7,500 ft
- Yellowstone summits: 10,000-11,000 ft
- Beartooth Plateau: 9,500-11,000 ft

You're coming from sea level (Arlington, MA). Altitude effects are generally noticeable above 8,000 ft and significant above 10,000 ft. Symptoms: shortness of breath on exertion, headache, fatigue.

**With the counterclockwise itinerary, your acclimatization progression is favorable:** You arrive and immediately go to the Tetons (valley floor ~6,700 ft), with hikes reaching 9,000-10,700 ft on Days 1-3. By the time you reach Yellowstone (Days 4-7, mostly 6,000-8,000 ft with summit hikes to 10,000-11,000 ft), you have several days of altitude exposure. When you hit the Beartooth Plateau (Days 8-10, base camp at 9,000-10,000 ft), you'll have been at altitude for a full week. The Paintbrush Divide crossing (10,720 ft on Day 1) will be your highest point while least acclimatized — start slow, drink water, and listen to your body. Ibuprofen helps with altitude headaches.

### Trail Conditions in Late August

- **Snow:** Most trails below 10,000 ft will be completely snow-free. Passes above 10,000 ft (Paintbrush Divide, Static Peak Divide) are usually clear by late August but may have lingering snow patches in heavy snow years. Check NPS trail reports. Microspikes are lightweight insurance for high passes — consider packing them.
- **Stream crossings:** Late August has the lowest water levels of the summer. Creek crossings that are dicey in June are trivial in August. You won't need to worry about fording.
- **Wildflowers:** Past peak at lower elevations, but alpine areas (Beartooth Plateau, Teton passes, Death Canyon Shelf) may still have flowers in late August.
- **Daylight:** In late August at this latitude, sunrise is around 6:30-6:45 AM and sunset around 8:00-8:15 PM. That gives you about 13.5 hours of daylight — plenty for even the biggest day hikes. By early September, you lose about 3 minutes of daylight per day.
- **Afternoon thunderstorms:** Common in the mountains, especially above treeline. The pattern is: clear morning, clouds building by noon, possible thunderstorms by 2-4 PM. For high-altitude hikes (Paintbrush Divide, Electric Peak, Beartooth Plateau, Table Mountain), plan to be off exposed summits and ridges by noon. Start early.

### What to Bring on Every Hike

This is for two experienced hikers — you know the basics. The Yellowstone/Teton specifics:

- **Bear spray** (both of you, on hip/chest, not in pack)
- **3+ liters of water each** (altitude and dry air dehydrate you faster than New England)
- **Rain layer** (afternoon thunderstorms are common)
