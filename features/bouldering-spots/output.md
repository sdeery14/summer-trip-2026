## Overview: Bouldering on the Yellowstone Loop

The Greater Yellowstone area is not a top-tier bouldering destination the way Bishop, Hueco Tanks, or Joe's Valley are — but it has one genuinely world-class area (Teton Boulder Park) and several worthwhile secondary spots. For a V10 climber, Teton Boulder Park alone justifies building a bouldering day into the itinerary. The other spots are good for casual sessions when the opportunity aligns with the route.

**The big picture:**

| Area | Quality Rating | Grade Range | Drive from Loop | Worth a Dedicated Day? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Teton Boulder Park** (Driggs, ID) | World-class | V0–V12+ | 1 hr from Jackson | **Yes** — this is the destination |
| **Gallatin Canyon** (MT) | Good roadside bouldering | V0–V10 | On the route (BZN to West Yellowstone) | No — great for a 1–3 hour session when driving through |
| **Jackson area scattered boulders** | Limited | V0–V6 | In the area | No — only if you have a spare hour |
| **Red Lodge / Beartooth area** | Very limited | Unknown / exploratory | On the route | No — not worth seeking out for bouldering specifically |

## Teton Boulder Park (Driggs, ID)

### The Main Event — World-Class Granite Bouldering

Teton Boulder Park is a legitimate bouldering destination that happens to sit on the west side of the Tetons, perfectly positioned for a dedicated day during the Greater Yellowstone Loop. This is high-quality Teton granite with featured faces, cracks, aretes, and slabs spread across a forested hillside. The area has been developed significantly over the past 15 years and now hosts hundreds of problems across all grades, with a concentration of quality lines in the V3–V10 range that will keep a V10 climber engaged for a full day.

### Location and Access

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Location** | East of Driggs, ID, on the western slope of the Teton Range. The boulders are in the Caribou-Targhee National Forest (public land — free access). |
| **Driving from Jackson, WY** | ~45 minutes to 1 hour via Teton Pass (WY-22 over the pass at 8,431 ft, then ID-33 into Driggs, then east toward the mountains). Teton Pass is steep (10% grade) but fully paved and well-maintained. |
| **Driving from Driggs** | ~15–20 minutes east of town, heading toward the mountains on Alta Road / Targhee Road area. |
| **Driving from Jenny Lake (Tetons east side)** | ~1 hour 15 minutes via Teton Pass. |
| **Driving from camp at Gros Ventre CG** | ~1 hour via Jackson and Teton Pass. |
| **Parking** | Pullouts and informal parking areas along the forest roads. No fee. Roads are generally passable by regular passenger car in summer, though some areas benefit from higher clearance. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check road conditions — some forest roads may have ruts after rain. |
| **Land status** | Caribou-Targhee National Forest — public land, no permits required, no climbing restrictions. |

### Rock Type and Quality

- **Rock:** Teton granite (gneiss) — the same rock that forms the Teton Range itself
- **Quality:** Excellent. High-friction granite with crystals, edges, crimps, and features. The rock is generally clean and well-featured. Landings vary from flat dirt to sloped/rocky — pad placement matters.
- **Style:** Varied — steep faces, technical slabs, aretes, cracks, roofs, and mantels. There is enough variety to keep any style of climber engaged.
- **Conditions:** Best on cool, dry days. Granite friction is significantly better when temperatures are below 70F. Morning shade is common in the forested areas. Late summer (your trip timing) should be good — mornings and evenings are ideal, midday in shade works fine.

### Grade Range and Problem Count

| Grade Range | Approximate Problem Count | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| V0–V3 | 80–100+ | Great warmups and fun moderates on featured granite |
| V4–V6 | 60–80+ | Deep selection of quality problems — the heart of the area |
| V7–V9 | 30–50+ | Many classic hard problems; this is where the area really shines for a strong climber |
| V10–V12+ | 15–25+ | Test-piece problems on the best rock. A V10 climber will find multi-session projects here. |
| **Total** | **200–300+** | The area continues to be developed; new problems are put up regularly |

### Classic Problems Worth Doing

These are widely regarded as the best problems at Teton Boulder Park. A V10 climber should target the starred problems for a single-day session.

#### Warmups and Moderates (V0–V4)

| Problem | Grade | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Various warmup traverses** | V0–V2 | Multiple boulders have long, fun traverses on featured granite — perfect for warming up |
| **Standard face problems** | V2–V4 | The area has dozens of good moderate face problems; wander and pick what looks good |

#### The Sweet Spot (V4–V7)

| Problem | Grade | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **The Wave** | V5 | One of the area's most popular problems. Technical face climbing on a beautiful wave-shaped feature. Great movement. |
| **Full Sail** | V5 | Steep, powerful climbing with good holds. One of the better-known problems in the park. |
| **Flying Squirrel** | V6 | A classic — dynamic movement on steep granite. Physical and fun. |
| **Teton Arete** (various arete problems) | V4–V6 | Several arete problems on clean granite — the arete style suits the rock beautifully |
| **Crimp face problems** | V5–V7 | The granite here rewards precision on small holds. Multiple face problems in this range are excellent. |

#### Hard Classics (V7–V10+)

| Problem | Grade | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **The Rumble** | V8 | Powerful, steep problem. One of the signature hard lines. Physical climbing on good granite. |
| **Teflon** | V9 | Technical and sustained face climbing. Requires excellent footwork and body tension on featureless granite — hence the name. |
| **Goldfinch** | V8 | Beautiful problem on great rock. Frequently cited as one of the best hard problems in the area. |
| **The Big Easy** | V10 | Despite the name, this is a sustained and technical testpiece. A great goal problem for a V10 climber visiting the area. |
| **Broken Wing** | V10 | Powerful climbing on steep terrain. One of the harder established lines. |

**Note:** Problem names, grades, and descriptions are based on community consensus from Mountain Project and guidebook information. Grades may feel different depending on your style (the granite here rewards crimp strength and footwork). **[VERIFY 2026]** Check Mountain Project or the local guidebook for the most current problem list, as new lines are developed each season.

### Area Sectors

Teton Boulder Park is organized into several sectors spread across the hillside:

- **Lower areas** — Shorter approach, easier access, good mix of grades
- **Upper areas** — Longer approach (15–30 min uphill), often better rock quality, less traffic
- **Scattered individual boulders** — Some of the best problems are on standalone boulders that require a short walk between them

A strong climber can sample multiple sectors in a day. Plan to spend at least 4–5 hours climbing to make the drive worthwhile.

### Conditions and Timing

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Best time of day** | Morning (before 10 AM) or late afternoon (after 4 PM) for best friction. Many boulders are in forest shade, so midday is workable if temps stay below ~75F. |
| **Late August conditions** | Excellent. Daytime highs in Driggs are typically 75–85F in late August, with mornings in the 40s–50s. Morning sessions will have superb friction. |
| **Rain** | Granite dries quickly. A morning rain may clear by afternoon. Do not climb on wet granite — it damages the rock and is dangerously slippery. |
| **Bugs** | Mosquitoes can be present in forested areas, especially near water. Bring repellent. Usually not terrible by late August. |
| **Crowds** | Low to moderate. This is not Bishop — you will likely have most problems to yourself, even on weekends. Weekday sessions will be very quiet. |

### Approach

- **Walk from car to boulders:** 5–30 minutes depending on which sector you visit. Lower areas are a short walk; upper areas require a moderate uphill hike through forest.
- **Terrain:** Forest trails and some off-trail walking. Not technical but can be brushy in spots. Good trail shoes are fine.
- **Finding problems:** A guidebook or Mountain Project topos are essential. The boulders are spread through forest and not all are visible from trails.

### Guidebook

- **Teton Bouldering** by local developers — check availability at Yostmark in Driggs or Teton Mountaineering in Jackson **[VERIFY 2026]**
- **Mountain Project:** https://www.mountainproject.com — search "Teton Boulder Park" or "Teton Bouldering" for topos, problem descriptions, and area maps. Download the Mountain Project app for offline access.
- **27 Crags** may also have topos for the area

## Gallatin Canyon (Montana)

### Roadside Bouldering on the Route

Gallatin Canyon runs along US-191 between Bozeman and West Yellowstone — a road you will very likely drive if flying into BZN. The canyon has roadside bouldering on gneiss and metamorphic rock, with boulders scattered along the Gallatin River. This is not a destination area, but it is a very convenient place to stop and climb for a session while driving through.

### Location and Access

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Location** | Along US-191 in the Gallatin Canyon, roughly between mile markers 47 and 62 (between Big Sky turnoff and the Yellowstone border). Gallatin National Forest — public land. |
| **Driving from Bozeman** | 30–60 minutes south on US-191, depending on which boulders you target. |
| **Driving from West Yellowstone** | 30–45 minutes north on US-191. |
| **Parking** | Roadside pullouts. Many boulders are visible from the road or within a 1–5 minute walk. No fee. |
| **Land status** | Gallatin National Forest (Custer Gallatin National Forest) — public land, no permits, no restrictions on bouldering. |

### Rock Type and Quality

- **Rock:** Gneiss and metamorphic rock — somewhat variable quality. Some boulders are excellent with featured granite-like climbing; others are chossy or lichen-covered.
- **Quality:** Mixed. The best boulders are good, but you need beta (Mountain Project or locals) to find the quality rock and skip the junk.
- **Style:** Face climbing, cracks, aretes. Tends toward crimpy and technical on the better rock.

### Grade Range and Problem Count

| Grade Range | Approximate Count | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| V0–V4 | 50+ | Many moderate problems, good for casual sessions |
| V5–V8 | 20–30+ | Some quality hard problems |
| V9+ | A handful | Limited but present |
| **Total** | **80–120+** | Spread along several miles of canyon, multiple distinct areas |

### Classic Problems

| Problem | Grade | Area/Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Various roadside moderates** | V0–V4 | The appeal of Gallatin Canyon is the convenience — pull over, walk 2 minutes, climb. Many good moderate problems on river boulders. |
| **Steep cave problems** | V5–V8 | Several steep/overhanging problems on larger boulders in the canyon. Physical climbing. |
| **Named testpieces** | V7–V9 | A few hard problems have developed reputations but the area is less documented than Teton Boulder Park. Check Mountain Project for current information. |

### Conditions and Timing

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Best time** | Morning or evening. The canyon runs roughly north-south, so boulders get variable sun depending on which side of the canyon they are on. River-level boulders can be cool even midday due to canyon shade and river proximity. |
| **Crowds** | Very low. You may see local Bozeman climbers on weekends but the boulders are spread out enough that it is never crowded. |
| **Approach** | 0–5 minutes from the car for most problems. This is the definition of roadside bouldering. |
| **Hazards** | The road is busy (US-191 is a main highway). Be careful with parking and crossing the road. Some boulders are near the river — be aware of water levels, especially in early summer (by late August, flows are lower). |

### When to Session Here

**Do not make a special trip for Gallatin Canyon bouldering.** Instead, plan to stop here for 1–2 hours when you are driving between Bozeman and Yellowstone (either direction). It is perfect for:
- A morning session after camping near Big Sky or on the way into the park
- A leg-stretching climbing break on a long drive day
- An evening session if camping near the West Yellowstone area

## Other Bouldering Near the Loop

### Jackson Area

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **What exists** | Scattered boulders in the Jackson area, including some along the road toward Teton Village and in Bridger-Teton National Forest. Nothing that constitutes a developed bouldering area comparable to Teton Boulder Park. |
| **Quality** | Limited development, limited documented problems. A few fun boulders but nothing worth seeking out for a V10 climber when Teton Boulder Park is an hour away. |
| **Recommendation** | Skip Jackson-area bouldering. Go to Teton Boulder Park instead. |

### Red Lodge / Beartooth Area

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **What exists** | Very little developed bouldering. There are rocks along the Beartooth Highway and in the Red Lodge area, but systematic bouldering development is minimal. Some scrambling/bouldering opportunities on roadside boulders along the Beartooth Highway at high elevation, but these are undocumented and the rock quality is variable (metamorphic, often lichenous). |
| **Quality** | Exploratory at best. The rock along the Beartooth is often not climbable quality (metamorphic, heavily weathered, lichen-covered). |
| **Recommendation** | Do not plan bouldering here. If you happen to see a clean boulder while hiking on the Beartooth Plateau, by all means scramble on it — but this is not a bouldering destination. |

### Bozeman Area (If You Have Time Near BZN)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **What exists** | Some bouldering in Hyalite Canyon (south of Bozeman), which is primarily known for ice climbing and sport climbing but has some boulders. Also scattered boulders in Gallatin Valley. |
| **Quality** | Moderate. Hyalite has some decent problems but it is a sport climbing area first. Not worth a special trip for bouldering when you are passing through Gallatin Canyon anyway. |
| **Indoor option** | Bozeman has indoor climbing gyms if weather is terrible and you need a climbing fix: **Spire Climbing Center** in Bozeman is a well-regarded gym. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check if Spire is still open and their day pass prices. |
| **Recommendation** | Only if you have a spare morning/evening in Bozeman and it is raining. Otherwise, save your skin for Teton Boulder Park. |

### Rental Strategy — Recommendation

1. **First call: Yostmark in Driggs** (208-354-2828). Reserve a pad at least 1–2 weeks before the trip. This is your best bet — they are in the right location and serve boulderers specifically.
2. **Backup call: Teton Mountaineering in Jackson** (307-733-3595). If Yostmark is out, try here.
3. **If both are unavailable:** Consider buying a pad (see next section) or using an online rental service.
4. **For Gallatin Canyon:** You can boulder without a pad on low problems if you are careful and have a spotter. If you want a pad for Gallatin Canyon specifically (and are not going to Driggs first), call Barrel Mountaineering in Bozeman.

## Buying a Pad Locally — Backup Option

If rental is not available, buying a cheap crash pad and either using it for the trip and donating/selling it before flying home is a viable option.

### New Pad Options

| Option | Where | Approximate Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Metolius Session II** | Any climbing shop (Yostmark, Teton Mountaineering) | $180–220 | Good quality, compact. One of the most affordable new pads from a reputable brand. |
| **Mad Rock Mad Pad** | Climbing shops or ordered online | $150–200 | Budget-friendly, functional. Adequate for a trip. |
| **Organic Simple Pad** | Order online, ship to accommodation | $200–280 | Excellent pad, but may not be available locally. |
| **Amazon budget pad** | Order to a hotel/campground/Amazon Locker in Jackson or Driggs | $80–150 | Brands like Beal, Evolv, or generic options. Quality varies. Fine for a week of use. |

### Used Pad Options

| Option | Where | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Facebook Marketplace** | Search Jackson Hole, Driggs, Bozeman | Active climbing communities = used pads available. Search "crash pad" or "bouldering pad." Post a WTB (want to buy) a week before arrival. |
| **Mountain Project For Sale forum** | Online | Search for pads for sale in the Jackson/Driggs area. |
| **Gear swap/consignment** | Yostmark or other local shops may have used gear sections | Ask when you call about rentals. |

### End-of-Trip Disposal

If you buy a pad, you cannot fly home with it (too large for luggage). Options:
- **Sell on Facebook Marketplace** — list it the day before you fly out. Climbers in Jackson/Driggs will buy a used pad quickly.
- **Donate to Yostmark or another shop** — some shops accept gear donations for lending library or community use.
- **Leave at the campground** — not ideal but someone will take it.
- **Ship home via UPS/FedEx** — expensive ($40–80+) but possible. FedEx and UPS have locations in Jackson.

### Recommendation

Renting is strongly preferred over buying. Buying is a $150–250 expense plus the hassle of disposal. Only go this route if rental is truly unavailable. If you do buy, a Metolius Session II from a local shop is the best balance of price, quality, and availability.

## Recommended Bouldering Plan for the 7-Day Itinerary

Based on the 7-Day Classic itinerary skeleton from the loop overview, here is how to integrate bouldering:

### Primary Bouldering Day: Day 6 (Teton Flex Day)

This is the day designated in the itinerary skeleton as the flex day in the Tetons. It is the ideal bouldering day.

| Detail | Plan |
|---|---|
| **The night before** | Camp at Jenny Lake, Gros Ventre, or Climbers' Ranch area. Pick up crash pad from Yostmark in Driggs on the way to the boulders (if you have not already). |
| **Morning** | Drive from Jackson/Teton area over Teton Pass to Driggs (~45–60 min). Stop at Yostmark when they open to pick up pad if not already in hand. Continue to Teton Boulder Park (~15 min from Driggs). |
| **Climbing session** | Arrive by 9–10 AM. Climb for 4–6 hours. The morning temps will be ideal for friction. Start with warmups on moderate granite, then work through the V5–V8 classics, and attempt the V9–V10 testpieces while you are fresh. |
| **Afternoon** | Options after climbing: (1) Lunch in Driggs — more affordable and chill than Jackson, (2) explore the west side of the Tetons from the Idaho side (Teton Canyon trailhead for a short hike), or (3) drive back over Teton Pass and hit Jackson for the evening — Snake River Brewing, dinner, and celebration vibes. |
| **Evening** | Return pad to Yostmark if it is a same-day rental, or keep it for a morning session the next day. Dinner in Driggs or Jackson. |

### Secondary Bouldering Opportunity: Day 0 or Day 1 (Gallatin Canyon)

If flying into Bozeman:

| Detail | Plan |
|---|---|
| **When** | On the drive from Bozeman to Yellowstone via US-191 (Gallatin Canyon). This could be Day 0 (arrival day, driving to the park) or any day you are transiting between Bozeman and the west entrance. |
| **Duration** | 1–2 hours. Pull over at known bouldering pullouts in the canyon. |
| **Pad situation** | You probably will not have a pad yet (it is in Driggs). Options: (1) rent a pad in Bozeman from Barrel Mountaineering before driving south, (2) boulder pad-free on low, safe problems with good landings and a spotter, or (3) skip it and save the skin for Teton Boulder Park. |
| **Recommendation** | If it is a travel day and you are tired from flying, skip Gallatin Canyon bouldering and just enjoy the drive. Save your skin and energy for Teton Boulder Park. If you have a spare afternoon and already have a pad, stop and climb. |

### Session Planning for a V10 Climber

A suggested session at Teton Boulder Park for a single-day visit:

| Phase | Duration | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Warmup** | 30–45 min | Easy traverses and V0–V2 problems. Get the granite friction dialed — it may feel different from your home gym or local rock. |
| **Moderate classics** | 1–1.5 hr | Work through V4–V6 problems. The Wave (V5), Full Sail (V5), Flying Squirrel (V6). Enjoy the movement and rock quality. |
| **Hard sends** | 1.5–2 hr | Target your V7–V9 range. The Rumble (V8), Goldfinch (V8), Teflon (V9). These are the problems that will challenge you and give the most satisfaction. |
| **Project time** | 1 hr | Try The Big Easy (V10) or Broken Wing (V10) for a serious session. Even if you do not send, working elite-level Teton granite is a memorable experience. |
| **Cool down** | 30 min | Easy problems, fun moves, enjoy the setting. |

**Skin management:** If bouldering is on Day 6 and you have been hiking for 5 days, your hands may be rough but your fingertips will not have the climbing-specific calluses built up. Bring a nail file or sandpaper to manage flappers. Do not climb to the point of splitting skin — you want to enjoy the rest of the trip.

## Packing List for Fly-In Bouldering

Everything you need that fits in luggage (pad is rented locally):

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Climbing shoes** | Bring your bouldering shoes. This is the one item you absolutely cannot rent well. Pack in carry-on to avoid lost-luggage disaster. |
| **Chalk bag + chalk** | Chalk is allowed in checked luggage (it is not restricted). Bring your preferred chalk — loose or block. A chalk bucket is better for bouldering sessions. |
| **Brush** | A boar's hair brush for cleaning holds. Small, light, essential. |
| **Finger tape** | For skin splits or tweaky tendons. Athletic tape works. |
| **Nail file / sandpaper** | For managing calluses and preventing flappers. |
| **Approach shoes or sturdy trail shoes** | You will already have hiking shoes — these work fine for boulder approaches. |
| **Phone with Mountain Project app** | Download the Teton Boulder Park and Gallatin Canyon areas for offline use before the trip. |
| **Guidebook** | If you can find a Teton bouldering guidebook online, order it before the trip. Otherwise, buy at Yostmark. |
| **Sunscreen** | You are at 6,500–7,500 ft elevation. Sun is strong. |
| **Bug spray** | For forested boulder areas. |
| **Warm layer** | Mountain mornings are cold. A puffy or fleece for belaying/resting between attempts. |
