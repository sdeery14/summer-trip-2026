## Mountain Biking Overview

He is learning mountain biking — beginner to intermediate. You're flying from BOS, so bikes need to be rented at the destination. The Greater Yellowstone Loop passes through three areas with legitimate mountain biking: Red Lodge (Beartooth day), Jackson Hole (Teton days), and Bozeman (if you fly in/out of BZN and have time on arrival or departure day). Note that mountain biking is **not permitted on trails inside Yellowstone or Grand Teton National Parks** — you need to ride in the gateway communities and surrounding national forest land.

**Key considerations for a learning rider:**
- Look for trails rated green (easy) to blue (intermediate) on MTB Project / Trailforks
- Avoid sustained steep descents, heavy rock gardens, or exposure until comfort builds
- Full-suspension rental bikes are more forgiving and worth the price premium
- Most shops offer half-day (4 hr) and full-day (8 hr) rentals
- Late August trail conditions are typically excellent — dry, packed dirt, minimal mud

## Red Lodge Area — Mountain Biking

Red Lodge sits at the eastern base of the Beartooth Highway and is a genuine Montana mountain town with a growing trail system. This is the most natural place to ride on your Beartooth day — you could ride in the morning and then drive the highway in the afternoon, or ride the evening before/after your Beartooth crossing.

### Trails

| Trail / System | Difficulty | Distance | Elevation Gain | What to Expect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **West Fork Trail System** | Green to Blue (Easy to Intermediate) | Multiple loops totaling 15+ miles | Gentle to moderate | The main local trail network. Mix of singletrack and doubletrack through open meadows and aspen groves along the West Fork of Rock Creek. Well-signed and maintained. The lower loops are very beginner-friendly; upper loops add moderate climbing and flow. Best option for a learning rider. |
| **Nichols Creek / Silver Run Trails** | Blue (Intermediate) | 6-10 mi depending on loop | 800-1,200 ft | Singletrack with moderate climbing through forest. More technical than West Fork but still manageable for an ambitious beginner. Good views of the Beartooths. |
| **Willow Creek Trail** | Green-Blue (Easy-Intermediate) | ~5 mi out-and-back | ~400 ft | Mellow singletrack following a creek drainage. Good warm-up ride or casual option. |
| **Lion's Camp / Coal Miners' Trail** | Green (Easy) | 2-3 mi | Minimal | In-town paved and gravel paths along Rock Creek. Good for a warm-up spin or if the mountain trails feel intimidating. Not singletrack — more of a bike path. |

**Best bet for a beginner-intermediate rider:** Start with the **West Fork Trail System** lower loops. If those feel good, add the upper loops or venture to Nichols Creek. You can easily fill 2-3 hours of riding.

**Searchable on:** AllTrails (search "Red Lodge mountain biking"), Trailforks, MTB Project. The West Fork system is well-documented.

## Jackson Hole Area — Mountain Biking

Jackson Hole has world-class mountain biking, though much of the famous terrain (Teton Pass, Jackson Hole Mountain Resort bike park) skews intermediate-to-expert. There are excellent beginner-friendly options too, especially on the in-town pathway system and the Cache Creek / Game Creek trail networks.

### Trails

| Trail / System | Difficulty | Distance | Elevation Gain | What to Expect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cache Creek to Game Creek Loop** | Blue (Intermediate) — with easier out-and-back options | Full loop: ~15 mi. Out-and-back on Cache Creek only: 6-10 mi. | Loop: ~1,500 ft. Cache Creek alone: 600-1,000 ft. | The most popular local ride. Cache Creek starts from the east edge of town and climbs gradually on well-maintained singletrack through aspen and pine forest. The full loop over to Game Creek adds a fun descent. **For a beginner: ride Cache Creek out-and-back** — the grade is steady and mellow for the first several miles. Turn around when it gets steeper. |
| **Teton Pass Trails (Old Pass Road, Phillips Canyon)** | Blue to Black (Intermediate to Expert) | Varies: 5-15+ mi | 1,500-3,000+ ft | Legendary riding with big descents and alpine views. Old Pass Road is the most accessible — a gravel road climb with a long descent. **Phillips Canyon and the upper Teton Pass trails are expert-level and not appropriate for a beginner.** Old Pass Road is OK for intermediate. |
| **Jackson Hole Mountain Resort Bike Park** | Green to Double Black | Lift-served, various runs | Lift-served descending | JHMR runs a full bike park in summer with lift-served downhill trails. They have green-rated trails that work for beginners, plus progression terrain. This is a great option because the lift does the climbing for you. Rental bikes (including full-face helmets and armor) available on-site. Half-day or full-day lift tickets. **[VERIFY 2026]** if the bike park is operating in late August — it typically runs late June through early September. |
| **Stilson Loop / Josie's Ridge** | Blue (Intermediate) | Stilson: ~4 mi loop. Josie's: ~6 mi. | Stilson: ~500 ft. Josie's: ~1,000 ft. | South of town in the Bridger-Teton NF. Fun, flowy singletrack. Stilson is a good beginner loop; Josie's Ridge adds more climbing and exposure but rewards with views. |
| **Snake River Levee Path** | Green (Easy) | Up to 15+ mi out-and-back | Flat | Paved/gravel path along the Snake River levee. Not mountain biking per se, but a beautiful flat ride with Teton views. Good for a casual spin or warm-up. |
| **Teton County Pathway System** | Green (Easy) | Various — 50+ miles of connected paths | Flat to gentle | Paved multi-use paths connecting Jackson to Wilson, Teton Village, and into Grand Teton NP (the multi-use pathway runs along Teton Park Road). Great for casual riding with world-class scenery. |

**Best bet for a beginner-intermediate rider:** Two strong options:
1. **Cache Creek out-and-back** — ride from town, go as far as you're comfortable, turn around. Free, accessible, scenic.
2. **Jackson Hole Mountain Resort Bike Park** — if the bike park is open, the green trails with lift assist are a blast for a learner. More structured, more fun factor, but costs more.

**How to book:** Most Jackson shops take reservations online or by phone. JHMR bike park tickets can be purchased on jacksonhole.com. For late August, availability is usually fine but reserving 1-2 weeks ahead is smart.

## Bozeman Area — Mountain Biking (If Time Allows)

If you fly into/out of BZN, Bozeman has an excellent mountain biking scene. You might have a few hours on arrival day or departure day. Bozeman's trail networks are some of the best in Montana.

### Trails

| Trail / System | Difficulty | Distance | Elevation Gain | What to Expect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gallagator Linear Trail → Peets Hill** | Green (Easy) | 2-3 mi | 200 ft | Right in town. Gentle gravel path and hilltop loop with views of the Bridger Range. Good 30-minute warm-up or arrival-day spin. Not true mountain biking but a nice way to stretch legs after flying. |
| **Drinking Horse Mountain** | Blue (Intermediate) | 4 mi loop | 900 ft | Just north of town. Popular local ride with a moderate climb and fun descent through open hillside. Great views. Doable in 1-1.5 hours. Good for a beginner pushing into intermediate. |
| **Leverich Canyon / Bozeman Creek Trail** | Blue (Intermediate) | 6-10 mi depending on turnaround | 800-1,500 ft | South of town. Forest singletrack along Bozeman Creek. Steady climbing, not too technical. Scenic ride through the Gallatin Range foothills. |
| **Sourdough Trail** | Blue-Black (Intermediate-Advanced) | 7-8 mi loop | 1,200 ft | South of town. One of the signature Bozeman rides. More technical than Drinking Horse — rocky sections and rooty descents. An ambitious beginner could handle it but may walk a few sections. |
| **Hyalite area trails** | Green to Black (full range) | Various | Various | Hyalite Canyon, 20 minutes south of Bozeman, has a mix of everything. The reservoir road itself is a good beginner ride. Surrounding trails range widely. |

**Best bet if you have 2-3 hours:** **Drinking Horse Mountain** — close to town, moderate, rewarding views, about 1-1.5 hours of riding.

The Greater Yellowstone region has outstanding river options. Three main rivers are directly on your loop, each with a distinct character:

| River | Location on Your Loop | Character | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Snake River** | Jackson Hole / Teton area | Both scenic flatwater sections (in GTNP) and exciting whitewater (Snake River Canyon south of Jackson) | Scenic floats with Teton views AND legit whitewater — two very different experiences on the same river |

## Snake River — Jackson Hole Area

The Snake River is the headline river option on your trip. It offers two completely different experiences:

### Scenic Float (Inside Grand Teton National Park)

**What it is:** A mellow, non-motorized float on the flatwater section of the Snake River inside Grand Teton National Park. No rapids — this is about scenery and wildlife. The Teton Range rises dramatically above the river. Bald eagles, osprey, moose, and occasionally bears are visible from the river.

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Class of rapids** | Flatwater (Class I — gentle current, no real rapids) |
| **Duration** | 10-13 miles, ~3 hours on the water. Typically a half-day trip with shuttle time. |
| **Difficulty** | None — suitable for all ages and abilities |
| **Season** | Late May through late September. Late August: excellent — water levels are lower and calmer, wildlife is very active. |
| **What's included** | Guide, raft, life jackets, shuttle to/from put-in and take-out. Some include binoculars. |
| **What to bring** | Layers (river is cool even on warm days), camera, sunscreen, water |

| **National Park Float Trips (Solitude Float Trips)** | 10-mile float, ~3 hrs. Well-reviewed, similar experience to Barker-Ewing. | ~$85-95/adult **[VERIFY 2026]** | https://www.solitudefloattrips.com/ — Book online. |

**Advance reservation needed?** Yes, recommended for late August. These trips are popular and run fixed departure times (typically early morning and late afternoon slots). Book at least 1-2 weeks ahead. Some outfitters sell out specific dates in peak season.

### Snake River Canyon Whitewater (South of Jackson)

**What it is:** Real whitewater on the Snake River through the canyon south of Jackson. This is a different section of the Snake — below the park, cutting through a steep-walled canyon with named rapids including Big Kahuna, Lunch Counter, Rope, and Cottonwood. Class II-III, with some bigger hits at higher water.

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Class of rapids** | Class II-III (fun, splashy, exciting but not dangerous for healthy adults). At higher water early in summer, some rapids push Class III+. By late August, water levels are moderate — a sweet spot for fun without intimidation. |
| **Duration** | 8 miles of whitewater. Half-day trips: ~3-3.5 hours (including shuttle). Full-day options available but less common on this stretch. |
| **Difficulty** | Moderate. No experience needed. You will get wet. Guides do the hard work; you paddle on command. |
| **Season** | Late May through September. Late August: great conditions — water is warm enough to be comfortable, rapids are fun but not pushy. |
| **What's included** | Guide, raft, paddles, life jackets (PFDs), helmets, wetsuits or splash gear (some outfitters), shuttle. |
| **What to bring** | Clothes you don't mind getting soaked. Secure strap for glasses. Water shoes or sandals with heel straps (no flip-flops). Sunscreen. Change of clothes for after. |
| **Minimum age** | Typically 6-8 years old depending on water levels. Not relevant for you, but indicates the accessibility. |

**Outfitters for Snake River Canyon Whitewater:**

| Outfitter | Trip Details | Price (approx) | Contact / Booking |
|---|---|---|---|

| **Mad River Boat Trips** | Half-day whitewater, Class II-III. One of the most popular outfitters. Known for high-energy, fun guides. Also offer scenic floats. | ~$80-100/adult **[VERIFY 2026]** | https://www.mad-river.com/ — Book online. |

### Snake River — Decision: Scenic Float vs. Whitewater

| | Scenic Float (In-Park) | Whitewater (Canyon, South of Jackson) |
|---|---|---|
| **Vibe** | Peaceful, contemplative, wildlife, Teton views | Adrenaline, teamwork, laughter, getting soaked |
| **Best time of day** | Early morning (wildlife, light on Tetons) or late afternoon (golden light) | Morning or midday |
| **Time commitment** | ~3 hrs on water + shuttle = half day | ~3 hrs on water + shuttle = half day |
| **Cost** | ~$80-95/person | ~$80-105/person |
| **Pairs well with** | A big hiking day (chill morning or afternoon float) | A flex day or chill day |
| **Our take** | A scenic float at sunrise with the Tetons is a "celebration" moment — special and memorable | Whitewater is pure fun and you both love it — a different kind of celebration |

**You could do both** if you have a flex day in the Tetons. Morning scenic float, afternoon whitewater (some outfitters offer combo packages). Or choose one based on the day's energy.

**Recommendation:** If you only do one, do the **whitewater**. You both love whitewater rafting, you can see the Tetons from plenty of trails, but the Snake River Canyon whitewater is a unique experience you can't replicate elsewhere on the trip. If you want that sunrise-on-the-river-with-Tetons moment, add the scenic float too — it's a completely different experience and worth the $80-95/person.

## Yellowstone River — Gardiner Area

The Yellowstone River runs through Paradise Valley north of Gardiner and through the town itself. It's the longest undammed river in the lower 48. Rafting here has a wilder, less commercial feel than the Snake — fewer outfitters, more remote canyon scenery, and excellent wildlife viewing from the river.

### Trip Options

| Trip Type | Section | Class | Duration | What to Expect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Yankee Jim Canyon Whitewater** | Gardiner area, just north of the park boundary | Class II-III | Half-day (~2-3 hrs on water) | The signature Yellowstone River raft trip. Named rapids through a narrow canyon. Fun, bouncy whitewater with beautiful canyon walls. Less crowded than Snake River trips. |
| **Paradise Valley Scenic Float** | North of Gardiner through Paradise Valley | Class I-II (gentle) | Half-day (~3 hrs) | Mellow float with stunning views of the Absaroka Range. Wildlife spotting — osprey, eagles, deer, occasionally bears on the banks. Very relaxing. |
| **Full-Day Yellowstone River** | Extended section combining canyon + valley | Class I-III | Full day (5-7 hrs on water with lunch) | Some outfitters offer full-day trips that combine the whitewater canyon section with a longer scenic stretch. Includes riverside lunch. |

## Gallatin River — Big Sky / Bozeman Area

The Gallatin River runs through Gallatin Canyon between Bozeman and West Yellowstone along US-191. If your route takes you through Gallatin Canyon (e.g., entering Yellowstone via the west entrance from Bozeman, or coming from the bouldering at Gallatin Canyon), this river is right there.

### Trip Options

|---|---|---|---|---|

The "Mad Mile" section is the most intense — a continuous stretch of Class III-IV with names like House Rock and Hilarity Hole. Exciting and more intense than the Yellowstone River trips. |
| **Mellow Upper Gallatin Float** | Above the canyon, near Big Sky | Class I-II | Half-day | Calmer water above the canyon stretch. Scenic, suitable for families. |

- If you enter Yellowstone via the **west entrance** from Bozeman, US-191 runs right through Gallatin Canyon. You could raft in the morning and enter the park in the afternoon.
- If you enter via the **north entrance** (Gardiner), the Gallatin is out of your way and the Yellowstone River is the better option.
- The Gallatin Canyon also has bouldering — you could combine a bouldering session for her with a rafting trip for both in the same area.

**Advance reservation needed?** Recommended, especially for morning trips. Big Sky/Gallatin area is popular in summer. Book 1 week ahead.

## Other Rivers on the Route

### Shoshone River — Cody, WY

If your route takes you through Cody (east of Yellowstone, possible if you're doing a loop via the east entrance), the Shoshone River has good whitewater.

The Hoback joins the Snake River south of Jackson and has some whitewater sections, but most Jackson-area outfitters incorporate Hoback sections into their Snake River Canyon trips. You don't need to seek this out separately.

## Lake Paddling — Canoeing and Kayaking

The Greater Yellowstone area has beautiful alpine lakes where you can rent canoes, kayaks, or stand-up paddleboards. This is a great activity for a rest day, a chill morning, or an afternoon when you've already hiked.

### String Lake (Grand Teton National Park)

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | A shallow, warm (by mountain standards) lake at the base of the Tetons. Crystal clear water, stunning mountain backdrop. Connected to Jenny Lake and Leigh Lake. |
| **Paddling** | Flat water, no current. Canoe or kayak String Lake, or portage into Leigh Lake for a longer paddle. Leigh Lake is larger, deeper, and less crowded. |
| **Rentals** | **No on-site rentals at String Lake.** You need to bring your own or rent in Jackson/Moose. Some outfitters deliver to the lake. |
| **Rental sources** | **Dornan's Adventure Sports** (Moose Junction, inside GTNP) — canoe and kayak rentals. **Rendezvous River Sports** (Jackson) — kayak rentals and tours. **Adventure Sports** at Signal Mountain Lodge — canoe and kayak rentals at the lodge. |
| **Pricing** | Canoe/kayak rental: ~$25-40/hour or ~$60-90/full day. **[VERIFY 2026]** |
| **Why it's special** | Paddling on String Lake with the Tetons rising directly above you is a bucket-list moment. Warm enough to swim in late August. Very popular — go early morning for calm water and solitude. |

### Jenny Lake (Grand Teton National Park)

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | The iconic Teton lake. Larger, deeper, and colder than String Lake. Dramatic mountain scenery. |
| **Paddling** | Canoe or kayak on the lake. You can paddle across to the west shore and access the Cascade Canyon trail (a classic approach — paddle instead of hiking the flat lakeshore section). |
| **Rentals** | **Jenny Lake Boating** rents canoes and kayaks at the Jenny Lake shore. This is the most convenient option — walk up, rent, paddle. |
| **Pricing** | Canoe: ~$30/hour. Kayak (single): ~$25/hour. Kayak (tandem): ~$35/hour. **[VERIFY 2026]** |
| **Why it's special** | Paddling Jenny Lake is the most scenic paddle in the area. Arriving at the Cascade Canyon trailhead by canoe instead of the crowded shuttle boat is a much cooler experience. |
| **Tips** | Go early (before 9 AM) for calm water and fewer boats. Afternoon winds can pick up and make paddling back harder. Jenny Lake Boating also runs shuttle boats across the lake if you'd rather not paddle. |

### Yellowstone Lake (Yellowstone National Park)

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | The largest high-elevation lake in North America. 136 square miles. Cold, deep, and wild. |
| **Paddling** | Possible but more serious than the Teton lakes. The lake is large enough to generate dangerous waves and weather can change rapidly. Hypothermia risk is real — water temperature rarely exceeds 45-50°F even in August. |
| **Rentals** | **Bridge Bay Marina** rents small motorboats (not canoes/kayaks as of recent years — **[VERIFY 2026]**). For canoe/kayak use, you'd need to rent in a gateway town and transport. |
| **Recommended?** | **Not the best paddling option** for your trip. The cold water, wind risk, and lack of convenient rentals make the Teton lakes far superior for a casual paddle. If you're experienced cold-water paddlers with your own gear, specific bays (West Thumb, Sedge Bay) can be paddled on calm mornings. But String Lake and Jenny Lake are better choices in every way for a fun, safe paddle experience. |

### Lewis Lake and Shoshone Lake (Yellowstone National Park)

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | Lewis Lake is accessible by road; Shoshone Lake (the largest backcountry lake in the lower 48) is reachable via a canoe/portage route from Lewis Lake. |
| **Paddling** | The Lewis Lake → Lewis River Channel → Shoshone Lake canoe route is a classic backcountry paddling trip. Requires a backcountry permit for overnight. Day-paddling Lewis Lake is possible. |
| **Recommended?** | Only if you're specifically interested in a backcountry canoe-camping trip on Shoshone Lake. This is a multi-day commitment and requires planning, permits, and cold-water preparedness. Not a casual activity — but it's an incredible experience if it calls to you. |

### Lake Paddling Recommendation

**Do this:** Rent canoes or kayaks at **Jenny Lake** (from Jenny Lake Boating) or at **String Lake** (from Dornan's in Moose and bring to String Lake, or rent at Signal Mountain Lodge). For the most iconic experience, **paddle across Jenny Lake** to the Cascade Canyon trailhead on a calm morning — it combines a scenic paddle with the start of one of the best hikes in the Tetons. String Lake is warmer, easier, and great for a relaxed afternoon paddle with swimming.

**Skip this:** Yellowstone Lake paddling (too cold, too risky, not enough fun for the effort) unless you're experienced cold-water paddlers looking for adventure.

### If You Only Do One Water Activity

**Do the Snake River Canyon whitewater.** You both love whitewater, it's an exciting experience unique to this area, and it pairs perfectly with a Teton flex day. Book with Mad River, Barker-Ewing, or Dave Hansen — all are excellent.

### If You Want Two Water Activities

**Snake River whitewater + Jenny Lake kayak.** The whitewater gives you adrenaline and the kayak gives you serenity. Paddle Jenny Lake early morning, do the whitewater in the afternoon (or on different days).

### If You Want to Ease into the Trip on Day 0

**Yellowstone River scenic float from Gardiner.** Land in Bozeman, drive to Gardiner, float the river as the sun gets low. Wildlife, mountain views, zero effort. A perfect way to decompress from travel and transition into trip mode.
