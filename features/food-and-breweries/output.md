## How to Use This Document

You are car camping and cooking most meals. Eating out is a **treat**, not the default — and that's what makes it special. This doc covers:

- **Where to eat when you decide to treat yourselves** (after a big hike day, on a rest evening, arrival/departure)
- **Where to stock up on groceries** at each stage of the loop
- **What's available in the parks** for a quick bite or an ice cream when you don't want to drive to town

Budget context: Most meals are camp-cooked from Walmart groceries. Plan for **3-5 meals out** over the week. At the price points in these towns, that's roughly $25-50 per meal for two (less at quick eats, more at Jackson celebration dinner). Total eating-out budget for the trip: **$150-250** is comfortable; $300+ if you go hard on the Jackson celebration.

## 1. Bozeman, MT — Arrival & Stock-Up

**When you're here:** Day 0 — arrive at BZN, pick up rental car, stock up on groceries, maybe grab dinner before driving to Gardiner or camping near town.

**The vibe:** Bozeman is a legit mountain college town (Montana State University). It has a real food and beer scene — not touristy, not pretentious, just good. Main Street and the surrounding blocks have more breweries per capita than almost anywhere in Montana. If your flight arrives with time to spare, this is worth an evening.

## 2. Gardiner, MT — North Entrance

**When you're here:** Day 0 evening (if you drive from BZN after landing) and/or Day 1 morning, possibly again when exiting through the north after Lamar Valley.

**The vibe:** Gardiner is a tiny gateway town (population ~900) at the original north entrance to Yellowstone. It's not fancy and it's not trying to be. One main drag (Park Street / Scott Street), a handful of restaurants, and the iconic Roosevelt Arch. Options are limited but there are a few spots worth knowing.

## 3. Red Lodge, MT — Beartooth Base

**When you're here:** Day 2 evening after driving the Beartooth Highway. This is your brewery and dinner stop on the east side of the loop.

**The vibe:** Red Lodge is a genuine Montana mountain town, not a tourist trap. Population ~2,300, historic Main Street, surrounded by the Beartooth Range. It's unpretentious, walkable, and has a surprising food and beer scene for its size. Locals are ranchers, ski bums (Red Lodge Mountain ski area), and outdoor people. The Main Street buildings are mostly historic brick — it feels real and lived-in. This town punches well above its weight for food and drink.

## 4. West Yellowstone, MT — If Passing Through

**When you're here:** Possibly on Day 3 or 4 if your route takes you through the west entrance. On the recommended clockwise loop (north entrance in, south out to Tetons), you may not pass through West Yellowstone at all. But if you do — or if you want to make a grocery run — here's what to know.

**The vibe:** West Yellowstone is the most commercial gateway town. It exists to serve tourists. Main Street (Canyon Street / Yellowstone Ave) is wall-to-wall souvenir shops, outfitters, and restaurants. It's not charming like Red Lodge or gritty like Gardiner — it's a tourist town. But it has the most services, including the only Walmart-adjacent option on the Montana side of the loop.

## 5. Jackson, WY — Celebration Town

**When you're here:** Days 5-7 on the recommended itinerary. This is your Teton base, your celebration dinner, your end-of-trip reward. Jackson has the best food and drink scene on the entire loop — and the highest prices. Plan your biggest "treat yourself" meal here.

**The vibe:** Jackson is a wealthy resort town. The town square has elk antler arches, the boardwalks have western charm, and the side streets have $200 ski jackets and $15 cocktails. It's touristy, yes, but the food scene is genuinely excellent — propped up by the resort economy. The dark-wood-and-metal aesthetic you love is well-represented here. Jackson has leaned hard into the rustic-industrial look — exposed timber, blackened steel, Edison bulbs. Many restaurants and bars hit this note.

**Price reality:** Jackson is expensive. A burger and two beers will run $30-45 for two. A nice dinner with drinks: $60-100. This is where you spend the "treat" budget, not where you eat three meals a day. Eat one or two great meals here and cook the rest at camp.

### Breweries

|---|---|---|---|
| **Snake River Brewing** | The crown jewel. Winner of multiple Great American Beer Festival medals and a James Beard Award (America's outstanding bar). | **Roadhouse Brewing** | Excellent small-batch brewery with a focus on experimental and seasonal beers. The tap list rotates frequently and leans creative (sours, barrel-aged, saisons). If you're a beer nerd, this might be your favorite brewery on the trip. | S Hwy 89 area, south of town |
| **Melvin Brewing** | Originally from Alpine, WY (30 min south of Jackson). Won multiple GABF awards for their IPAs — 2x4 DIPA is famous. They've expanded distribution but check for a taproom presence in Jackson. | Irreverent, punk-rock brewery branding. **Recommendation:** **Snake River Brewing** is the must-do. Go on a night after a big hike day. Expect to wait 20-45 minutes for a table on a summer evening — put your name in, walk around the town square, come back. The wait is worth it. If you want a second brewery night, **Roadhouse** is excellent and less crowded.

### Banh Mi Situation

**Honest answer:** Jackson does not have a dedicated banh mi shop. The Vietnamese food scene in western Wyoming is essentially nonexistent. Your best bets for Asian food are:

- **Thai Me Up** — Thai, not Vietnamese, but the closest to the flavor profile you're after
- **Lotus Organic Cafe** — Pan-Asian, may have Vietnamese-inspired items
- **Bozeman** had slightly more options but also no standout banh mi shop

Save the banh mi craving for when you get home to the Boston area, where you have some of the best banh mi in the country. Out here, lean into what the region does well: burgers, steaks, pizza, and craft beer.

## 6. Driggs, ID — West-Side Tetons

**When you're here:** Day 6 (flex day) if you drive over Teton Pass for bouldering at Teton Boulder Park, or as an alternative base to Jackson.

**The vibe:** Driggs is the "locals' side" of the Tetons. Teton Valley on the Idaho side is dramatically more affordable and less touristy than Jackson Hole. Driggs is a small town (population ~2,000) with a growing food scene. It's where Jackson locals go when they don't want to deal with Jackson prices. The town has a laid-back, slightly hippie-meets-rancher feel. Think: organic grocery store next to a feed store.

## In-Park Food Options

You're cooking at camp most of the time, but sometimes you want a quick meal, an ice cream, or a coffee without driving to a gateway town. Here's what's available inside Yellowstone and Grand Teton.

### Yellowstone National Park

Yellowstone's in-park food is operated by **Yellowstone National Park Lodges** (Xanterra). It's institutional food at elevated prices — think national park cafeteria quality. Fine for a quick meal, not a destination.

| Location | What's There | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Mammoth Hot Springs Hotel Dining Room** | Sit-down restaurant. Breakfast, lunch, dinner. | Decent for a sit-down meal. Not cheap ($15-25 per entree) but not horrible. The setting near the hot springs is nice. |
| **Mammoth General Store** | Snacks, ice cream, sandwiches, groceries, ice, firewood. | Good for ice cream after Mammoth Hot Springs. |

## Grocery Strategy

### The Big Stock-Up: Bozeman

**Do your main grocery run in Bozeman immediately after landing.** This is the largest town on the route with the best prices and selection.

| Store | Address Area | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Walmart Supercenter** | N 7th Ave, Bozeman | The big stock-up. Everything you need for a week of car camping: cooler food, canned goods, snacks, breakfast supplies, sandwich stuff, camp cooking staples, beer/wine (Montana Walmart sells beer — check for wine), ice, charcoal/propane, paper goods. Cheapest option by far. |
| **Costco** | Mandeville Ln area, Bozeman | If you have a membership and want bulk snacks, a case of beer, or a rotisserie chicken for your first camp dinner. |
| **Town & Country Foods** / **Albertsons** | Various Bozeman locations | Standard grocery, slightly higher prices than Walmart. Better produce and meat selection. Good if you want higher-quality camp food. |
| **Community Food Co-op** | W Main St, Bozeman | Premium prices but excellent quality. Good for trail mix, jerky, cheese, nice bread, and specialty items that Walmart doesn't carry well. |

**What to buy in Bozeman for the whole trip:**
- Ice (buy a fresh bag and top off as needed along the route)
- Cooler food: deli meat, cheese, eggs, bacon, tortillas, butter
- Staples: oatmeal, rice/pasta, canned beans, cooking oil, spices, coffee
- Snacks: trail mix, bars, jerky, fruit, chips, crackers
- Drinks: beer (a case or variety pack for camp), water, electrolyte mix
- Camp supplies: paper towels, trash bags, dish soap, aluminum foil, Ziplocs
- Firewood and fire starters (or buy these at campground stores — sometimes required to buy local wood to prevent invasive species)

**Important note on firewood:** Many areas in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem restrict transporting firewood to prevent the spread of invasive insects. Buy firewood locally (at or near each campground) rather than hauling it from Bozeman. Most campground hosts, general stores, and gas stations near the parks sell bundles for $5-8.

### Mid-Route Top-Offs

| Location | When | What to Get |
|---|---|---|
| **Red Lodge — Beartooth Market (IGA)** | Day 2 evening | Ice, firewood, any fresh food that's running low. Beer restock if needed. |
| **Yellowstone General Stores** (Mammoth, Canyon, Bridge Bay, etc.) | Days 1-4 | Ice, firewood, snacks, ice cream, basic supplies. Prices are 50-100% above normal — buy only what you need. Firewood is usually available and sometimes required to be park-sourced. |
| **Dornan's General Store** (Moose Junction, Tetons) | Days 5-6 | Good selection for a park store. Ice, firewood, beer, wine, snacks, some produce and deli items. Prices are elevated but selection is better than most in-park stores. |
| **Jackson — Albertsons or Smith's** | Days 5-7 | If you need a real restock for the Teton portion. This is your best grocery option after Bozeman, though pricier. |
| **Driggs — Broulim's** | Day 6 (bouldering day) | Good standard grocery at better prices than Jackson. If you're driving to Driggs anyway, this is a smart grocery stop. |

### Ice Strategy

Your cooler is your lifeline for a week of car camping in summer. Ice management matters.

- **Buy two bags of ice in Bozeman** on Day 0.
- **Top off every 1-2 days.** Every general store, gas station, and campground store sells ice ($3-5/bag).
- **Pre-chill your cooler** if possible — put ice in it the night before you load food.
- **Freeze water bottles** to use as supplementary ice packs that become drinking water as they melt.
- Ice availability in the parks is reliable at all general stores. You will never be far from ice.
