## 1. Beartooth Highway (US-212)

### The Signature Drive

Charles Kuralt called this "the most beautiful drive in America," and he was not exaggerating. The Beartooth Highway climbs from Red Lodge, Montana (5,555 ft) to the Beartooth Plateau summit at 10,947 ft, then descends through alpine tundra, past dozens of glacial lakes, and down to Cooke City at Yellowstone's northeast entrance. This is not just a connecting road between two points — this is a destination in itself.

### Quick Facts

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | US-212 from Red Lodge, MT to Cooke City, MT (Yellowstone NE entrance) |
| **Distance** | 68 miles |
| **Drive time without stops** | 2 hours |
| **Realistic time with stops** | 3.5 to 5 hours (you will stop constantly) |
| **Summit elevation** | 10,947 ft (Beartooth Pass) |
| **Elevation gain from Red Lodge** | ~5,400 ft |
| **Seasonal closure** | Typically opens late May (Memorial Day target), closes mid-October |
| **2026 status** | **[VERIFY 2026]** Check Montana DOT: https://www.511mt.net/ |
| **Road surface** | Fully paved, two lanes, no guardrails in many sections |
| **RV / trailer limits** | No formal ban, but large RVs (35+ ft) and trailers are strongly discouraged due to tight switchbacks and steep grades. Not recommended for nervous drivers. |

### Best Direction to Drive

**East to West (Red Lodge to Cooke City) is the recommended direction.** Here is why:

| Direction | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **East to West (Red Lodge → Cooke City)** | The dramatic reveal: you climb through switchbacks and the landscape transforms from pine forest to alpine tundra. The summit hits you with a panorama you have earned. Afternoon sun lights the western descent beautifully. You end at Cooke City, positioned for Lamar Valley at dawn the next morning. | Morning sun can be in your eyes if you start early. Switchbacks on the east side are the steepest part. |
| **West to East (Cooke City → Red Lodge)** | Morning light illuminates the eastern face of the Beartooths. You descend into Red Lodge, positioned for dinner and a brewery. | The big views come first and then gradually diminish as you descend. Less dramatic buildup. The switchback descent on the east side is steeper (harder on brakes, more nerve-wracking). |

**Recommendation:** If your itinerary flows naturally east-to-west (Red Lodge → Cooke City → Yellowstone NE entrance), take it that way. The drama of climbing up to the summit is worth it. If you are coming from inside Yellowstone (Lamar Valley) heading to Red Lodge for the evening, driving west-to-east still delivers stunning scenery — just in reverse order.

### Best Time of Day

- **Morning (7-10 AM):** Best light for photography on the eastern switchbacks. Fewer cars. Cool temperatures at summit (bring layers regardless). Less wind typically.
- **Midday (11 AM - 2 PM):** Harsh overhead light for photos, but the views are still jaw-dropping. Summit can be warm-ish in direct sun (50s-60s) even when air temp is cold.
- **Late afternoon (3-6 PM):** Golden light on the western descent. Fewer cars than midday. Watch for afternoon thunderstorms at summit — they build fast above 10,000 ft and are dangerous (lightning on exposed tundra with nowhere to hide).
- **Recommendation:** Start from Red Lodge by 8-9 AM. This gives you morning light on the climb, midday at the summit with time to hike, and early afternoon for the descent. You arrive in Cooke City by early-mid afternoon.

### What to Bring

The summit is above 10,900 ft and conditions can be dramatically different from the valleys:

- **Layers:** It can be 80F in Red Lodge and 40F with 30 mph wind at the summit. Bring a fleece or puffy jacket, wind shell, and long pants even in August.
- **Sun protection:** UV is intense at 11,000 ft. Sunscreen, sunglasses, hat.
- **Water and snacks:** Limited services between Red Lodge and Cooke City (one seasonal store at the summit).
- **Full gas tank:** Fill up in Red Lodge. Next reliable gas is Cooke City, 68 miles away.
- **Camera with charged battery:** You will take hundreds of photos.
- **Binoculars:** For scanning alpine meadows for mountain goats and scanning the plateau.

### Mile-by-Mile Highlights (East to West, Red Lodge to Cooke City)

#### Mile 0-5: Red Lodge to the Base
- **Mile 0 — Red Lodge (5,555 ft):** Fill your gas tank. Last full-service town until Cooke City. Grab coffee at Coffee Factory Roasters or breakfast at Cafe Regis before the drive.
- **Mile 1-5:** Gentle climb through Rock Creek Valley. Pine forest, creek alongside the road. Setting the stage.

#### Mile 5-15: The Switchbacks Begin
- **Mile 5-10 — Rock Creek Vista Point:** The first major pullout. A short paved path leads to overlooks of the Rock Creek glacial valley far below. Worth a 10-minute stop. Interpretive signs explain the geology.
- **Mile 10-15 — The switchbacks:** The road begins its dramatic ascent via a series of tight switchbacks carved into the canyon wall. Each turn reveals a wider view. You gain about 2,000 ft in these miles. The treeline starts to thin.

#### Mile 15-25: Above Treeline
- **Mile 15-18:** You break above treeline. The landscape transforms: suddenly you are on open alpine tundra with 360-degree views. Patches of snow linger into August. This is where the drive becomes something else entirely.
- **Mile 20 — Gardner Lake and surrounding alpine lakes:** Several pullouts access views of small glacial tarns. These lakes are bright turquoise from glacial flour. Short scrambles (5-15 minutes) from pullouts reach lakeshores.
- **Mile 20-25 — Alpine meadows:** In late July through mid-August, wildflowers carpet the tundra. Look for alpine forget-me-nots, Indian paintbrush, and fields of yellow arnica.

#### Mile 25-35: The Summit Zone
- **Mile 25-30 — Approach to Beartooth Pass:** The road traverses the open plateau. You can see for miles in every direction. Snow-capped peaks ring the horizon. This section feels like driving on the roof of the world.
- **Mile 30 — Beartooth Pass Summit (10,947 ft):** The high point. A pullout and sign mark the summit. Stop here — this is it. Walk around on the tundra (stay on durable surfaces). On a clear day, you can see the Absaroka Range, the Beartooth Plateau stretching to the horizon, and dozens of alpine lakes below. Wind is often fierce.
  - **Short hike from summit:** Walk south from the summit parking area along the ridge for 0.5-1 mile. No formal trail, just open tundra. The views get even better and you escape any other tourists at the pullout.
  - **Mountain goats:** Scan the rocky areas near the summit. Mountain goats frequent this zone.

#### Mile 35-45: The Plateau and High Lakes
- **Mile 33-35 — Top of the World Store:** A seasonal general store (open approximately June through mid-September, hours vary). Snacks, souvenirs, and restrooms. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check if open and hours.
- **Mile 35-40 — Beartooth Lake and Island Lake:** Two beautiful alpine lakes visible from the road. Beartooth Lake has a USFS campground (Beartooth Lake Campground — a potential overnight option with a gorgeous setting at ~9,000 ft). Island Lake has a pullout with a short walk to the shore. Both are excellent for photography, especially with the Beartooth Butte reflected in the water.
  - **Short hike: Island Lake to Beauty Lake and beyond.** From the Island Lake trailhead (marked pullout), a trail leads 1.5 miles to Beauty Lake, then continues to several more alpine lakes. You can do an easy 3-mile out-and-back or extend to a 6+ mile loop through the high lakes. Minimal crowds. This is the best short-hike-from-the-highway option.
  - **Short hike: Beartooth High Lakes Trail.** From the Beartooth Lake area, trails connect a chain of alpine lakes across open tundra. Easy to moderate terrain, spectacular scenery.
- **Mile 40-42 — Clay Butte Lookout turnoff:** A 3-mile side road (gravel, passable for cars in good conditions) leads to Clay Butte Fire Lookout at 9,811 ft. The lookout offers 360-degree views including Granite Peak (Montana's highest at 12,799 ft), the Absaroka Range, and on clear days, the Tetons far to the south. This is one of the best viewpoints in the region and most people skip it because they do not know about the turnoff. **Strongly recommended.**
  - The lookout is staffed seasonally and you can usually go up into the tower. Check locally for 2026 status.

#### Mile 45-55: The Western Descent
- **Mile 45-50:** The road begins descending from the plateau. You re-enter scattered trees (krummholz — wind-stunted whitebark pine). Views of the Absaroka Range open up to the south and west.
- **Mile 47-48 — Pilot and Index Peaks viewpoint:** Pullout with views of two dramatic rock spires (Pilot Peak, 11,699 ft, and Index Peak, 11,313 ft) to the south. These are part of the Absaroka Range and are prominent landmarks visible for many miles. Good photo opportunity.
- **Mile 50-55 — Descending toward Cooke City:** The road drops through subalpine forest. Creeks and meadows appear. The character shifts from dramatic alpine to lush mountain valley.

#### Mile 55-68: Cooke City and the Gateway
- **Mile 55-60 — Colter Pass (8,066 ft):** A gentle pass between the Beartooths and Absarokas. Historically a route used by fur trappers.
- **Mile 60-65 — Silver Gate:** A tiny community (population ~20) just east of Cooke City. A few lodges and the Log Cabin Cafe (good food in a rustic setting). Feels frozen in time.
- **Mile 68 — Cooke City (7,600 ft):** End of the Beartooth Highway. Small frontier town at Yellowstone's northeast entrance. Gas station (fill up here), a few restaurants, general stores. The Bearclaw Bakery is a local favorite for pastries. The Miner's Saloon has cold beer and pub food.
  - From Cooke City, it is 4 miles west to the Yellowstone NE entrance station and then 29 miles to Tower Junction through the Lamar Valley.

### Road Conditions and Hazards

- **Summer snowstorms:** Even in August, snow squalls can hit the summit. These are usually brief (30 minutes to a few hours) but can reduce visibility to near zero. If a storm rolls in, wait it out at a pullout or the Top of the World Store. Montana DOT will close the road if conditions warrant.
- **Lightning:** The summit is completely exposed. If you are hiking on the plateau and see thunderstorms building, get back to your car immediately. Afternoon storms are most common.
- **Wildlife on road:** Watch for mountain goats, marmots, and occasionally bears crossing the road, especially in the lower sections.
- **No cell service:** Expect zero cell coverage for most of the highway. Download offline maps before you start.
- **Construction:** Road maintenance and repaving projects are common. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check Montana DOT (511mt.net) before driving.

## 2. Lamar Valley Road (Northeast Entrance Road)

### Overview

The Lamar Valley is the Serengeti of North America. This stretch of road from Tower Junction to the Northeast Entrance (and continuing to Cooke City) passes through broad, open valleys where Yellowstone's mega-fauna congregates — wolves, grizzlies, bison herds, elk, pronghorn, and more. The drive itself is beautiful, but the wildlife is the reason you are here.

### Quick Facts

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | Tower Junction to Yellowstone NE Entrance (continuing to Cooke City) |
| **Distance** | 29 miles (Tower Junction to NE entrance); 33 miles to Cooke City |
| **Drive time without stops** | 45 minutes to NE entrance |
| **Realistic time with stops** | 2 to 4+ hours (you will stop for every animal sighting) |
| **Elevation** | 6,200-6,600 ft (valley floor) |
| **Seasonal closure** | Open year-round from Tower Junction to Cooke City (one of the few Yellowstone roads that stays open in winter for snowmobiles) |
| **Road surface** | Paved, two lanes |
| **RV / trailer limits** | No restrictions, but pullouts fill quickly during wildlife jams |

### Best Times for Wildlife Viewing

| Time | What You Will See | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Dawn (5:30-8:00 AM)** | Wolves, grizzly bears, coyotes actively hunting. Bison herds moving. Elk grazing in meadows. Best light for photography. | **This is THE time.** Set your alarm. Drive out from camp before sunrise. The valley is magical at dawn — mist rising, animals everywhere, almost no cars for the first hour. |
| **Morning (8:00-10:00 AM)** | Wildlife still active but starting to bed down. Wolf packs may still be visible. The "wolf watchers" with spotting scopes will be set up at known locations — approach them politely and they will often let you look through their scopes. | Still excellent. The wolf-watching community is incredibly generous with their knowledge and equipment. |
| **Midday (10:00 AM - 4:00 PM)** | Most predators resting. Bison visible but less active. Fewer dramatic sightings. Good time for hiking instead. | Drive through but do not expect the dawn experience. |
| **Evening (5:00-8:00 PM)** | Animals active again. Wolves often hunt at dusk. Bears foraging. Beautiful golden light on the valley. | Almost as good as dawn. The light in the valley at sunset is extraordinary. Fewer people than morning. |

### Where to Pull Over and What to Look For

Driving east from Tower Junction toward the NE entrance:

#### Mile 0-5: Tower Junction to Tower Fall
- **Tower Junction:** Starting point. The road splits here — south to Canyon Village, east to Lamar Valley. Take the east fork.
- **Note:** The road from Tower Junction toward Canyon (the Tower-Canyon segment) may be under construction. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check NPS road status: https://www.nps.gov/yell/planyourvisit/roads.htm

#### Mile 5-10: Entering the Lamar
- **Floating Island Lake (Mile ~6):** Small lake on the south side of the road. Trumpeter swans sometimes nest here. Moose are occasionally spotted in the willows. Small pullout.
- **Elk Creek confluence (~Mile 8):** Where Elk Creek meets the Yellowstone River. Scan the hillsides for bears digging for roots or ground squirrels, especially in early morning.

#### Mile 10-18: The Heart of Lamar Valley
This is the core wildlife zone.

- **Slough Creek turnoff (Mile ~10):** A dirt road turns north to Slough Creek Campground (2 miles). The campground itself is one of the most coveted in Yellowstone — famous for its setting on Slough Creek with excellent fishing and wildlife viewing. Even if not camping here, the pullout at the turnoff is a good wildlife-scanning spot.
  - **Slough Creek drainage:** This area is one of the most reliable spots in Yellowstone for wolf sightings. Wolves den in the hillsides above Slough Creek. The wolf-watching community sets up spotting scopes here regularly.
- **Lamar Valley proper (Miles 12-18):** The road traverses wide-open valley floor with sagebrush flats and the Lamar River meandering through. This is where the big bison herds gather — hundreds to thousands of animals.
  - **Multiple pullouts on both sides of the road.** Every pullout is a potential wildlife-viewing spot. Stop when you see other cars stopped — there is almost always a reason.
  - **Bison jams:** Bison regularly walk on and across the road. Do not approach them (they are fast and dangerous — 2,000 lbs running at 35 mph). Stay in your car. Wait patiently. This is part of the Lamar experience.
  - **Wolf observation points:** The Druid Peak wolf pack (now succeeded by other packs — the Junction Butte pack and others are active in this area as of recent years) is often visible from several pullouts along this stretch. Look for clusters of people with large spotting scopes — these are the dedicated wolf watchers who track the packs daily.
  - **Grizzly bears:** Scan the hillsides with binoculars, particularly the grassy slopes above the valley floor. Bears are often visible at a distance, digging or grazing. In late August/early September, they are in hyperphagia (pre-hibernation feeding frenzy) and are especially active.

#### Mile 18-25: Upper Lamar and Soda Butte
- **Soda Butte Creek area (Mile ~20):** The valley narrows slightly. Soda Butte, a now-extinct travertine hot spring cone, sits right next to the road. Small pullout. Historically a landmark for travelers since the 1800s.
- **Round Prairie (Mile ~22):** An open meadow area. Good for elk and sometimes moose in the willows along the creek.
- **Pebble Creek area (Mile ~23):** Pebble Creek Campground turnoff. Another excellent wildlife area. The Pebble Creek trail is a good day hike destination (see hiking-trails feature).
- **Barronette Peak viewpoint:** Views of this dramatic peak (10,404 ft) to the north. Good photo opportunity.

#### Mile 25-29: Approach to NE Entrance
- **Icebox Canyon:** The road passes through a narrow, shaded canyon. Cooler temperatures, different character than the open valley.
- **Northeast Entrance Station (Mile 29):** Park boundary. From here, it is 4 miles to Cooke City.

### Tips for Lamar Valley
- **Bring binoculars.** Essential. You will not see wolves or distant bears with the naked eye. 8x42 or 10x42 binoculars are ideal.
- **Bring a spotting scope if you have one.** For wolf watching, 20-60x magnification makes the difference between "I see a dot" and "I can see the wolf's expression."
- **Be respectful of the wolf-watching community.** They are out there every single day, tracking pack movements. They are generally happy to share their scopes and knowledge if you are polite and interested. Do not walk in front of their scopes or make loud noises.
- **Stay at least 100 yards from wolves and bears, 25 yards from bison and elk.** These are Yellowstone regulations and they are serious. Use your zoom lens, not your feet.
- **Traffic etiquette:** When you stop for wildlife, pull fully off the road. Do not block traffic. Do not stop in the middle of the road even if there is a bear 50 feet away (this happens constantly and causes dangerous situations).
- **No cell service** through most of the valley.

### Photo Opportunities

| Subject | Best Time | Best Location |
|---|---|---|
| **Wolves** | Dawn, first light | Slough Creek pullouts, Lamar Valley pullouts Miles 12-18 |
| **Bison herds with valley backdrop** | Dawn or dusk (golden light) | Miles 14-18, any pullout with a wide valley view |
| **Grizzly bears** | Dawn, dusk | Scan hillsides from any pullout Miles 10-22 |
| **Lamar River with mountains** | Morning (front-lit) or evening (back-lit drama) | Miles 14-16, pullouts on south side of road |
| **Sunrise over the valley** | Pre-dawn to 30 min after sunrise | Any elevated pullout facing east; the Slough Creek area is excellent |

## 3. Grand Loop Road (Yellowstone Interior)

### Overview

The Grand Loop Road is a figure-eight circuit totaling approximately 142 miles that connects all of Yellowstone's major attractions. You will inevitably drive portions of it. Rather than treating it as one long drive, think of it as distinct segments, each with a different character.

### The Figure-Eight at a Glance

```
                    MAMMOTH
                   /        \
                  /          \
           NORRIS ---- TOWER JUNCTION
              |    \       /    |
              |     CANYON      |
              |    /       \    |
           MADISON          FISHING BRIDGE
              |    \       /    |
              |     OLD FAITHFUL |
              |    /       \    |
           WEST           YELLOWSTONE
           ENTRANCE         LAKE
                  \          /
                   \        /
                  GRANT VILLAGE
```

### Segment-by-Segment Guide

---

#### Segment A: Mammoth Hot Springs to Tower Junction (18 miles, 30-45 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Open sagebrush valleys, mountain views, transition from hot springs to river canyon |
| **Key stops** | Mammoth Hot Springs terraces, Undine Falls, Blacktail Plateau Drive (one-way side road) |
| **Wildlife** | Elk (they literally lounge on the Mammoth lawns), pronghorn on Blacktail Plateau, sometimes bears |
| **Best time** | Morning or evening for wildlife. Mammoth terraces are good any time. |
| **Construction** | The Mammoth-to-Tower segment has had recent construction. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check NPS road status. |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Mammoth Hot Springs Terraces:** Boardwalk system through active travertine terraces. Upper and Lower terraces. Budget 45-60 minutes. The formations change year to year as hot springs shift.
- **Undine Falls (Mile ~5 from Mammoth):** A 60-foot waterfall visible from the road. Short pullout, no hiking required.
- **Blacktail Plateau Drive (Mile ~8):** A one-way, 6-mile gravel road that loops off the main road through open sagebrush plateau. Excellent for wildlife (pronghorn, bison, sometimes bears) and avoids the main-road crowds. Takes about 30 minutes. Recommended if you have the time.
- **Petrified Tree (Mile ~14):** Short spur road to a standing petrified tree behind a fence. Quick 5-minute stop, a geological curiosity.

---

#### Segment B: Tower Junction to Canyon Village (19 miles, 40-60 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Mountain pass over Dunraven Pass (8,859 ft), panoramic views, subalpine forest |
| **Key stops** | Tower Fall, Dunraven Pass / Mt. Washburn trailhead, Washburn Hot Springs overlook |
| **Wildlife** | Bears (especially near Dunraven Pass — this is prime grizzly habitat), marmots at high elevation |
| **Best time** | Morning for clear views from Dunraven Pass. Midday haze reduces visibility. |
| **Construction** | **[VERIFY 2026]** This segment has been subject to major construction in recent years (complete road rebuild). Check NPS road status. |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Tower Fall (Mile ~2 from Tower Junction):** A 132-foot waterfall. Short walk from the parking lot (less than 0.5 miles). The overlook viewpoint is easy; the trail to the base has been closed/modified in recent years due to erosion — check current status.
- **Dunraven Pass (Mile ~10):** The highest point on the Grand Loop Road at 8,859 ft. Mt. Washburn trailhead (Dunraven Pass side) is here — a popular day hike to the 10,243 ft summit fire lookout with 360-degree views. The hike is 6.4 miles round trip with 1,400 ft gain, moderate difficulty.
- **Washburn Hot Springs Overlook (Mile ~14):** Views down into a thermal area in the valley below. A good pullout for photos.

---

#### Segment C: Canyon Village to Fishing Bridge / Yellowstone Lake (16 miles, 25-40 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Hayden Valley — wide open valley with the Yellowstone River, then transition to lake shore |
| **Key stops** | Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone (Upper and Lower Falls), Artist Point, Hayden Valley, Mud Volcano |
| **Wildlife** | Hayden Valley is second only to Lamar for wildlife — bison herds, grizzlies, wolves occasionally, pelicans, sandhill cranes |
| **Best time** | Dawn and dusk for Hayden Valley wildlife. Midday for the Canyon waterfalls (the mist creates rainbows in midday sun). |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone:** This alone could take 1-3 hours. The canyon is 800-1,200 ft deep with yellow-colored walls (this is how Yellowstone got its name). Two must-see viewpoints:
  - **Artist Point (South Rim):** The classic view of Lower Falls (308 ft) framed by the golden canyon walls. Short paved walk from parking lot. This is the most photographed view in Yellowstone. Go early morning for best light and fewest people.
  - **Brink of the Lower Falls (North Rim):** A steep trail (0.5 miles, 600 stairs) descends to a platform at the very brink of the falls. You feel the spray and hear the roar. Strenuous but short. The climb back up is the hard part.
  - **Lookout Point and Red Rock Point (North Rim):** Additional viewpoints of the falls from different angles.
  - **Upper Falls viewpoint:** A shorter, less dramatic falls (109 ft) but still impressive. Quick stop from the road.
- **Hayden Valley (Miles 5-12 from Canyon):** Wide, treeless valley — a former lake bed. The Yellowstone River meanders through. Drive slowly and scan for bison herds (often hundreds), grizzly bears (especially along the river in August when cutthroat trout spawn), and waterfowl. Multiple pullouts on both sides. This segment can take 30 minutes or 2 hours depending on wildlife.
- **Mud Volcano area (Mile ~13):** A boardwalk loop (0.7 miles) through one of Yellowstone's most active and violent thermal areas. Dragon's Mouth Spring, Mud Volcano, and Churning Caldron are highlights. Smells intensely of sulfur. Budget 30-45 minutes. Fascinating and different from the prettier thermal areas.
- **Fishing Bridge (Mile ~16):** Historic bridge over the Yellowstone River at the outlet of Yellowstone Lake. Fishing is no longer allowed here (to protect spawning cutthroat trout), but it is a good stop for views and a visitor center.

---

#### Segment D: Fishing Bridge to West Thumb / Grant Village (21 miles, 30-45 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Yellowstone Lake shoreline drive, forested with lake views |
| **Key stops** | Lake overlooks, Natural Bridge, West Thumb Geyser Basin |
| **Wildlife** | Bald eagles, pelicans, occasionally bears on the lakeshore |
| **Best time** | Any time. West Thumb is nice in morning light. |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Yellowstone Lake pullouts:** Several pullouts along the shore. The lake is massive (136 square miles, elevation 7,733 ft) — on windy days, waves look oceanic. On calm days, the Absaroka Range reflects in the water.
- **Natural Bridge (Mile ~2 south of Bridge Bay):** A 1-mile trail from the road leads to a 51-foot natural rock bridge over Bridge Creek. Easy walk, interesting geology.
- **West Thumb Geyser Basin (Mile ~20):** Thermal features right on the shore of Yellowstone Lake. Hot springs flow directly into the lake. A boardwalk loop (0.6 miles) takes 30-45 minutes. Smaller and less crowded than Old Faithful area, but the juxtaposition of boiling water and the cold lake is unique and fascinating.

---

#### Segment E: West Thumb to Old Faithful (17 miles, 30-40 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Forested, crossing the Continental Divide (twice!) |
| **Key stops** | Continental Divide crossing, Isa Lake, Craig Pass |
| **Wildlife** | Less reliably visible than Hayden or Lamar |
| **Best time** | Any time. Just a connecting segment. |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Continental Divide (Craig Pass, 8,262 ft):** A sign marks where you cross the Continental Divide. Isa Lake, right at the summit, is one of the only lakes in the world that drains to both the Atlantic and Pacific oceans (depending on water level and wind direction). Quick photo stop.

---

#### Segment F: Old Faithful Area (Budget 2-4 hours here, not a drive-through)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | The heart of Yellowstone's geothermal activity |
| **Key stops** | Old Faithful, Upper Geyser Basin, Grand Prismatic Spring, Midway Geyser Basin, Biscuit Basin, Black Sand Basin |
| **Best time** | Grand Prismatic: midday (10 AM - 2 PM) for the most vivid colors, seen from the overlook trail. Old Faithful: check eruption predictions at the visitor center (erupts approximately every 90 minutes). |

**Stops worth making (south to north):**
- **Old Faithful:** Yes, it is touristy. Yes, you should still see it. The eruption is genuinely impressive (130-180 ft). Check predicted eruption times at the visitor center and arrive 30 minutes early for a seat on the benches.
- **Upper Geyser Basin boardwalk:** The area around Old Faithful has the densest concentration of geysers on Earth. Walk the boardwalk loop (4+ miles if you do the full thing) past Morning Glory Pool, Castle Geyser, Grand Geyser (the world's tallest predictable geyser — if you can time it, it is more impressive than Old Faithful), and dozens more.
- **Biscuit Basin (2 miles north of Old Faithful):** Small boardwalk, less crowded, beautiful turquoise pools. The short Mystic Falls trail (1 mile to the falls) starts here.
- **Black Sand Basin (1 mile north of Old Faithful):** Another small thermal area with beautiful colored pools. Quick boardwalk, 15-20 minutes.
- **Midway Geyser Basin / Grand Prismatic Spring (6 miles north of Old Faithful):** Grand Prismatic is the largest hot spring in the US (370 ft diameter) and the third largest in the world. From the boardwalk at basin level, it is hard to grasp the scale. **The overlook trail (Fairy Falls trailhead, 1.2 miles round trip with ~100 ft gain) is essential** — this is where you get the Instagram-famous aerial view of the orange, yellow, and blue rings. Go midday for the most vivid colors and least steam obscuring the view.

---

#### Segment G: Old Faithful to Madison to Norris (30 miles, 50-70 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Firehole River canyon, forested, connecting two major geothermal areas |
| **Key stops** | Firehole Canyon Drive, Madison Junction, Gibbon Falls, Norris Geyser Basin |
| **Wildlife** | Bison and elk along the Firehole and Madison rivers, especially at dawn/dusk |
| **Best time** | Norris is excellent in morning light when steam is thick and dramatic. |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Firehole Canyon Drive (2 miles south of Madison):** A one-way side road through a narrow canyon with the Firehole River. Includes a swimming area — one of the only places in Yellowstone where swimming is officially allowed. The river is geothermally warmed. Great for a quick dip on a hot day. Also includes Firehole Falls (40 ft). Highly recommended as a brief detour.
- **Madison Junction:** Where the Firehole and Gibbon Rivers merge to form the Madison River. Good wildlife viewing area (elk, bison). Madison Campground is located here.
- **Gibbon Falls (Mile ~5 north of Madison):** An 84-foot waterfall visible from the road. Quick pullout stop.
- **Norris Geyser Basin (Mile ~14 north of Madison):** The hottest, oldest, and most dynamic thermal area in Yellowstone. Two loop trails (Porcelain Basin, 0.75 miles; Back Basin, 1.5 miles). Porcelain Basin is an alien landscape of milky blue pools and hissing steam vents. Steamboat Geyser in Back Basin is the world's tallest active geyser (300+ ft when it erupts — but eruptions are unpredictable and can be years apart). Budget 1-1.5 hours for both loops.

---

#### Segment H: Norris to Mammoth (21 miles, 35-50 min)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Character** | Transition from geothermal plateau to the lower, drier Mammoth area |
| **Key stops** | Roaring Mountain, Obsidian Cliff, Sheepeater Cliff, Golden Gate |
| **Wildlife** | Elk near Mammoth, sometimes bighorn sheep on cliff faces |
| **Best time** | Any time. |

**Stops worth making:**
- **Roaring Mountain (Mile ~5 from Norris):** A hillside of steaming fumaroles visible from the road. The "roaring" sound has diminished over the decades but it is still an impressive thermal display.
- **Obsidian Cliff (Mile ~9):** A cliff face of volcanic obsidian (black volcanic glass). Native Americans quarried obsidian here for thousands of years, and artifacts from this specific source have been found across the continent. Interpretive sign. Do not take samples.
- **Sheepeater Cliff (Mile ~12):** Columnar basalt formations along the Gardner River. Named for the Sheepeater band of the Shoshone, who were the only year-round inhabitants of the Yellowstone plateau. Nice picnic area here.
- **Golden Gate (Mile ~18):** The road passes through a narrow canyon with golden-yellow rock walls. A dramatic gateway to the Mammoth area. The old wooden trestle road (visible in historic photos) has been replaced by modern road engineering, but the canyon is still striking.

## 4. Teton Park Road and Jenny Lake Scenic Drive

### Overview

Grand Teton National Park has two main north-south roads: US-26/89/191 (the "outer" highway, fast and straight through the valley) and Teton Park Road (the "inner" road, closer to the mountains with the best views). Jenny Lake Scenic Drive is a one-way loop off Teton Park Road that gets you even closer to the base of the Tetons.

### Teton Park Road

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | Moose Junction to Jackson Lake Junction (runs parallel to the Teton Range on the west side of the valley) |
| **Distance** | 20 miles |
| **Drive time without stops** | 30-40 minutes |
| **Realistic time with stops** | 1.5 to 3 hours |
| **Elevation** | ~6,400-6,800 ft |
| **Seasonal closure** | Typically open early May to late October. The segment from Taggart Lake trailhead to Signal Mountain Lodge closes in winter (November-April). **[VERIFY 2026]** Check https://www.nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/roads.htm |
| **Road surface** | Paved, two lanes, good condition |

#### Key Stops (South to North)

- **Moose Junction / Craig Thomas Discovery and Visitor Center:** Starting point. The visitor center is well worth a stop — excellent exhibits on Teton geology and history, plus a large relief map of the range. Backcountry permit office is here.
- **Menor's Ferry Historic District (near Moose):** A preserved homestead with a replica ferry across the Snake River. The Chapel of the Transfiguration has a famous window framing the Tetons behind the altar. Quick 15-20 minute stop.
- **Teton Glacier Turnout (Mile ~3):** Pullout with interpretive sign identifying the major Teton peaks and the tiny remaining Teton Glacier. This is one of the first dramatic views of the range if you are driving north. The Tetons rise 7,000 ft directly above you.
- **Taggart Lake Trailhead (Mile ~4):** Not a viewpoint, but the start of a popular day hike to Taggart and Bradley Lakes (5-6 miles round trip, moderate). Good to know its location for planning.
- **Jenny Lake Scenic Drive turnoff (Mile ~8):** See the dedicated section below.
- **Jenny Lake Visitor Center and South Jenny Lake area (Mile ~8-9):** Visitor center, boat dock (shuttles across the lake to Cascade Canyon trailhead), general store, and several trailheads. This is the hub of Teton activity.
- **String Lake Trailhead (Mile ~10):** Access to String Lake, a shallow, warmer lake popular for swimming and kayaking. The String Lake loop trail (3.7 miles, flat, easy) is a beautiful walk. Leigh Lake, at the north end, is quieter and more scenic.
- **North Jenny Lake Junction (Mile ~10):** Where the scenic drive rejoins Teton Park Road. Continue north toward Signal Mountain.
- **Mt. Moran Turnout (Mile ~14):** Views of Mt. Moran (12,605 ft), the second-highest peak in the Tetons. The Skillet Glacier on its east face is a distinctive visual landmark.
- **Signal Mountain Summit Road (Mile ~15):** A 5-mile paved side road climbs to the top of Signal Mountain (7,593 ft, ~800 ft above the valley floor). Two overlooks at the top:
  - **Jackson Point Overlook:** Faces south toward the full Teton Range with the Snake River winding through the valley below. This was the vantage point for one of Ansel Adams' most famous photographs. Sunrise here is spectacular.
  - **Emma Matilda Overlook:** Faces east toward Two Ocean and Emma Matilda Lakes.
  - **Recommendation:** Drive Signal Mountain Road at sunrise or sunset. At sunrise, the Tetons are front-lit with alpenglow. At sunset, the valley glows golden. Either is unforgettable. Budget 45 minutes for the round trip including time at the overlooks.
- **Signal Mountain Lodge (Mile ~15):** Restaurant, gas station, and marina on Jackson Lake. One of the few gas stations inside the park.
- **Jackson Lake Junction (Mile ~20):** End of Teton Park Road. Junction with US-89 continuing north toward Yellowstone.

### Jenny Lake Scenic Drive

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | One-way loop off Teton Park Road along the eastern shore of Jenny Lake |
| **Distance** | 4 miles (one-way, southbound only) |
| **Drive time without stops** | 10-15 minutes |
| **Realistic time with stops** | 30-60 minutes |
| **Direction** | One-way southbound only |
| **Seasonal closure** | Same as Teton Park Road |

#### Why It Matters

This short one-way road puts you directly at the base of the Tetons with the peaks towering overhead. The Cathedral Group (Grand Teton, Mt. Owen, Teewinot) dominates the view. Several pullouts have short trails or views.

#### Key Stops

- **Cathedral Group Turnout:** The money shot. A pullout with an interpretive sign identifying the Grand Teton (13,775 ft), Mt. Owen (12,928 ft), and Teewinot Mountain (12,325 ft) directly above you. On a clear morning, the peaks glow pink with alpenglow. This is the definitive Teton viewpoint from road level.
- **String Lake pullout:** Views across String Lake toward the peaks.
- **Jenny Lake Overlook:** Views across Jenny Lake with the Tetons reflected on calm mornings. For the best reflection photos, be here before 9 AM when the morning air is still and the lake is glass.

### Other Teton Viewpoints Accessible by Car

These are on US-26/89/191 (the "outer" highway), not Teton Park Road, but are essential stops:

- **Schwabacher Landing:** Turn off US-89 onto a short dirt road to the Snake River bank. This is THE sunrise spot in the Tetons. Beaver ponds along the river perfectly reflect the full Teton Range. In late August/September, golden cottonwood trees frame the scene. Arrive 30-45 minutes before sunrise. This is a short walk from the parking area (0.1 miles). Widely regarded as the single best photography location in the park.
- **Snake River Overlook:** The Ansel Adams viewpoint. A pullout on US-89 where Adams took his famous photograph of the Snake River curving toward the Tetons. The cottonwood trees have grown and the view is slightly different now, but still magnificent. Best at sunrise.
- **Teton Point Turnout:** Another US-89 pullout with a classic Teton view. Less famous than Schwabacher or Snake River Overlook, but still stunning and often less crowded.
- **Mormon Row Historic District:** Turn off US-89 onto a side road leading to the iconic Moulton Barns — weathered wooden barns with the full Teton Range rising behind them. This is one of the most photographed scenes in the American West. Best at sunrise (front-lit Tetons behind the barns). The T.A. Moulton Barn and the John Moulton Barn are both here.
  - **Short walk:** It is flat and easy — walk from the small parking area along the road. Budget 15-30 minutes.
  - **Wildlife:** Bison are regularly seen in the sagebrush flats around Mormon Row.

## 5. Teton Pass (ID-22)

### Overview

Teton Pass is the mountain road connecting Jackson, Wyoming (east side) to Driggs and Victor, Idaho (west side). You will drive this if you visit Teton Boulder Park for bouldering or want to explore the quieter, less expensive west side of the Tetons.

### Quick Facts

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | WY-22 from Jackson to the summit, becoming ID-33 on the Idaho side to Victor/Driggs |
| **Distance** | ~25 miles (Jackson to Driggs) |
| **Drive time without stops** | 45 minutes to 1 hour |
| **Realistic time with stops** | 1 to 1.5 hours |
| **Summit elevation** | 8,431 ft |
| **Elevation gain** | ~2,000 ft from Jackson (6,237 ft) to summit; ~3,400 ft descent from summit to Driggs (~5,100 ft) |
| **Road surface** | Paved, two lanes, well-maintained but steep and winding |
| **Seasonal closure** | Open year-round (plowed in winter), but winter driving can be treacherous. In late summer, no issues. |
| **Grade** | 10% grade on the west (Idaho) side — steep. Use low gear on descent. |
| **RV / trailer** | Not recommended for large RVs or trailers. The grades and switchbacks are serious. |

### What to Expect

- **East side (Jackson to summit):** The road climbs steadily through Wilson, WY (a small community with a few restaurants and the Stagecoach Bar — famous for Thursday night country music dancing). The climb is gradual at first, then steepens dramatically in the final miles to the summit. Switchbacks with good views back toward the Teton Range.
- **Summit:** A parking area at the top. The Coal Creek trailhead is here — a popular starting point for backcountry skiing in winter but also a hiking access point in summer. Views to both sides. There is a small pullout with interpretive information.
  - **Short hike from summit:** The Glory Bowl area and the summit ridge offer short walks (0.5-1 mile) with panoramic views of both the Teton Range (east) and the Teton Valley / Big Hole Mountains (west). This is also a popular paragliding launch site — you may see pilots soaring off the ridge.
- **West side (summit to Victor/Driggs):** The descent is steeper than the climb. 10% grade for several miles. Runaway truck ramps exist — this tells you something about the grade. Use low gear, do not ride your brakes. The road descends through aspen groves into the broad Teton Valley.
- **Victor, ID (Mile ~18 from Jackson):** Small town at the base of the pass. A few restaurants and shops. Victor Emporium is a quirky general store/soda fountain worth a quick stop.
- **Driggs, ID (Mile ~25 from Jackson):** The main town on the west side. Home to Yostmark Mountain Equipment (bouldering pad rental), several restaurants, and access to Teton Boulder Park. More affordable and less crowded than Jackson. See the bouldering-spots feature for details on Teton Boulder Park.

### Why Drive Teton Pass

| Reason | Details |
|---|---|
| **Bouldering** | Teton Boulder Park is accessed from the Driggs side. If she wants to boulder, this is the drive. See feature 5 (bouldering-spots). |
| **West-side Teton trailheads** | Several excellent hikes start on the west side: Table Mountain (the Ansel Adams view of the Grand Teton), Alaska Basin, South Teton. The Teton Canyon trailhead in the Jedediah Smith Wilderness is reached from Driggs. |
| **Cheaper food/gas** | Victor and Driggs are significantly cheaper than Jackson for gas, groceries, and restaurants. |
| **The drive itself** | Scenic, dramatic, and a genuine mountain pass. Not as long or famous as Beartooth but a real driving experience with great views. |

### Gas

- Fill up in Jackson (multiple stations, competitive prices for the area) before heading over the pass.
- Victor and Driggs both have gas stations on the Idaho side.
- The pass itself has no services.

## 6. Other Scenic Drives Worth Knowing

### A. Paradise Valley (US-89, Bozeman to Gardiner)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | US-89 from Livingston/Bozeman area south to Gardiner (Yellowstone north entrance) |
| **Distance** | ~55 miles (from Livingston to Gardiner) |
| **Drive time** | 1 hour 10 minutes |
| **Character** | The Yellowstone River valley flanked by the Absaroka Range (east) and Gallatin Range (west). Ranch country with big open views. |

**Why it matters:** If you fly into Bozeman and drive to Yellowstone's north entrance, you will drive this road. It is beautiful — rolling ranch land with the river beside you and mountains on both sides. Not as dramatic as Beartooth, but a wonderful entry to the ecosystem.

**Stops:**
- **Chico Hot Springs (Mile ~30 from Livingston):** A historic hot springs resort with a large outdoor pool. Open to day visitors for a fee (~$8-10). Good for soaking tired muscles after a hiking day. The Chico Saloon has live music and food. **[VERIFY 2026]** Check hours and prices: https://www.chicohotsprings.com/
- **Yankee Jim Canyon (Mile ~45):** The road passes through a narrow canyon — the most dramatic section of the drive. The Yellowstone River roars through a tight gorge.
- **Gardiner:** Gateway town at the north entrance. The Roosevelt Arch marks the original entrance to Yellowstone (1903). Worth a photo stop.

### B. Gallatin Canyon (US-191, Bozeman to West Yellowstone)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | US-191 from Bozeman through Gallatin Canyon to West Yellowstone |
| **Distance** | ~91 miles |
| **Drive time** | 1.5 hours |
| **Character** | Tight mountain canyon along the Gallatin River, opening into broad meadows near West Yellowstone |

**Why it matters:** If you enter Yellowstone via the west entrance, this is your road. The canyon itself is scenic — steep canyon walls, the Gallatin River (famous for fly fishing and the movie "A River Runs Through It"), and roadside bouldering.

**Stops:**
- **Gallatin Canyon bouldering:** Several pullouts along the canyon have roadside boulders. This is the closest bouldering to Yellowstone. Problems range from V0 to V10+. See the bouldering-spots feature for specific locations.
- **Big Sky turnoff (Mile ~45):** The road to Big Sky Resort and the Lone Mountain area. If mountain biking is on the agenda, Big Sky has trail systems. Not directly on the loop route but worth knowing about.
- **Earthquake Lake / Hebgen Lake area (Mile ~75-85):** In 1959, a massive earthquake (7.3 magnitude) triggered a landslide that dammed the Madison River, creating Earthquake Lake and killing 28 people. The Earthquake Lake Visitor Center tells the story and overlooks the slide area. A sobering and fascinating stop. The ghost village of cabins buried by the slide is still visible.

### C. John D. Rockefeller Jr. Memorial Parkway (US-89/191, Yellowstone South Entrance to Grand Teton)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | The 7.5-mile stretch of US-89/191 connecting Yellowstone's south entrance to Grand Teton's north boundary |
| **Distance** | ~7.5 miles |
| **Drive time** | 10-15 minutes |
| **Character** | Forested corridor along the Snake River headwaters. Connecting tissue between the two parks. |

**Why it matters:** You will drive this when transitioning from Yellowstone to the Tetons. It is not as dramatic as either park, but Flagg Ranch (at approximately the midpoint) has a gas station, general store, and campground — useful for topping off fuel.

**Stop:**
- **Flagg Ranch:** Gas station (one of few between Grant Village in Yellowstone and Colter Bay in the Tetons), small store, restrooms. Not scenic, but useful.

### D. Ox Bow Bend (on US-89 in Grand Teton NP)

Not a separate drive, but a critical pullout on US-89 between Moran Junction and Jackson Lake Junction.

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **What** | A sweeping bend in the Snake River with Mt. Moran reflected in still water |
| **Best time** | Sunrise, when the water is calm and the mountains are lit |
| **Wildlife** | Moose, bald eagles, great blue herons, beavers, sometimes otters |
| **Photo quality** | World-class. One of the iconic Teton compositions alongside Schwabacher Landing and Mormon Row |

Pull off at the signed turnout. Bring your camera and a tripod if you have one. The reflections on a calm morning are otherworldly.

### E. Antelope Flats Road / Mormon Row Road (Grand Teton NP)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | Turn off US-89 near Moose Junction, loop through sagebrush flats past the Moulton Barns |
| **Distance** | ~5 miles loop |
| **Drive time** | 15-20 minutes (mostly gravel, well-graded) |
| **Character** | Open sagebrush flats with the Teton Range as backdrop |

**Why it matters:** This is where you find the iconic Moulton Barns (mentioned in Section 4). Also excellent for wildlife — bison, pronghorn, and sometimes moose. The flats have a big-sky, American West feel that contrasts with the vertical drama of the Tetons.

### F. Grassy Lake Road (connecting Flagg Ranch to Ashton, ID)

| Detail | Info |
|---|---|
| **Route** | Unpaved road from Flagg Ranch (between Yellowstone and Tetons) west to Ashton, Idaho |
| **Distance** | ~50 miles |
| **Drive time** | 2-3 hours (unpaved, slow going) |
| **Character** | Remote, wild, backcountry. Passes through national forest with access to Cascade Corner / Bechler area trailheads. |

**Why it matters:** Only for the adventurous. This is the back way to the Bechler region (Yellowstone's "Cascade Corner" with waterfalls unreachable by other roads) and provides access to several trailheads. The road is rough but usually passable for passenger cars in dry conditions. AWD/high-clearance recommended.

**Only drive this if:**
- You want to access Bechler region trailheads from the south/west
- You enjoy remote backcountry road driving
- Your rental car can handle rough gravel

### Golden Rules

3. **Fill up in gateway towns** (Bozeman, West Yellowstone, Jackson, Red Lodge) where prices are lower than park concessioner stations.
4. **Check hours for small stations** — Cooke City, Tower Junction, and some park stations may close early or seasonally. **[VERIFY 2026]** Park concessioner gas station hours at https://www.yellowstonenationalparklodges.com/ and https://www.gtlc.com/.

## Final Notes

- **Expect wildlife jams.** In Yellowstone, a bear or bison near the road causes traffic to stop completely. This is not an inconvenience — this is part of the experience. Build extra time into every driving estimate.
- **The speed limit matters.** Park speed limits (45 mph max, 25 in developed areas) are strictly enforced and exist to protect wildlife. Animals are everywhere, including on the road.
- **Early morning is magic.** For both wildlife and photography, the period from first light to about 9 AM is worth more than the rest of the day combined. Plan your most scenic drives for this window.
- **Sunscreen and layers, always.** The elevation difference across these drives is enormous (5,555 ft in Red Lodge to 10,947 ft on Beartooth Pass). Weather at the top can be 30-40 degrees colder than the bottom. Always carry layers, even on a warm day.
- **Download everything offline.** Maps, music, podcasts. Cell coverage is unreliable to nonexistent across most of this route.
