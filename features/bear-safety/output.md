## What You Already Know vs. What's Different About Grizzly Country

You are not starting from zero. Your Yosemite experience gave you solid foundations. Here is an honest assessment of what transfers and what does not.

### What Your Yosemite Experience Taught You (and Still Applies)

- **Food storage discipline.** You already know: all food, coolers, scented items go in the bear box (or canister) every single time, no exceptions. This habit is critical and you already have it.
- **Cooking away from camp.** You know not to cook in or right next to your tent. Good.
- **Bear canister use.** You own one and have used it. You know how to pack it, close it properly, and deploy it away from your sleeping area.
- **General awareness.** You watch for bears on trail, you don't approach wildlife, you understand bears are motivated by food, not malice.
- **Calm demeanor around wildlife.** You have seen bears (black bears) and did not panic. This emotional baseline matters.

### What's Different in Grizzly Country (The Critical Gaps)

| Topic | Yosemite (Black Bear Country) | Greater Yellowstone (Grizzly Country) |
|---|---|---|
| **Species behavior** | Black bears are generally timid, avoid confrontation, and can be scared off by yelling and looking big. They rarely injure humans. | Grizzlies can be defensive-aggressive, especially mothers with cubs. They may charge when surprised. They do NOT always flee from humans. |
| **Defensive attacks** | Black bears almost never attack defensively. If one approaches, you fight back. | Grizzlies frequently mount defensive attacks when surprised at close range. **You do NOT fight a defensive grizzly — you play dead.** This is the single most important behavioral difference. |
| **Encounter protocol** | One protocol: make yourself big, yell, throw things, fight back. | Two different protocols depending on the type of encounter (defensive vs. predatory). Getting this wrong can make things worse. |
| **Bear spray** | Optional / rarely carried in Yosemite. | **Absolutely essential. Carry it on your body at all times. Each person needs their own canister.** NPS recommends it for all hikers in Yellowstone and Grand Teton. |
| **Noise on trail** | Nice to have but not critical. | **Critical habit.** Announce your presence around blind corners, dense brush, stream crossings. Most grizzly encounters happen because the bear was surprised. |
| **Food attraction distance** | Black bears learn to associate humans with food but are mostly scavenging at night. | Grizzlies have an even stronger sense of smell (estimated 2,100x better than humans). They travel long distances for food. A single violation of food storage can attract a bear that then becomes a problem for the next camper. |
| **Camp layout** | Important but flexible. | **Strict triangle layout:** Cook/eat area, food storage area, and sleep area should each be 200+ feet apart. This is not a suggestion — it is regulation in backcountry and best practice in frontcountry. |
| **Nighttime behavior** | Black bears may visit campsites but are typically scared off by noise. | Grizzlies can visit campsites too. If a grizzly enters your camp at night and is NOT going for food, the situation is different from a daytime surprise — it may be predatory (very rare, but the protocol differs). |
| **Consequences of a mistake** | A black bear gets your food. Annoying, possibly dangerous, but rarely life-threatening. | A grizzly encounter gone wrong can be fatal. The stakes are genuinely higher. This is not meant to scare you — it is meant to ensure you take preparation seriously. |

### The Bottom Line

Your Yosemite experience gives you **excellent food-handling discipline and general wildlife awareness** — these are foundational. What you need to add is:

1. **Grizzly-specific encounter protocols** (defensive vs. predatory — learn the difference, practice the responses mentally)
2. **Bear spray proficiency** (buy it, learn to draw it, know the range, practice the motion)
3. **Trail noise as a constant habit** (not optional, not occasional — every blind corner, every dense section)
4. **Understanding that grizzlies demand respect, not fear** (you will likely see one from a safe distance and it will be one of the highlights of the trip)

## How to Identify Grizzly vs. Black Bear

This matters because your response to an encounter depends partly on the species. Both grizzly bears and black bears live in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem.

**Important:** "Black bear" is a species name, not a color. Black bears can be brown, cinnamon, or blonde. Grizzlies can be very dark. Color alone is NOT reliable.

| Feature | Grizzly Bear | Black Bear |
|---|---|---|
| **Shoulder hump** | Prominent muscular hump on shoulders — visible in profile. **This is the single best identifier.** | No shoulder hump. Highest point is the rump. |
| **Face profile** | Concave (dish-shaped) face — the forehead curves inward from brow to nose. | Straight face profile — relatively flat line from forehead to nose. |
| **Ears** | Short, rounded ears that appear small relative to the head. | Taller, more pointed ears that appear larger. |
| **Claws** | Long (2-4 inches), light-colored, visible from a distance. Designed for digging. | Short (1-1.5 inches), dark, curved. Designed for climbing. |
| **Size** | Adults typically 300-700 lbs (males can exceed 600 lbs). Standing up: 6-7 feet. | Adults typically 150-400 lbs. Standing up: 5-6 feet. |
| **Tracks** | Front toes relatively straight across. Claw marks far from toe pads. | Front toes more curved. Claw marks close to toe pads. |
| **Behavior when treed** | Adults do NOT climb trees (too heavy). Cubs can climb. | Excellent climbers — adults and cubs will go up trees readily. |

**At a distance, focus on:**
1. Shoulder hump (present = grizzly)
2. Face shape (dish = grizzly, straight = black bear)
3. Ear size relative to head (small = grizzly)

**In practice:** If you are close enough that encounter protocol matters and you are not sure of the species, treat it as a grizzly. That is always the safer assumption in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem.

## Grizzly Encounter Protocols

This is the most important section of this document. Read it, discuss it together, and mentally rehearse the scenarios before the trip.

### Scenario 1: Distant Sighting (100+ yards / length of a football field)

**What's happening:** You see a bear (any species) at a distance. It may or may not have noticed you.

**What to do:**
- Stop and observe. Enjoy the moment — this is why you came.
- Do NOT approach. NPS regulations require you stay at least **100 yards (91 m) from bears** in both Yellowstone and Grand Teton.
- If the bear is near the trail ahead, wait for it to move off or take a detour. Do not try to sneak past.
- If the bear is moving toward the trail, stop and give it time. Most bears will pass through.
- Use binoculars or a zoom lens. Never approach for a photo.
- Note wind direction — if the wind is blowing from you toward the bear, it may smell you and move away on its own.

### Scenario 2: Medium Distance (50-100 yards) — The Bear Notices You

**What's happening:** You and the bear are aware of each other but not in immediate close range.

**What to do:**
- Stop moving. Assess the situation calmly.
- Talk in a calm, low voice so the bear can identify you as human (not prey). "Hey bear, we're here, we're just passing through."
- Slowly wave your arms to help the bear identify you as human.
- If the bear stands on its hind legs, it is NOT being aggressive — it is trying to see and smell you better. This is curiosity, not threat.
- Begin slowly backing away while facing the bear. Move diagonally away from the bear's path.
- **Do NOT run.** Running can trigger a chase response. Grizzlies can run 35+ mph. You cannot outrun one.
- Do NOT drop your pack. It can provide protection if things escalate.
- Get your bear spray out and ready (thumb on safety, pointed generally toward the bear).

### Scenario 3: Surprise Close Encounter (Under 50 yards) — Bear is Startled

**What's happening:** You come around a corner, crest a ridge, or step through dense brush and suddenly there is a grizzly very close. The bear is surprised. It may "woof," snap its jaws, lay its ears back, or bluff charge.

**This is the most common dangerous encounter scenario and the one most likely to happen if you are not making enough noise.**

**What to do:**
- **FREEZE.** Do not run, do not scream. Stay still.
- Talk calmly and quietly: "Hey bear, it's okay, we're just people."
- Avoid direct eye contact (can be perceived as a threat), but keep the bear in your peripheral vision.
- Slowly back away if the bear is not approaching.
- **If the bear charges:** Many grizzly charges are BLUFF charges. The bear runs toward you and veers off at the last moment. Standing your ground through a bluff charge is terrifying but often the right move. If the bear keeps coming:
  - **Deploy bear spray** when the bear is within 20-30 feet (see bear spray section below).
  - **If the bear makes contact (defensive attack): PLAY DEAD.**
    - Drop to the ground face down.
    - Spread your legs to make it harder for the bear to flip you.
    - Clasp your hands behind your neck to protect your neck and spine.
    - Keep your pack on — it protects your back.
    - **Do not move or make noise.** The bear is trying to neutralize a perceived threat. Once it believes the threat is gone, it will usually leave.
    - Remain still for several minutes after the bear leaves. Bears sometimes watch from a distance and will return if you get up too quickly.

### Scenario 4: Predatory Approach — Bear Follows You or Approaches Silently

**What's happening:** A bear (usually at some distance initially) is following you, approaching you quietly, circling you, or approaching your camp at night in a focused, deliberate way. It is NOT acting startled. It is NOT defending cubs. It appears interested in YOU, not in your food.

**This is extremely rare but has a completely different protocol from a defensive encounter.**

**How to tell the difference:**
- **Defensive:** Bear is startled, huffing, jaw-popping, ears back, acting stressed. Often a mother with cubs. The bear wants you to GO AWAY.
- **Predatory:** Bear is calm, quiet, focused, following you, approaching deliberately. The bear sees you as potential food. This is vanishingly rare with grizzlies but you need to know the protocol.

**What to do:**
- **Do NOT play dead.** Playing dead during a predatory encounter tells the bear it has succeeded.
- Make yourself as large and intimidating as possible.
- Shout, bang pots, throw rocks. Be aggressive.
- Stand your ground. Group together with your partner to look larger.
- Deploy bear spray if the bear comes within range.
- **If the bear makes contact in what you believe is a predatory attack: FIGHT BACK** with everything you have. Hit the nose, eyes, and face. Use rocks, trekking poles, anything available.

### Scenario 5: Bear Approaching Your Camp (for Food)

**What's happening:** A bear enters your camp and is going for food, coolers, or scented items. This is food-conditioned behavior, not predatory.

**What to do:**
- Make noise to try to scare it away (yelling, banging pots) — this sometimes works, especially with black bears.
- **Do not try to retrieve your food from the bear.** It is not worth it.
- If the bear is not deterred by noise and is focused on food, back away and let it have whatever it has found. Report it to a ranger immediately.
- If you are in a developed campground, other campers making noise together can sometimes deter the bear.
- **Never get between a bear and food it has found.** Never get between a bear and its escape route.

### Scenario 6: Bear on a Carcass or Mother with Cubs

**What's happening:** You encounter a bear feeding on an animal carcass, or you see a mother grizzly with cubs.

**What to do:**
- Back away immediately. Do not approach under any circumstances.
- If a bear is on a carcass, leave the area entirely and report it to a ranger (it may warrant a trail closure).
- If you see cubs, the mother is nearby even if you do not see her. Back away in the direction you came from.
- A mother grizzly with cubs is the single most dangerous situation. She will aggressively defend her cubs. Give her every reason to believe you are not a threat.

### Quick Reference: Defensive vs. Predatory

| | Defensive | Predatory |
|---|---|---|
| **Bear behavior** | Startled, huffing, jaw-popping, stressed | Calm, quiet, deliberate, following |
| **Context** | Surprise encounter, cubs nearby, on a carcass | Bear approaches silently, follows you, enters tent at night |
| **Your response** | Stay calm, back away, play dead if contacted | Be aggressive, fight back, do not play dead |
| **Bear spray** | Yes, deploy at 20-30 feet | Yes, deploy at 20-30 feet |
| **Frequency** | The vast majority of bear encounters | Extremely rare |

## Bear Spray: Your Most Important Piece of Gear

Bear spray is the single most effective tool for stopping a charging grizzly. Studies show it is more effective than firearms in stopping bear attacks (Smith et al., 2008 — Journal of Wildlife Management: bear spray stopped aggressive bear behavior 92% of the time; 98% of people who used spray were uninjured).

### Specifications — What to Buy

| Spec | Requirement | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Active ingredient** | Capsaicin and related capsaicinoids (CRC), 2% concentration | This is the standard for bear-specific sprays. NOT the same as self-defense pepper spray. |
| **EPA registration** | Must be EPA-registered as a bear deterrent | This ensures it meets the spray distance and concentration standards. Look for the EPA registration number on the canister. |
| **Minimum size** | 7.9 oz (225g) net weight of spray | Smaller canisters do not spray long enough. The standard canisters are 7.9 oz or 9.2 oz. |
| **Spray distance** | Minimum 20 feet, ideally 25-30+ feet | You need it to reach a charging bear at distance. |
| **Spray duration** | Minimum 6 seconds of continuous spray | This gives you enough to lay down a wall of spray. |
| **Holster** | Chest or hip holster that allows one-handed draw | CRITICAL. Bear spray in your pack is useless. It must be on your body, accessible in 2 seconds. |

### Recommended Brands

- **Counter Assault** (8.1 oz or 10.2 oz) — the original bear spray, made in Montana. Widely available locally. 30+ foot range, 7-second spray duration.
- **UDAP Bear Spray** (7.9 oz or 13.4 oz) — developed by a bear attack survivor. 30-foot range. Very popular in the Yellowstone area.
- **Frontiersman Bear Spray** (7.9 oz or 9.2 oz) — 30-foot range, 5-second (7.9 oz) or 8-second (9.2 oz) spray duration.

All three are excellent. Buy whichever is available at your first stop. The brand matters far less than having it, knowing how to use it, and carrying it accessibly.

### How to Use Bear Spray

**Practice the draw motion before you hit the trail.** Do this in the parking lot (without firing the canister). You want it to be muscle memory.

**Steps:**

1. **Draw.** Reach across to your holster (chest or hip). Grab the canister, pull it out. The motion should be smooth — practice it.
2. **Safety off.** Remove the safety clip with your thumb. On most canisters, this is a plastic tab or flip-top on the trigger.
3. **Aim low.** Point the canister slightly downward toward the ground in front of the charging bear. You are creating a wall of spray the bear runs into, not trying to hit it in the face like a sniper.
4. **Fire at 20-30 feet.** Start spraying when the bear is about 20-30 feet away (roughly 2-3 car lengths). Spray too early and the cloud dissipates before the bear reaches it.
5. **Spray in a side-to-side sweeping motion** — creating a cloud wall, not a narrow stream.
6. **Keep spraying** until the bear diverts or stops.
7. **Back away** while continuing to face the bear. Have a second canister (your partner's) ready if the bear continues.

**Wind considerations:**
- Before heading out each day, note the wind direction.
- If spraying into a strong headwind, the spray will blow back toward you. You will get a dose of pepper spray in your face. This is painful and temporarily debilitating but not dangerous. **Spray anyway.** A face full of pepper spray is far better than a grizzly attack. The spray will still create a cloud that the bear hits.
- Ideally, angle your spray slightly to the side if the wind is directly at you.
- Side wind: spray ahead of the bear's path (lead it, like shooting a moving target).

**Important notes:**
- Bear spray is NOT a preventive measure — do not spray it on your gear, tent, or body. It is an irritant attractant when residue dries.
- Do not test-fire your canister. You need every second of spray. (Buy a practice canister — an inert training canister — if you want to practice the actual motion of firing. Available at REI and online.)
- Bear spray expires. Check the expiration date when purchasing. Most canisters are good for 3-4 years from manufacture.
- Each person carries their own canister. Do not share one between two people — if you are separated on the trail (bathroom break, one person is ahead), you each need protection.

### Do You Each Need One? (Yes.)

**Yes. One per person, accessible on your body at all times while hiking, and within arm's reach while in camp.** This is not optional and not overkill. Here is why:

- You may be briefly separated on the trail (bathroom, one person stopping to photograph).
- If one person's spray misfires or the wind carries it away, the second person is the backup.
- In a sustained charge, you may need the volume of two canisters.
- It is what the NPS recommends.

**Budget: Two canisters with holsters = approximately $60-100 total.** This is the best money you will spend on the trip.

## Making Noise on the Trail

Most grizzly encounters — and virtually all dangerous ones — happen when a bear is surprised at close range. Making noise eliminates most of this risk.

### When to Make Noise

- **Always** when approaching blind corners on the trail
- **Always** when hiking through dense brush, thick forest, or tall willows
- **Always** when hiking near streams or rivers (the water noise masks your footsteps)
- **Always** when hiking in the wind (same reason)
- **Always** when approaching a rise or crest where you cannot see the other side
- **Extra noise** in the early morning and late evening when bears are most active
- **Extra noise** in areas with known bear activity (check with rangers, trailhead signs)
- **Extra noise** when you can see bear sign (scat, tracks, dug-up ground, overturned rocks)

### How to Make Noise

**Best methods (in order of effectiveness):**

1. **Talk loudly and frequently.** "Hey bear! Coming through! Just two hikers!" This is the best method because your voice is uniquely human and bears recognize it. Have regular conversations at a volume that carries.
2. **Clap your hands** at blind corners and before entering dense sections.
3. **Call out "Hey bear!"** specifically before blind corners. This is a Greater Yellowstone trail habit — you will hear other hikers doing it. It feels silly the first time. Do it anyway.

**Less effective but still used:**
- **Bear bells.** The small jingle bells you clip to your pack. Studies are mixed on their effectiveness — the sound is not loud enough in wind, near water, or over distance. They are better than silence but much worse than your voice. **Do not rely on bear bells alone.** Use them as a supplement to voice.
- **Clapping trekking poles together.** Useful when your voice is tired.

### Hiking in Groups

- A group of two or more talking at normal conversation volume is far less likely to surprise a bear than a solo hiker.
- **Stay together.** Do not let your group spread out so much that you are effectively solo hikers on the same trail.
- Groups of 4+ have almost zero risk of a bear attack. Groups of 2 are very safe as long as you are making noise and staying together.
- The two of you hiking and talking together is a solid safety setup. Just keep the conversation going through the sections listed above.

### Solo Hiking Considerations

If either of you hikes solo for any reason (one person resting at camp, split up for different objectives):
- Make MORE noise than you would together.
- Sing, talk to yourself, call out frequently.
- Be especially cautious on less-traveled trails where bears are less accustomed to human traffic.
- Carry bear spray at the ready (hand near the holster, not buried in your pack).
- Consider avoiding solo hiking in areas with known recent grizzly activity — check at the ranger station or backcountry office.

## Camp Safety in Grizzly Country

### The Bear-Safe Camp Layout

In grizzly country, your camp should be organized in a **triangle (or line) with at least 200 feet (70 paces) between each zone:**

```
                       SLEEPING AREA
                      (your tent)
                          /\
                         /  \
                        /    \
                200+ ft/      \200+ ft
                      /        \
                     /          \
    COOKING/EATING AREA ---- FOOD STORAGE AREA
                    200+ ft
                    (bear box, bear canister,
                     or approved hang)
```

**Zone 1 — Sleeping Area (your tent):**
- Nothing with any scent: no food, no wrappers, no gum, no toothpaste, no sunscreen, no lip balm, no bug spray, no deodorant.
- Change out of clothes you cooked in before getting in the tent. Store cooking clothes with your food.
- Empty pockets — no energy bar wrappers, no trail mix residue.

**Zone 2 — Cooking/Eating Area:**
- At least 200 feet from your tent (roughly 70 adult paces).
- At least 200 feet from your food storage.
- Ideally DOWNWIND of your tent (check prevailing wind).
- Cook, eat, wash dishes, and brush teeth in this zone.
- Strain dishwater and pack out food particles. Scatter strained water broadly (do not dump it in one spot).
- Do not dump grease — pack it out in a container.

**Zone 3 — Food Storage Area:**
- At least 200 feet from your tent.
- In frontcountry campgrounds: use the provided bear box (metal food locker at your campsite).
- In backcountry: use your bear canister or the bear pole if the campsite has one.
- Store ALL scented items here (see full list below).

### What Counts as a "Scented Item" (Store with Food, Not in Tent)

This list is more extensive than you might expect:

- All food and drinks (including sealed/canned items)
- Coolers (even empty ones — they smell like food)
- Cooking pots, utensils, stoves, and fuel canisters
- Dishes and cups
- Garbage and recycling
- Toothpaste and toothbrush
- Soap, shampoo, conditioner
- Deodorant
- Sunscreen and lip balm
- Bug spray / DEET
- Hand sanitizer
- Feminine hygiene products
- Pet food (not applicable to you, but notable)
- Clothes you cooked in
- Empty food containers and wrappers

**Rule of thumb:** If it has a smell — any smell — it goes in the bear box or canister, not in your tent. When in doubt, store it.

### Frontcountry Campground Rules (Car Camping)

In developed campgrounds in Yellowstone and Grand Teton:
- Every campsite has a **bear box** (metal food storage locker). Use it for ALL food and scented items when you are not actively eating or cooking.
- **Do not leave food in your car** — bears can (and do) break into cars. NPS regulations require food to be in the bear box or on your person. Some campgrounds allow food in hard-sided vehicles (not convertibles, not tents-on-cars) as an alternative. Check the specific campground's posted rules.
- **Do not leave food unattended at your campsite**, even briefly.
- Keep a clean camp at all times. Wipe down your picnic table after meals.
- Lock your car and close windows at night (bears can open unlocked car doors).

### Backcountry-Specific Camp Safety

If you do a backcountry overnight:
- Use your **bear canister** for all food and scented items. Place it at least 200 feet from your tent, on flat ground away from a cliff edge (bears may knock it around).
- Some Yellowstone backcountry campsites have **bear poles** (tall poles with hooks for hanging food bags). If a bear pole is present, use it.
- Cook at least 200 feet from your tent — the backcountry campsite will often have a designated cooking area (look for the fire ring or food prep area away from the tent pad).
- No food or scented items in the tent. Ever. Period.
- Hang your pack (or store in canister area) if it has been in contact with food.
- Filter and treat water — this is not bear-related, but relevant for backcountry safety.

### Nighttime Protocol

- Keep bear spray within arm's reach inside your tent. Unholster it and place it next to your sleeping pad where you can grab it without fumbling.
- If you hear a bear in camp at night:
  - **First:** Determine if the bear is going for food or approaching your tent.
  - **Bear going for food at the bear box/canister:** Make noise from inside your tent. Bang pots, yell. Most bears will be deterred. If not, let it go — the bear box or canister will hold. Do not leave your tent to confront a bear at night.
  - **Bear approaching or sniffing your tent (with no food inside):** This is unusual and potentially concerning (predatory interest). Talk firmly: "Hey bear, get out of here." If the bear persists and is pressing on the tent, prepare to use bear spray and be prepared to fight. This scenario is extraordinarily rare.
- Use a headlamp if you need to leave the tent at night. Scan around before stepping out. Make noise.

## Park-Specific Regulations

### Yellowstone National Park

| Regulation | Details |
|---|---|
| **Minimum distance from bears** | 100 yards (91 m) for bears (grizzly and black bear) and wolves. 25 yards for other wildlife. **This is federal law, not a guideline.** Violation can result in a fine of up to $5,000 and/or 6 months in jail. |
| **Bear spray** | "Recommended for all hikers" per NPS. Not legally required but effectively mandatory by every measure of common sense. |
| **Food storage** | Required to store all food, garbage, and scented items in bear-resistant containers (bear boxes in campgrounds, bear canisters or bear poles in backcountry). Food may not be left unattended. |
| **Cooking in backcountry** | Must cook and eat at least 200 feet from your tent in designated cooking areas at backcountry campsites. |
| **Trail closures** | Yellowstone closes trails and areas when there is significant bear activity (bears on carcasses, denning areas, etc.). Closures are posted at trailheads and visitor centers. **Check current closures before each hike.** |
| **Reporting** | Report all bear encounters (especially aggressive behavior, bears near trails, bears in campgrounds) to the nearest ranger or visitor center. |
| **Backcountry permits** | Required for all overnight backcountry trips. A bear safety orientation is part of the backcountry permit process. You WILL receive a briefing. |
| **Carcass protocol** | If you encounter a large animal carcass (dead elk, bison, etc.), leave the area immediately. A grizzly may be nearby defending it. Report it to a ranger. |

**Where to check trail closures:**
- Yellowstone NPS website: https://www.nps.gov/yell/planyourvisit/situationreport.htm **[VERIFY 2026 URL]**
- Backcountry office at any visitor center
- Ranger stations at trailheads (posted signs)
- Yellowstone NPS social media accounts for real-time updates

### Grand Teton National Park

| Regulation | Details |
|---|---|
| **Minimum distance from bears** | 100 yards (91 m) for bears and wolves. Same as Yellowstone. |
| **Bear spray** | Recommended for all hikers. Same guidance as Yellowstone. |
| **Food storage** | Required in all campgrounds and backcountry sites. Use bear boxes where provided. Bear canisters required in backcountry where no bear boxes or poles are available. |
| **Required bear canister zones** | Grand Teton has specific zones where bear canisters are REQUIRED for backcountry camping (as opposed to areas where bear poles are available). **Check with the backcountry permit office for current canister-required zones.** **[VERIFY 2026]** |
| **Cooking distance** | Same 200-foot rule as Yellowstone for backcountry. |
| **Trail closures** | Grand Teton also closes trails for bear activity. Check at the Craig Thomas Discovery and Visitor Center or the Jenny Lake Ranger Station. |
| **Backcountry permits** | Required. Bear safety orientation included. |

**Where to check trail closures:**
- Grand Teton NPS website: https://www.nps.gov/grte/planyourvisit/conditions.htm **[VERIFY 2026 URL]**
- Craig Thomas Discovery and Visitor Center (Moose, WY)
- Jenny Lake Ranger Station
- Colter Bay Visitor Center

### Key Differences Between the Two Parks

The bear safety regulations are nearly identical between Yellowstone and Grand Teton. The main differences:
- Grand Teton may have specific backcountry zones requiring bear canisters (vs. bear poles). Check when getting your permit.
- Yellowstone has more extensive trail closure programs due to grizzly activity in the Hayden Valley and Pelican Valley areas.
- Both parks share the Interagency Grizzly Bear Study Team data and coordinate on bear management.

### Bridger-Teton and Shoshone National Forests (Adjacent Areas)

If you hike or camp on national forest land between or near the parks:
- Bear spray is recommended (same as parks).
- Food storage requirements vary by forest and district. Check with the local ranger district.
- Generally, the same best practices apply.

## Where to Buy Bear Spray

You cannot fly with bear spray (it is a pressurized canister containing capsaicin — TSA prohibits it in both carry-on and checked luggage). You must buy it at or near your destination.

### Near Bozeman Yellowstone International Airport (BZN)

### What to Look For When Buying

1. **EPA registration number** on the label — confirms it is a bear deterrent, not just pepper spray.
2. **Minimum 7.9 oz** net weight (not total canister weight).
3. **2% capsaicin (CRC) concentration.**
4. **Spray distance of 20+ feet** (stated on the label).
5. **Expiration date** — should be 3-4 years out from manufacture. Do not buy expired or close-to-expiration spray.
6. **A holster that works for you.** Chest holsters (cross-draw across the chest strap of your pack) are popular for hiking because they keep the spray at the front of your body. Hip holsters (belt attachment) work for around-camp carry. Some people prefer a hip holster that attaches to their pack's hip belt.
7. **Practice drawing from the holster** in the parking lot before you hit the trail. Remove the safety, aim, and go through the motion (without actually firing) several times.

### End-of-Trip Disposal

You cannot fly home with bear spray either. Options:
- **Give it to another visitor** at your last campground or trailhead. Someone will happily take nearly-full bear spray.
- **Return it** — some stores (REI, Schnees) accept returns of unused/partially-used bear spray. Check their policy.
- **Donate it** — some visitor centers or ranger stations accept bear spray donations for redistribution.
- **Dispose of it** as hazardous waste at a designated facility (check with the local waste management). Do NOT puncture the canister or throw it in regular trash.

### The Single Best Preparation Step

**Have a conversation with each other about what you will do in each scenario BEFORE you arrive.** Discuss:
- "If we see a grizzly at 50 yards, what do we do?"
- "If a bear charges, what do we do? Who sprays first?"
- "If we hear something in camp at night, what's the plan?"

Talking through scenarios in advance means you have a plan instead of relying on adrenaline-fueled improvisation. You have a partner — use that advantage. You can divide tasks: one person talks to the bear while the other readies the spray. One person watches the bear while the other slowly gathers belongings for a retreat.

## Practical Tips for the Anxious First-Timer

### How Common Are Encounters, Really?

Let's put this in perspective with data.

| Statistic | Number | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Annual Yellowstone visitors** | ~4.9 million (2023 figure) | NPS visitor statistics |
| **Grizzly bears in Greater Yellowstone** | ~1,000+ (as of ~2024 estimates) | Interagency Grizzly Bear Study Team |
| **Bear-caused injuries in Yellowstone (average)** | ~1 per year (average over the last 40+ years) | NPS records |
| **Fatal bear attacks in Yellowstone history** | 8 deaths since 1872 (the park's founding) | NPS records |
| **Your odds of being injured by a bear in Yellowstone** | Roughly 1 in 2.7 million visits | Calculated from NPS data |
| **For context: odds of being struck by lightning in a given year** | 1 in 1.2 million | National Weather Service |

**Translation:** You are more likely to be injured in a car accident driving to the trailhead than you are to be injured by a bear on the trail. The risk is real but small — and it is **overwhelmingly mitigable** through the precautions in this document.

### What Makes It Even Safer for You

Several factors work in your favor:

1. **You are a pair, not solo.** Groups of 2+ have dramatically lower risk than solo hikers. Most bear attacks involve solo hikers or people who were separated from their group.
2. **You are experienced in the outdoors.** You know how to behave in wilderness — no food in the tent, proper storage, awareness of your surroundings. This is not your first time camping in bear country (black bear experience counts).
3. **You will have bear spray.** Bear spray stops aggressive bear behavior 92% of the time (Smith et al., 2008). Carrying and knowing how to use it is the single biggest risk reducer.
4. **You are reading this document.** Most bear encounters that go badly involve people who were unprepared, unaware, or violated basic protocols (approaching bears for photos, leaving food out, running from a charge). You are doing the preparation.
5. **You will be on maintained trails in a national park** with ranger patrols, trail closure systems, and bear management programs. This is not the Alaskan backcountry.

### The Confidence Spectrum

You want to land here on the confidence spectrum:

```
TERRIFIED -------- RESPECTFULLY PREPARED -------- COMPLACENT
   (bad)                (where you want to be)           (bad)
```

**Too much fear** ruins the trip. If you are jumping at every snapped twig, unable to enjoy the scenery, and lying awake all night in your tent, the preparation has failed. Fear is not safety — it is stress.

**Too little concern** is dangerous. The person who leaves food on the picnic table, hikes in silence with earbuds in, and approaches a grizzly for a selfie is the person who creates dangerous situations.

**Respectful preparedness** is the sweet spot. You know the protocols. You have the gear. You make noise on the trail. You keep a clean camp. You enjoy the wildlife from a safe distance. And when you see a grizzly foraging on a hillside 200 yards away, you stop, watch, take photos, feel your heart race with awe (not panic), and think: "That is one of the most incredible animals on the planet, and I am standing here watching it in its home."

That is the experience you are going to have. The vast majority of grizzly sightings in Yellowstone and the Tetons are exactly this: a magnificent animal at a safe distance, going about its day, completely uninterested in you.

### Quick Anxiety Reducers

- **Thousands of people hike in grizzly country every single day in summer.** The trails are not empty — you will see other hikers, and that is additional safety.
- **Bears do not want to interact with you.** They are interested in whitebark pine nuts, army cutworm moths, elk calves, and berries. You are loud, weird-smelling, and unappetizing.
- **The bear spray on your chest is proven more effective than a firearm** in stopping bear attacks. You have the best tool available.
- **You will likely see bears on your trip — from a safe distance.** This is one of the highlights of visiting Greater Yellowstone. Embrace it. Bring binoculars. You will tell this story for years.
- **Rangers are everywhere.** If you are unsure about conditions, ask. Every ranger station, visitor center, and backcountry office has staff who deal with bear safety daily. They will tell you what is current, what areas to be extra cautious in, and how things are looking.

## Summary: The Four Non-Negotiable Rules

If you remember nothing else from this document, remember these four things:

1. **Make noise on the trail.** Talk, clap, call out "Hey bear!" at blind corners. Surprise encounters are how people get hurt. Prevent them.

2. **Carry bear spray on your body, accessible in 2 seconds.** One canister per person. Chest or hip holster. Not in your pack. Not in your car. On your body.

3. **Know the difference between defensive and predatory.** Defensive (surprised/stressed bear) = play dead if contacted. Predatory (calm, following, deliberate) = fight back. Bear spray first in both cases.

4. **Keep a clean camp.** All food and scented items in the bear box or canister when not in active use. Nothing scented in the tent. Cook 200+ feet from where you sleep.

Do these four things and you will be safer than the vast majority of visitors to the Greater Yellowstone area. Then relax, hike hard, and enjoy one of the most spectacular wildlife ecosystems on the planet.
