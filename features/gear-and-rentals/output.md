## 1. Must Acquire at Destination

### Bear Spray (x2)

Bear spray is EPA-registered capsaicin spray specifically designed for deterring charging bears. You need two canisters — one per person — and they **cannot be transported on any commercial flight** (not in carry-on, not in checked bags). You must buy them at or near your destination.

#### What Specs to Look For

| Spec | What You Want | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **EPA registration** | Must say "bear spray" or "bear deterrent" on the label, with an EPA registration number | Pepper spray / self-defense sprays are NOT the same thing. Bear spray has a specific spray pattern and concentration. |
| **Spray distance** | Minimum 20 feet, ideally 25-30 feet | You need standoff distance. Shorter-range sprays are less effective. |
| **Spray duration** | Minimum 6 seconds | A charging grizzly covers ground fast. You need enough spray for multiple bursts. |
| **Canister size** | 7.9 oz minimum, 9.2 oz preferred | Larger canisters = more spray duration. The weight difference is trivial. |
| **Expiration** | Check the date — should be good through your trip and ideally beyond | Bear spray loses pressure over time. Don't buy old stock. |

**Recommended brands:** Counter Assault (8.1 oz or 10.2 oz), UDAP Bear Spray (7.9 oz or 13.4 oz), Frontiersman Bear Spray (7.9 oz or 9.2 oz). All are proven and widely available in Greater Yellowstone gateway towns. Counter Assault is made in Montana and is the most commonly stocked brand in the area.

**Holster:** Each canister should come with a hip holster. If it doesn't, buy one separately. Bear spray in your backpack is useless — it needs to be on your hip or chest, accessible in under 2 seconds. Some people prefer chest holsters that clip to pack straps; these are available at the same shops.

**Recommendation:** Buy bear spray at **REI Bozeman** or **Murdoch's** on your way out of the airport. This is a 10-minute detour and gets it handled immediately. You'll also want to pick up fuel canisters at the same stop (see Section 4).

**Expected total cost for 2 canisters with holsters:** $80-120

#### Can You Return Unused Canisters?

**REI:** Yes — REI has a generous return policy. Unused, unexpired bear spray with the safety clip intact can be returned within one year. If you buy at REI Bozeman, you could return unused canisters at any REI location (including one closer to home) after the trip. **This is the single best reason to buy at REI.** [VERIFY 2026 — confirm REI's current return policy hasn't changed]

**Other shops:** Generally no. Most shops do not accept returns on bear spray because it's a pressurized safety device. Some shops in Yellowstone gateway towns have informal "take a can, leave a can" arrangements, but don't count on this.

#### End-of-Trip Disposal

If you use the spray (even partially) or don't want to deal with returning it:

- **Do NOT throw bear spray in the trash or recycling.** It's a pressurized canister with capsaicin — it's hazardous waste.
- **NPS bear spray recycling:** Some Yellowstone and Grand Teton ranger stations and visitor centers accept used/expired bear spray canisters for recycling. Ask at any visitor center. The Craig Thomas Discovery Center in Moose (Grand Teton) and the Yellowstone Visitor Education Centers have accepted canisters in past years. [VERIFY 2026]
- **Gateway town options:** Some outfitters in Jackson and Gardiner accept used canisters. Ask where you bought it.
- **If you can't find a drop-off:** Fully discharge the canister outdoors (away from people, animals, buildings, and your car), then dispose of the empty canister as you would any aerosol can — most municipal recycling accepts empty aerosol cans.
- **Best strategy:** Buy at REI. If unused, return for full refund. If used, recycle at a park visitor center or discharge and dispose.

### Bouldering Crash Pads

She climbs V10 — Teton Boulder Park (outside Driggs, ID, on the west side of the Tetons) is a legitimate bouldering destination with classic granite problems from V0 to V12+. You're flying, so bringing pads isn't practical. Rental is the way to go.

#### Rental Options

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Yostmark Mountain Equipment** | 12 E Little Ave, Driggs, ID 83422 | ~10 min drive to the boulders | Yes — they rent crash pads | **$15-25/day per pad** [VERIFY 2026] | The go-to shop for Teton bouldering. Closest to the boulders. Staff knows the area well and can give beta on current conditions and problems. Call ahead in late August to confirm availability. Phone: (208) 354-2828 |
| **Peaked Sports** | 70 E Little Ave, Driggs, ID 83422 | ~10 min drive | Likely yes — they carry climbing gear and rentals | **$15-25/day** [VERIFY 2026] | Another Driggs option. Worth calling if Yostmark is out. Phone: (208) 354-2354 |
| **Teton Mountaineering** | 170 N Cache St, Jackson, WY 83001 | ~1 hr via Teton Pass | May rent pads — call ahead | **$20-30/day** [VERIFY 2026] | Jackson-based. Larger selection of climbing gear. The drive over Teton Pass to the boulders is ~45 min from Jackson. Phone: (307) 733-3595 |
| **Exum Mountain Guides Pro Shop** | Moose, WY (inside Grand Teton NP) | ~1.5 hrs to boulders | Unlikely to rent pads — primarily a guiding service | N/A | Worth asking if you're already in Moose, but don't count on it. |

**How many pads?** For V10 bouldering, two pads is ideal (one primary, one to cover the landing zone gap). Even one good pad plus a folded sleeping bag as a spotter pad can work. Rent two if affordable, one if tight.

**Reserve ahead?** Strongly recommended for late August. Driggs is a small town; Yostmark may only have 4-6 pads in their rental fleet. A quick phone call or email a week before your trip locks it in. If you're planning your bouldering day in advance (e.g., Day 6 per the itinerary skeleton), tell them the exact date when you reserve.

**What about buying a pad locally?** A new crash pad costs $200-350. For a single day of bouldering, rental is obviously better. If you're planning multiple bouldering days, do the math — but you'd also have to get it home (ship it back, ~$30-50 via UPS).

#### Teton Boulder Park Quick Info

- **Location:** Off Teton Canyon Road, west of Driggs, ID. The parking area is at the base of the Teton Range on the west side.
- **Rock type:** Gneiss and granite boulders ranging from 6 to 20 feet.
- **Classic problems:** The area has 200+ documented problems. Highlights in the V4-V10 range include problems on the Main Boulder, the Cube, and scattered satellite boulders. A guidebook or Mountain Project beta is essential.
- **Guidebook:** "Teton Bouldering" by Mike Bockino is the definitive guide. Available at Yostmark. Also check Mountain Project for free topos and recent tick lists.
- **Conditions in late August:** Excellent. Warm but not hot. Morning shade on many problems. Afternoon thunderstorms are possible — have an exit plan.
- **Approach:** Short — most boulders are 5-15 minutes from the parking area.

### Rafting Gear

If you do a guided rafting trip on the Snake River (highly recommended — see the biking-and-rafting feature for trip options), the outfitter provides virtually everything.

#### What's Typically Provided by Rafting Outfitters

- Raft, paddles, safety lines
- PFD (life jacket) — fitted at the put-in
- Wetsuit and/or splash jacket (essential — the Snake River is COLD, even in August, typically 50-55 degrees F)
- Helmet (for whitewater sections)
- Dry bag for your valuables (phone, keys, wallet)

#### What YOU Should Bring

| Item | Why |
|---|---|
| **Synthetic or wool base layers** | You'll get wet. Cotton = miserable. Wear quick-dry shorts and a synthetic or merino top under the wetsuit/splash jacket. |
| **Secure footwear** | Sandals with a heel strap (Chacos, Tevas) or old sneakers. No flip-flops — they will come off in rapids. |
| **Sunscreen + sunglasses with a retainer** | You're on the water. Glare is intense. A Croakies-style retainer keeps your glasses on your face. |
| **Change of dry clothes in the car** | You will be wet when you get off the river. Have warm dry clothes waiting. |
| **Waterproof phone case** | Optional but smart. A zip-lock bag works in a pinch. |

**You do NOT need to buy or rent any rafting-specific gear.** The outfitters handle everything water-related. Just show up in synthetic layers with secure footwear.

## 3. Gear You're Bringing — Double-Check List

### Bear Canister Compliance

You own a bear canister. Before you pack it, confirm it's on the approved list for both parks.

#### Yellowstone Requirements

- **Backcountry camping:** Hard-sided bear canisters are **required** in most backcountry campsites that don't have bear poles or bear boxes. Some sites have food storage poles or boxes, in which case your canister isn't required but is still smart to carry.
- **Approved canisters:** Yellowstone accepts canisters that are IGBC (Interagency Grizzly Bear Committee) certified. The IGBC maintains an approved list.
- **Common approved models:** BearVault BV500, BearVault BV450, Garcia Backpackers' Cache, Counter Assault Bear Keg, Bare Boxer Contender, UDAP No-Fed-Bear, Lighter1 Big Daddy. [VERIFY 2026 — check the current IGBC list]
- **Ursacks:** Yellowstone does **NOT** approve Ursacks (the fabric bear bags) as a standalone bear-resistant container. You must use a hard-sided canister.

#### Grand Teton Requirements

- **Backcountry camping:** Hard-sided, IGBC-approved bear canisters are **required** for all backcountry camping in Grand Teton National Park. No exceptions.
- **Same approved list** as Yellowstone — IGBC certified models.
- **Ursacks:** Also **NOT** approved in Grand Teton as a standalone container.

#### What to Check

1. Look at your canister for an IGBC certification label or check the model against the IGBC approved products list.
2. **IGBC approved list:** https://igbconline.org/bear-resistant-products/ [VERIFY 2026 — confirm URL and check your model]
3. If your canister is NOT on the list (rare for major brands, but possible for older or off-brand models), buy or rent an approved one. Rental is available at Yellowstone and Grand Teton visitor centers (typically $3-5/day) and at outdoor shops in Bozeman, Jackson, and Gardiner.

**If you're only car camping (no backcountry overnights):** You technically don't need the canister at all — car campgrounds have bear boxes at each site. But bringing it doesn't hurt, and if a spontaneous backcountry overnight opportunity arises, you'll be glad you have it.

### Cold Weather and Altitude Gear

Late August in Greater Yellowstone means warm days but genuinely cold nights, especially at elevation. The temperature swings are dramatic.

| Condition | Expected Temps | What You Need |
|---|---|---|
| **Valley floors at night** (6,000-7,000 ft — campgrounds) | Lows: 30-45 F | Sleeping bag rated to at least **20 F** (30 F is risky on the coldest nights). Insulated sleeping pad with R-value 4+. Warm hat and base layers for sleeping. |
| **Beartooth Highway summit** (10,947 ft) | Can be 35-50 F even at midday; add wind chill | Insulated jacket (puffy), wind-resistant outer layer, warm hat, gloves. Even in August, the summit can feel like late autumn. |
| **High Teton passes** (10,000+ ft on Paintbrush Divide, etc.) | 40-55 F at midday; colder with wind | Same as Beartooth. Wind is the real factor at the passes. A buff/neck gaiter is very useful. |
| **Morning hikes** | 35-50 F at trailhead, warming to 60-75 F by midday | Layer system: base layer + midlayer (fleece or light puffy) + wind/rain shell. You'll be peeling layers all morning. |

#### Checklist — Cold Weather / Altitude

- [ ] Sleeping bag rated 20 F or lower
- [ ] Insulated sleeping pad (R-value 4+)
- [ ] Insulated puffy jacket (down or synthetic)
- [ ] Fleece or wool midlayer
- [ ] Wind-resistant shell jacket
- [ ] Warm hat (beanie)
- [ ] Lightweight gloves (for Beartooth summit and early morning hikes)
- [ ] Merino or synthetic base layers (top and bottom) — for sleeping and for layering on hikes
- [ ] Buff / neck gaiter
- [ ] Warm socks for sleeping

### Rain Gear

Afternoon thunderstorms are common and can be intense — lightning is a real danger above treeline in the Tetons and on the Beartooth Plateau.

#### Checklist — Rain Gear

- [ ] Rain jacket (waterproof, not just water-resistant — Gore-Tex or equivalent)
- [ ] Rain pants (packable — you won't wear them often, but when you need them, you need them)
- [ ] Pack cover or waterproof pack liner (a contractor trash bag inside your pack works perfectly)
- [ ] Quick-dry hiking clothes (avoid cotton — if you get caught in a storm in cotton at 10,000 ft, hypothermia is a real risk even in August)

**Lightning protocol above treeline:** If you see/hear a storm building while on Paintbrush Divide, Static Peak, Beartooth Plateau, or any exposed ridge — descend immediately. Afternoon storms typically build between 1-4 PM. Start alpine hikes early (5-6 AM) to be off exposed terrain by early afternoon.

## 4. Packing Strategy: Flying with Camping Gear

### TSA Restrictions for Outdoor Gear

Flying BOS to BZN with a car-load of camping and hiking gear requires some planning. Here's what the TSA says:

| Item | Carry-On? | Checked Bag? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Trekking poles** | NO | YES | They're "pointed objects." Must go in checked luggage. |
| **Camp stove** (empty, no fuel) | YES (but may get flagged) | YES | The stove body is fine. It **must** be completely empty of fuel and clean — no residue. TSA agents may inspect it. Wrap it in a ziplock and be ready to explain. |
| **Camp stove fuel canisters** (isobutane/propane) | NO | NO | **Cannot fly at all. Buy at destination.** See fuel section below. |
| **Liquid fuel** (white gas) | NO | NO | Same — buy at destination. |
| **Bear spray** | NO | NO | **Cannot fly at all. Buy at destination.** |
| **Bear canister** | YES | YES | It's just a hard plastic cylinder. No restrictions. Heavy/bulky though — checked is better. |
| **Tent** (poles and stakes) | Stakes: NO carry-on | YES | Tent stakes are "sharp objects." The tent body and poles are fine in carry-on, but just check the whole tent. |
| **Sleeping bag** | YES | YES | Bulky but no restrictions. Compression sack recommended. |
| **Sleeping pad** | YES | YES | Inflatable pads are fine. |
| **Camp knife / multi-tool** | NO | YES | Blades of any length are prohibited in carry-on. |
| **Water filter / purifier** | YES | YES | No restrictions. |
| **Headlamp** | YES | YES | No restrictions. Spare batteries fine in carry-on. |
| **Lighter** | 1 disposable lighter in carry-on | YES | No torch lighters or fuel-filled lighters in checked bags. One standard BIC-type lighter per person is allowed in carry-on. |
| **Matches** | 1 book of safety matches in carry-on | NO (strike-anywhere matches prohibited entirely) | Just bring a lighter. |
| **Carabiners / climbing gear** | YES | YES | No restrictions on carabiners, harness, etc. Not relevant for bouldering, but noted for completeness. |
| **Trekking pole tips / protectors** | YES | YES | Harmless rubber caps. |

### Check vs. Carry-On Strategy

**The Two-Bag System:** You're packing for a camping trip, not a business trip. Think in terms of two large checked bags plus two carry-on bags (one backpack each).

#### Checked Bags (2 large duffel bags or suitcases)

The heavy, bulky, and TSA-restricted stuff:

| Checked Bag 1 (Shared Camp Gear) | Checked Bag 2 (Personal Gear + Clothing) |
|---|---|
| Tent (body, poles, stakes, footprint) | Sleeping bag #1 (compressed) |
| Sleeping bag #2 (compressed) | Sleeping pads x2 (deflated, rolled) |
| Camp stove (EMPTY, clean, no fuel) | Trekking poles x2 |
| Cookware (pot, pan, utensils) | Camp knife / multi-tool |
| Bear canister | Extra clothing layers |
| Water filter | Rain gear |
| Camp chairs (if bringing — optional, heavy) | Hiking boots (wear your lighter pair on the plane, check the other) |
| Rope, cord, carabiners | Toiletries, sunscreen, bug spray |

**Weight limit:** Most airlines allow 50 lbs per checked bag. Two duffels of camping gear will be close to limit. Weigh them at home. Overweight fees are typically $100+ per bag.

**Bag fees from BOS:**
- **Delta:** First checked bag $35, second $45 (or free with Delta credit card / SkyMiles status) [VERIFY 2026]
- **United:** First checked bag $35, second $45 (or free with United credit card / MileagePlus status) [VERIFY 2026]
- **Check your airline's current policy.** Bag fees are the most-changed airline policy year to year.

#### Carry-On Bags (1 personal item + 1 backpack each)

The stuff you want accessible and can't risk losing if a checked bag is delayed:

| What to Carry On | Why |
|---|---|
| Warm puffy jacket (wear it on the plane if bulky) | If your checked bag is delayed, you still have warmth for a cold night |
| One full change of hiking clothes | Same reason — you can function for Day 1 if bags are delayed |
| Headlamps + spare batteries | Small, critical |
| Phone, chargers, battery pack | Obvious |
| Medications, toiletry essentials | Can't replace easily |
| Wallet, IDs, reservation confirmations | Obvious |
| Snacks for the drive from the airport | You'll be hungry and there's no food between BZN and Gardiner |
| Water bottles / Nalgenes (empty through security) | Fill after security |
| Sunglasses, hat | Wear them |
| Maps / guidebooks / printed reservations | Just in case phone dies |

**Pro tip:** Wear your bulkiest items on the plane — hiking boots, puffy jacket, fleece. This saves significant checked bag space and weight.

This is your action-sequenced shopping list, organized by when you'll be at each location on the clockwise BZN-to-JAC loop.

### Before Leaving Home

- [ ] **Confirm bear canister is IGBC-approved** — check model against https://igbconline.org/bear-resistant-products/
- [ ] **Weigh checked bags** — stay under 50 lbs each
- [ ] **Pack camp stove empty and clean** — no fuel residue
- [ ] **Trekking poles, tent stakes, knife in checked bags** — NOT carry-on
- [ ] **Wear hiking boots and puffy jacket on the plane** — saves space and weight
- [ ] **Print key reservations** — campsite confirmations, flight details, rental car confirmation
- [ ] **Download offline maps** — Gaia GPS / AllTrails offline maps for Yellowstone, Tetons, and Beartooth area. Cell service is unreliable to nonexistent in most of the parks.
- [ ] **Call Yostmark in Driggs** — reserve 1-2 crash pads for your bouldering day. Phone: (208) 354-2828

### Day 0: Arrive BZN — The Bozeman Gear Stop

**One stop at REI Bozeman (2230 Tschache Ln) handles almost everything:**

- [ ] **Bear spray x2** with holsters — $80-120 total
- [ ] **Fuel canisters** (isobutane, 1-2 medium/230g canisters) — $14-20 total
- [ ] **Any forgotten items** — REI is a full outdoor retailer. Forgot a headlamp? Buff? Rain jacket? Get it here.
- [ ] **Lighter** (if you didn't bring one) — $2

**Alternative: Murdoch's (2275 N 7th Ave)** for bear spray at slightly lower prices, plus a Walmart run for groceries. REI + Walmart on the same Bozeman stop is efficient — they're about 5 minutes apart.

**Also on the Bozeman stop:**
- [ ] **Walmart grocery run** — 1500 N 7th Ave, Bozeman. Stock up on trip food: breakfast supplies, lunch stuff, snacks, water.

**Time budget:** 45-60 minutes for REI + Walmart, then 1.5 hr drive to Gardiner.

### Day 2: Red Lodge — Optional Bike Rental

If mountain biking in Red Lodge:

- [ ] **Rent a mountain bike** from Sylvan Peak Mountain Shop (Main St, Red Lodge). Half-day hardtail: ~$40-60. Includes helmet.
- [ ] Ask for trail recommendations for beginner-intermediate — West Fork system.

### Day 5 or 6: Teton Area — Bouldering and Paddling

If bouldering at Teton Boulder Park:

- [ ] **Pick up crash pad(s)** from Yostmark Mountain Equipment (12 E Little Ave, Driggs, ID). You should have reserved these before the trip.
- [ ] **Pick up or buy a bouldering guidebook** — "Teton Bouldering" by Mike Bockino, or get free beta from Mountain Project.
- [ ] **Return pads to Yostmark** same day or next morning per their policy.

If paddling Jenny Lake:

- [ ] **Rent canoe or kayak at Jenny Lake Boating** — arrive before 9 AM for best availability. ~$30/hr canoe, ~$25/hr kayak.
- [ ] No reservation needed (or possible) — first come, first served.

If renting a bike in Jackson area:

- [ ] **Rent from Hoback Sports or Wilson Backcountry Sports** — half-day ~$50-75.

### Day 6 or 7: End of Trip — Disposal and Returns

- [ ] **Bear spray (unused):** If bought at REI, keep receipt — return at any REI after the trip for full refund.
- [ ] **Bear spray (used or unwanted):** Drop off at a park visitor center for recycling, or fully discharge outdoors and recycle the empty canister.
- [ ] **Fuel canisters (empty):** Puncture with CrunchIt tool and recycle, or drop at a recycling station.
- [ ] **Return rented crash pads** to Yostmark.
- [ ] **Return rented bike** (if applicable).
